## Apple's Charitable App Guidelines (Requirements)

Based on Apple's App Store Review Guidelines (Section 4.3):

1. **Clarity of Beneficiary:** The charity/charities must be clearly named, not vague ("a charity" or "nonprofit organizations")
2. **No Ambiguity on Proceeds:** Define exactly what "proceeds" means. Apple prohibits misleading claims like "100% of proceeds" if it doesn't account for payment processing fees
3. **Third-Party Verification:** Apple strongly prefers links to Charity Navigator, charity registration numbers, or third-party rating sites
4. **Transparency Commitment:** If you claim to publish transparency reports, you must actually do it
5. **Charitable Status Proof:** The receiving organization must be verifiably charitable (501c3 in US, equivalent in other countries)
6. **No Endorsement Overstatement:** Don't imply charity endorsement unless you have written consent
7. **DAF/Donor-Advised Fund Disclosure:** If the structure is DAF-based (not direct donation), this must be disclosed to avoid misleading reviewers

**Result:** Apple will reject submissions that use phrases like "100% of proceeds" without defining proceeds and accounting for fees.

## Notes for Kiran

1. **Apple's Threshold:** Apple has rejected apps claiming "100% of proceeds" when fees aren't accounted for. Using "net proceeds" and defining it clearly avoids this problem.

2. **Charity Consent is Critical:** Using a charity name, logo, or implying endorsement without written permission can trigger:
   - App Store rejection (false/unverified claims)
   - Charity complaint (trademark/name misuse)
   - Legal exposure (false charitable endorsement)

   Don't use names in App Store listing until consent is confirmed.

3. **Quarterly Reports Are Load-Bearing:** If you promise transparency reports, you must publish them on schedule. Set a calendar reminder for Q2, Q3, Q4 2026 (or quarterly if ongoing). Missing one damages trust and could trigger app review issues.

4. **DAF Disclosure Matters:** Because net proceeds go through a DAF (not direct donation), Apple requires this to be stated. It's still charitable, but the structure matters legally.

5. **First Submission Strategy:** Use the ultra-conservative version first. Once approved, you can warm up the language in future updates without risking rejection.

6. **State Laws:** Charitable solicitation has state-by-state rules. If targeting users in CA, NY, or other heavily regulated states, have an attorney review—especially around terminology and disclosures.

7. **Screenshots:** The most persuasive part of your App Store listing will be screenshots of the "Your Impact" section showing the charity choice and running donation total. Invest in those.
