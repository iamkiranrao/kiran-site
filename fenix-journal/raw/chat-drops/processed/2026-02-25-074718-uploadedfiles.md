### Kiran `21:26`

when to use RAG vs. fine-tuning vs. system prompts

---

### Claude `21:27`

Great question, and one that could absolutely come up in the interview. Here's the simple version:

**System prompts** are like giving someone a cheat sheet before a conversation. You write instructions that say "You are Kate, GEICO's assistant. Here are the rules. Here's what you know." The AI reads that cheat sheet every time someone starts a chat.

- **Use when:** Your knowledge fits in a few pages. The info doesn't change often. You need quick setup.
- **GEICO example:** Telling the chatbot its personality, tone, what it can and can't discuss, general insurance FAQs.
- **Limitation:** There's a size limit. You can't stuff 10,000 policy documents into a system prompt. And the AI still might improvise beyond what you wrote.
- **Your experience:** You designed the Fenix system prompt architecture this way — career data, personality, response rules — because at that scale, it's the right tool.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** is like giving someone access to a filing cabinet. Instead of memorizing everything, the AI searches for the right document when a question comes in, reads the relevant part, then answers based on what it found.

- **Use when:** You have a lot of information that changes frequently. You need answers grounded in real data, not AI memory. Accuracy matters more than speed.
- **GEICO example:** Customer asks "what does my policy cover?" The chatbot searches that specific customer's policy document, pulls the coverage section, and generates an answer from it. It's not guessing — it's reading.
- **Why this matters for insurance:** This is how you prevent hallucination. The AI can only say what the documents say. If the policy says flood coverage is excluded, the chatbot says flood coverage is excluded. No making things up.
- **Your experience:** Your LangChain prototype uses this pattern — chains that retrieve documents before generating responses.

**Fine-tuning** is like sending someone to school for months. You take the base AI model and train it on thousands of your specific examples until it learns your patterns, terminology, and style at a deep level. The knowledge becomes baked into the model itself.

- **Use when:** You need the AI to behave in a fundamentally different way than the base model. You have specialized language or patterns that prompting can't capture. You need maximum speed (no retrieval step).
- **GEICO example:** Honestly, probably not the first choice. Maybe later — training a model on millions of past customer service conversations so it learns insurance-specific language patterns and common resolution paths.
- **Why it's usually overkill:** It's expensive, slow to iterate, requires ML engineering resources, and you lose flexibility. Every time your products change, you'd need to retrain. For a company evolving its chatbot quickly, that's a bottleneck.

**The real answer for GEICO (and most companies) is you use all three together:**

Think of it as layers. The system prompt sets the personality and rules — that's the foundation. RAG handles anything factual or customer-specific — policy details, billing info, claims status — by looking it up in real time. Fine-tuning is reserved for specialized capabilities that justify the investment, like understanding insurance jargon or classifying claim severity from photos.

The interview-ready way to say this: "I'd start with system prompts for speed and control, layer in RAG immediately for accuracy on customer-specific data, and only pursue fine-tuning when we have clear evidence that the first two layers can't handle a specific capability. At Wells Fargo with Fargo, we followed a similar progression — start simple, add complexity only when the data justifies it."

The key principle: **use the simplest approach that solves the problem.** System prompts are days of work. RAG is weeks. Fine-tuning is months. Start cheap and fast, escalate only when you have evidence you need to.

### Claude `21:33`

LangChain is basically the plumbing that connects all three of those things together.

Without LangChain, imagine you're building a chatbot from scratch. You need to write code that takes the user's message, adds the system prompt, searches a database for relevant documents (RAG), formats everything nicely, sends it to the LLM, gets the response back, and maybe remembers what was said earlier in the conversation. That's a lot of glue code.

LangChain gives you pre-built pieces for each of those steps so you don't have to wire it all up yourself.

**Think of it like a kitchen.** The LLM (Claude, GPT) is the chef. The system prompt is the recipe card. RAG is the pantry where ingredients are stored. LangChain is the kitchen itself — the counter space, the utensils, the organization system that lets the chef grab ingredients from the pantry, follow the recipe, and plate the dish.

Here's what LangChain actually does in practice:

**Chains** — The core idea. You string together steps: "first retrieve documents, then format them into a prompt, then send to the LLM, then parse the output." Each step feeds into the next. A simple chain might be: user question → search policy database → inject results into prompt → send to Claude → return answer. That whole pipeline is one "chain."

**Retrievers** — This is the RAG part. LangChain connects to your document stores (a vector database, a regular database, PDFs, whatever) and handles the search. Customer asks about their coverage → LangChain's retriever searches the policy database → pulls the relevant paragraphs → feeds them to the LLM. You don't have to write the search logic from scratch.

**Memory** — Chatbots need to remember what was said earlier in the conversation. "I asked about my auto policy" and then "what about the deductible" — the chatbot needs to know "the deductible" refers to the auto policy from two messages ago. LangChain has memory modules that handle this conversation history automatically.

**Agents** — This is where it gets powerful. Instead of following a fixed chain, an "agent" can decide what to do next based on the situation. Customer asks about a claim → agent decides to check claims database. Customer asks a general question → agent decides to search the FAQ. Customer asks something complex → agent decides to escalate. The LLM itself is making routing decisions.

**For GEICO specifically, here's how it would fit:**

A customer opens the app and asks "what's the status of my claim from last week?" LangChain orchestrates the whole thing. The agent receives the message, decides it needs to look up a claim, calls the claims API retriever to find the right claim, gets the status data back, combines it with the system prompt (which says "be friendly, don't give legal advice, use the customer's first name"), sends all of that to the LLM, and the LLM generates: "Hi Kiran, your claim from February 18th is currently being reviewed by our adjuster. You should hear back within 2 business days."

That entire flow — routing, retrieval, prompt assembly, generation — is what LangChain handles.

**Why this matters for your interview:** You built a prototype with LangChain, so you can speak to this from experience. If they ask how you'd architect the next-gen chatbot, you can say something like: "I'd use a framework like LangChain to orchestrate the conversation pipeline — retrieval from policy systems, memory management across sessions, and agent-based routing to decide when to answer directly vs. escalate. I've prototyped this architecture myself and understand the trade-offs between flexibility and control."

The key insight to land: LangChain isn't the brain (that's the LLM) and it isn't the knowledge (that's your databases). It's the nervous system that connects everything and makes it work as one coherent product.

### Claude `22:37`

Sure. Let me make this even simpler than last time.

**Imagine you're a new employee at GEICO.**

**System prompt** is your first-day orientation. Someone sits you down and says: "Here's who you are, here's how we talk to customers, here's what you're allowed to say." You carry that cheat sheet with you. It's quick to set up, but you can only fit so much on one page.

**RAG** is giving you access to the company filing cabinet. A customer calls and asks "does my policy cover rental cars?" You don't know off the top of your head — so you walk to the filing cabinet, pull their specific policy document, read the relevant paragraph, and then answer based on what you just read. You're not memorizing anything. You're looking it up fresh every time.

Why this matters: the filing cabinet is always current. When GEICO updates a policy, the filing cabinet gets updated, and your next answer reflects the change automatically. You never give stale information.

**Fine-tuning** is sending you to insurance school for six months. You study thousands of customer service transcripts, claims conversations, and policy documents until insurance knowledge is baked into your brain. You don't need to look anything up — you just *know* it.

The problem: if GEICO changes a policy after you graduated, your brain still has the old version. Someone has to send you back to school (retrain the model), which is expensive and slow. And sometimes you "remember" things slightly wrong because you're going from memory, not from the source document.

**The practical difference:**

RAG is like an open-book exam. You always get the right answer because you're reading from the source. It's slower (you have to look things up), but accurate and always current.

Fine-tuning is like a closed-book exam. You're faster because you just know things, but you might be wrong, and your knowledge gets stale.

**For GEICO's chatbot, RAG wins almost every time.** Insurance information changes constantly — state regulations, policy terms, pricing, coverage details. You can't retrain a model every time something changes. You need the chatbot to look up the current answer from the source of truth every single time.

Fine-tuning would only make sense for things that *don't change* — like learning the general rhythm of how to talk to an insurance customer, or understanding insurance jargon. But for any factual answer about a customer's specific policy? RAG, always.

**One sentence to drop in the interview if it comes up:** "For anything customer-specific or regulation-dependent, I'd always use RAG over fine-tuning — the cost of a stale or hallucinated answer in insurance is too high to rely on model memory."
