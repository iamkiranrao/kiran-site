## Data Program Delivery (Questions 13-22)

**13. Walk me through your experience leading data governance frameworks.**

*Answer:* At Wells Fargo, I led data governance for our AI assistant which processed millions of customer interactions. I established a framework covering data quality standards, source system ownership, PII handling protocols, and audit requirements. **14. How do you approach large-scale data platform delivery?**

*Answer:* I focus on incremental value delivery while building for scale. In my experience scaling data infrastructure for mobile banking, I prioritized foundational architecture decisions first—data models, integration patterns, security frameworks—then delivered capabilities in waves. Wave 1 focused on critical customer data flows, Wave 2 on analytics, Wave 3 on real-time processing. Each wave delivered measurable business value while building toward the enterprise vision. This approach maintained stakeholder confidence and allowed us to course-correct based on learnings.

**15. Describe your experience with data quality programs.**

*Answer:* Data quality requires both prevention and detection. At Wells Fargo, I implemented a multi-layered approach: source system validation rules, integration-layer data checks, and business-rule validation. Critical was establishing ownership—each data domain had a designated steward responsible for quality metrics. I created dashboards showing data quality trends by source system and established SLAs: Tier 1 data (customer accounts) needed 99.5% accuracy, Tier 2 (preferences) needed 95%. We measured weekly and held monthly reviews where stewards presented improvement plans.

**16. How do you manage data integration across multiple source systems?**

*Answer:* I start with a clear integration architecture and governance model. On the Fargo AI project, we integrated data from 14 source systems. I established an integration framework defining canonical data models, transformation standards, and error handling protocols. Each source system had documented mappings and transformation logic. I set up automated integration testing and data reconciliation processes. When conflicts arose—like different systems having different "customer names"—I facilitated decisions on system of record and implemented clear precedence rules.

**17. Tell me about implementing modern data tooling in an enterprise environment.**

I created a dual-track approach: maintain legacy system stability while building new capabilities in modern tools. I established clear criteria for which workloads moved to new platforms and which stayed on legacy. I also invested heavily in change management—training programs, documentation, and support channels. We migrated 60% of analytics workloads to modern platforms over 18 months without data outages.

**18. How do you approach enterprise data modeling?**

*Answer:* I balance consistency with pragmatism. At Wells Fargo, I participated in defining enterprise data models for customer, account, and transaction entities. Rather than trying to model everything upfront, I focused on high-value domains first and let models evolve based on actual use cases. I established versioning standards and backward compatibility requirements so model changes didn't break existing integrations. I also created a review process where proposed model changes went through architecture review to ensure enterprise alignment.

**19. Describe your experience with data platform ROI measurement.**

*Answer:* I track both efficiency gains and business impact. For Wells Fargo's mobile banking data infrastructure, I measured: reduced time-to-insight for analytics (from 3 days to 4 hours), cost savings from infrastructure optimization ($2.4M annually), and business impact from improved personalization (12% increase in feature adoption). **20. How do you handle competing data requirements from different business units?**

*Answer:* I establish clear prioritization criteria and transparent decision-making. At Wells Fago, marketing wanted real-time customer behavior data while risk needed historical transaction patterns—both requiring significant data platform investment. I facilitated a prioritization session using business impact, implementation complexity, and strategic alignment as criteria. **21. Tell me about managing technical debt in data programs.**

*Answer:* I treat technical debt as a portfolio management problem. At Wells Fargo, I maintained a technical debt register quantifying impact and remediation cost. Each quarter, I allocated 20% of engineering capacity to debt reduction, prioritized by risk and business impact. For example, we had aging ETL jobs that were fragile but stable—I kept those running while focusing debt reduction on high-traffic data pipelines where failures had customer impact. **22. How do you ensure data security and compliance in your programs?**

*Answer:* I embed security and compliance from the start, not as afterthoughts. On Wells Fargo programs, I included compliance and security stakeholders in design reviews, established data classification standards (public, internal, confidential, restricted), and implemented role-based access controls. I created audit trails for sensitive data access and regular compliance reviews. I also built automated compliance checks into our CI/CD pipelines so issues were caught before production. Security was a standing agenda item in program reviews.
