### Career Highlights Page: From Timeline to Capability Architecture
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Category:** Career Strategy

**Decision:** Fundamentally restructure the career-highlights page from a chronological timeline of initiatives to a four-zone capability architecture (Scale, Range, AI Trajectory, Builder). Remove all company-centric organization. Minimize company names and dates. Organize by what Kiran brings, not where he's been.

**Context:** The existing page has 30+ initiative cards organized by employer and role. While the content is thorough, it activates multiple negative hiring biases: breadth reads as shallowness, volume triggers disbelief, company names trigger banking stigma, and chronological layout invites timeline scrutiny. Kiran has been job hunting for 6 months and the page isn't serving its purpose. He's returning to Wells Fargo within 30 days, which will make the chronological layout even more problematic ("Wells → consulting → Wells" reads as regression).

**Reasoning:** The page's job isn't to document everything Kiran has done — it's to make a hiring manager think "seasoned, knows his stuff, I want to connect." That's a first-impression job, not an evidence-dump job. By organizing around capabilities instead of employers, the banking stigma doesn't fire (banks aren't named), the breadth reads as compounding range (domains inform each other), and the volume is contained (4 zones with focused proof points, not 30+ cards). The detailed initiative cards become a second-act artifact, surfaced after the visitor has self-identified and indicated what they're looking for.

**Impact:** Complete replacement of career-highlights.html content area. Page rename from "Career Highlights" to "Track Record." Potential downstream changes to navigation labels, homepage card titles, and any internal links referencing the old page name. The initiative cards aren't deleted — they'll be repositioned as curated depth content served through a persona-identified flow or Fenix.

**Open Questions:** How does the "second act" work mechanically? Does the visitor click through to filtered initiative cards? Does Fenix serve the curated depth? Does a persona-picker-style flow handle the self-identification? This needs a separate design session.

### Removing the Access Gate
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Remove the code-to-unlock access gate on the career-highlights page. The page is now fully public.

**Context:** The gate was originally implemented to prevent recruiters from misinterpreting the volume and breadth of Kiran's experience without context. The idea was to let visitors self-identify before seeing the full page, so content could be presented through a relevant lens. In practice, the gate was a friction barrier that most visitors wouldn't pass.

**Reasoning:** The new four-zone architecture solves the problem the gate was trying to solve — it controls the narrative through structure rather than access restriction. A capability-organized page doesn't need a gate because the framing itself prevents misinterpretation. Additionally, the relational principle from SITE-WHY.md ("invitation, not a sales pitch") is better served by openness than by gatekeeping.

**Impact:** One HTML attribute change (locked → unlocked). The gate CSS and JS remain in the codebase but are dormant. They can be removed entirely when the page is rebuilt.

### Page Naming: "Track Record" Over "Career Highlights"
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Category:** Brand/Identity

**Decision:** Rename the career-highlights page to "Track Record" in navigation and page title.

**Context:** Multiple alternatives were considered: "The Work," "My Journey," "My Product DNA," "My Craft," "What I've Built," "My Impact," "My Track Record." The evaluation criteria shifted during the discussion from "what sounds good" to "what would a recruiter click." Kiran pushed the conversation toward recruiter psychology: the name needs to trigger a click from someone scanning the nav and wondering "where do I see what this person has actually done?"

**Reasoning:** "Track Record" is the exact vocabulary hiring managers use when evaluating candidates. It promises proof, implies results accumulated over time, and sets no format expectations. It pairs well with "How I'd've Built It" (teardowns) in the nav — one shows what he's done, the other shows how he thinks. The possessive "My" was dropped because shorter is stronger in navigation and the possessive is implied on a personal site.

**Impact:** Affects nav labels across all pages, the page's `<title>` tag, meta description, canonical URL (potentially), and any cross-references from other pages. The URL may stay as career-highlights.html for SEO continuity with a redirect strategy, or change to track-record.html — this is a technical decision for the build session.

### Consulting Work: Not on the Public Record
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Category:** Career Strategy

**Decision:** Avatour and other consulting engagements will NOT appear on the Track Record page. They may be mentioned in conversations and interviews but are not part of the public career narrative.

**Context:** Kiran hasn't broadcast his layoff from Wells Fargo or his consulting work. When he returns to Wells (within 30 days), his HR record is being resurrected — he's not entering as a new hire. The public narrative will be that he moved teams, not that he left and returned. The consulting work exists as real experience (VP-level product strategy, 3.2x TAM expansion at Avatour) but surfacing it publicly creates a "Wells → Consulting → Wells" timeline that reads as regression.

**Reasoning:** The Track Record page's four-zone structure doesn't require company attribution. The metrics from consulting work (3.2x TAM expansion, AI summarization, conversational agents) can be included as capability evidence without naming Avatour or identifying the work as consulting. When relevant in 1:1 conversations, Kiran can mention the startup experience as additional context. But the page should not create a narrative that invites timeline scrutiny.

**Impact:** Content curation for the Track Record page — consulting metrics may appear but without company attribution. Resume customization (handled separately by the resume skill) may include Avatour depending on the target role. LinkedIn remains unchanged for now.

### Content Zone Terminology: "End-to-End User Journey" Over "Digital Experience"
**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Category:** Brand/Identity

**Decision:** Use "end-to-end user journey" instead of "digital experience" when describing the scope of Kiran's product work.

**Context:** "Digital experience" was flagged as dated — it implies a distinction between digital and non-digital that doesn't exist anymore. "Customer experience" was considered but rejected because it's been co-opted by CX/support organizations and could confuse Kiran's product role with a support role.

**Reasoning:** "End-to-end user journey" is current PM vocabulary that maps directly to Kiran's actual passion ("I've always owned the user problem"). It works at every target company, doesn't date itself, and doesn't get confused with CX support work. It centers the user rather than the technology, which is Kiran's genuine orientation.

**Impact:** Affects copy on the Track Record page (Zone 2 description) and potentially other site copy that references Kiran's scope. May also inform resume and cover letter language.
