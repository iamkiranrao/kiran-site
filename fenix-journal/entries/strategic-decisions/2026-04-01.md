### Renaming "Track Record" / "Career Highlights" → "The Work"
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Brand/Identity | Content Strategy

**Decision:** The page and homepage bento card were renamed from "Career Highlights" / "Track Record" to "The Work." This is the permanent public name.

**Context:** "Career Highlights" felt like a resume section header — generic, corporate, and passive. "Track Record" was the working title during development but still framed the content as a backward-looking scorecard. The page's actual purpose is showing the *work itself* — what was built, when, and why it matters — not a highlight reel.

**Reasoning:** "The Work" is direct, confident, and lets the content speak without framing it as either a sales pitch or a retrospective. It's also the kind of language someone uses when they're proud of what they've built without needing to explain why you should be impressed.

**Impact:** Homepage bento card updated (bento-cards.js `stat` field). OG image regenerated. All internal references need to migrate from "Track Record" to "The Work."

**Open Questions:** None — this is locked.

### Privacy Framing as Page Architecture, Not Disclaimer
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Product Philosophy | Brand/Identity

**Decision:** The privacy/framing note ("I've worked with incredible teams at companies I respect...") was restyled from a muted footnote into a prominent context-setting card with amber left border. It sits between the hero and the legend as the first thing a reader encounters after the title.

**Context:** Kiran's first instinct was to have this as a quiet disclaimer. But the framing note isn't a disclaimer — it's the lens through which the entire page should be read. It tells the reader: (1) this is intentionally restrained, (2) there's more behind it, and (3) the invitation to connect is genuine, not a sales tactic. Making it visually prominent means readers encounter the frame before they encounter the content.

**Reasoning:** The note does triple duty: it preempts "why aren't there more details?" skepticism, it establishes respect for former colleagues and companies, and it positions the page as an invitation to a conversation rather than a complete narrative. That's too important to be a footnote.

**Impact:** Changes how aggressively cards need to be trimmed (the note gives permission for restraint), how the CTA lands (it echoes the note), and how the page reads on first impression.

**Open Questions:** None.

### "Responded" Arrows Are the Core Story, Not a Weakness
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Career Strategy | Brand/Identity

**Decision:** Stopped trying to inflate the "already there" ← arrow count. Accepted the 8/14/4 ratio (already/responded/ahead) as honest. Reframed the arrow summary from a tally to a statement: "Every role, the hardest problem on the roadmap. Never maintenance. Never lights-on."

**Context:** Claude was pushing to reclassify five "responded" → arrows as "already there" ← arrows by asking whether Kiran had been "already planning" various capabilities before the market events that triggered them. Kiran pushed back on the premise: platform capabilities have to exist before you can build on them. You can't ship biometric login before Face ID ships.

**Reasoning:** The honest story is speed and quality of execution when the moment arrives, not fictional prescience. Fourteen "responded" arrows isn't a weakness — it's what most companies actually hire for. The "already there" arrows are impressive *because* they're rare and genuine, not because there are a lot of them. Trying to manufacture more would undermine the honest ones.

**Impact:** Arrow summary rewording. No further reclassification exercises. The page's argument shifts from "I predict the future" to "I'm always working on the hardest current problem."

**Open Questions:** The final wording of the arrow summary/closing statement evolved further in the production build — "The outcomes didn't happen by accident" is the current live version.

### Correcting the AI Timeline: Honesty Over Narrative Convenience
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Career Strategy | Brand/Identity

**Decision:** Corrected DOC AI start date from 2016 to 2021 and Eagle Invest to 2016. Changed the AI narrative from "built AI before anyone" to "10 years shipping AI products at increasing depth." The honest framing: Eagle Invest was PM-ing a product with ML under the hood (BlackRock's FutureAdvisor), not building AI. DOC AI was a genuine greenfield AI build, starting in 2021.

**Context:** The previous framing ("built years before 'AI product manager' was a job title") was based on DOC AI starting in 2016. It didn't. The correction meant the "9 years building with AI" synthesis stat and the "ahead of the curve" positioning were both wrong. Multiple prototype iterations had been built on this false assumption.

**Reasoning:** Kiran drew a clear line: "I was the PM for a platform that used ML under the hood" is the honest framing for Eagle Invest. That's AI product management — deciding what ML does for users — but it's not "building AI." The distinction matters because a technical interviewer who asks "what was your role in the ML?" would see through an inflated claim. At the same time, 2016-2026 IS 10 years of working with AI-powered products, and underselling that would be equally dishonest.

**Impact:** DOC AI world event moved from "2016-20" to "2021." Synthesis stat changed from "9 yrs Building with AI" to "10 yrs Shipping AI Products." Eagle Invest arrow changed from responded → to already ←. The AI thread through Era 2 is thinner than previously assumed.

**Open Questions:** Eagle Invest metrics still pending — Kiran is checking with former colleagues. The strength of this card depends on whether he can get adoption/AUM numbers.

### Evidence Taxonomy: Four Types, Differentiated by What They Prove
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Product Philosophy | Architecture

**Decision:** Expanded the skills page evidence system from 3 types (certification, prototype, project) to 4 by adding `teardown`. Each type has distinct visual identity (blue, green, gold, amber) and distinct modal content structure. The evidence set grew from 16 items with 44 links to 26 items with 89 links.

**Context:** The original evidence system treated certifications as the primary proof of skill. But certs are the weakest form of evidence — they prove you completed a program, not that you can apply the knowledge. Prototypes and teardowns are far stronger because visitors can evaluate the actual work. The question was: what from the site's body of work actually merits being called "evidence"?

**Reasoning:** Teardowns needed their own type because they're fundamentally different from prototypes: you didn't build something, you analyzed something. The modal labels reflect this — "What I Analyzed" instead of "What It Is", "Methods" instead of "Tech Stack", "What This Proves" instead of "Why It Matters." The amber color distinguishes them visually without competing with the green (built) or gold (shipped) types.

The key insight: teardowns are *stronger* evidence for strategy and analysis skills than certifications, because the visitor can read the actual analysis and judge the thinking for themselves. A cert says "I passed a test." A teardown says "here's how I think — judge for yourself."

**Impact:** Skills page data model expanded significantly. The graph view is denser but readable. Each published teardown and MadLab bot now contributes to the evidence graph, creating a compounding return on content creation.

**Open Questions:** The evidence data currently lives inline in skills.html. The maintenance question (how to update when new work ships) led to the CC integration decision below.

### Evidence Maintenance via CC Publishing Pipelines
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Category:** Architecture | Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Instead of building a standalone evidence management system, add an "Add Skills" feature to Command Center that serves as the shared evidence management primitive. MadLab and teardown publishing pipelines will call into this at the end of their flows. Manual entry handles certs and one-off projects.

**Context:** Four options were evaluated — manual checklist, external JSON file, CC API endpoint, and a hybrid (JSON now, CC later). Each had tradeoffs around complexity, operational friction, and long-term scalability.

**Reasoning:** Kiran's insight cut through the options: the publishing pipelines for MadLab bots and teardowns already exist in CC. Adding an evidence step to those flows means evidence updates happen at the natural moment — when you ship something — rather than as a separate maintenance task you might forget. The CC "Add Skills" page handles the data entry, the publish pipelines consume it, and the skills page renders whatever CC serves.

Projects are too infrequent for a pipeline — they get caught during periodic audits.

**Impact:** This is a CC feature build, not a site change. The skills page will eventually consume data from CC instead of having it baked into the HTML. Unlocks the pattern of "every publish enriches the evidence graph."

**Open Questions:** Build scope and timeline not yet defined. Whether the skills page fetches from CC API at runtime or CC generates a static JSON file on publish — that's an implementation choice to make during the build.
