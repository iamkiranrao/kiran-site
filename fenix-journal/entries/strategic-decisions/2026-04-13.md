### Kill Defensive Copy — Shift from Persuasion to Evidence
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Content Strategy / Brand Identity

**Decision:** Rewrite all visitor-facing copy across the evaluator experience to drop defensive framing entirely. Move from "making a case" and "building an argument" language to direct, confident language that describes what the system shows rather than defending what it is.

**Context:** Kiran identified that every major copy section carried an undertone of apology — "we're not a traditional score," "we're making an argument for," "here's why this is valid." The system was talking defensively about itself instead of talking about what it revealed. Every defensive phrase suggested doubt about whether the visitor should trust the approach.

Kiran's reframe: "If you thought I was the most amazing PM that a company would be absolutely nuts not to hire, what would you suggest?" That question dissolved the defensive frame entirely. Stop explaining why the system is valid. Just show what it shows.

**Reasoning:** Confidence isn't arrogance. It's the absence of apology. When you're confident in the evidence, you don't need to convince the reader to accept it — you just describe it. The shift from "we're building a case" to "here's what the evidence shows" removes the subtext of persuasion and replaces it with subtext of clarity. The visitor's mental model changes from "the system wants me to believe something" to "the system is showing me something true."

This also serves Kiran's positioning directly. If the fit narrative is positioned as confident, Kiran is positioned as confident. If it apologizes for how it works, Kiran is apologizing by proxy.

**Impact:**
- All introductions, context-setting, and supporting copy reframed
- Card title evolved: "What Differentiates Kiran for Your Role" (vs. "What Kiran Brings" or "Why Kiran for This Role")
- Narrative language shifted from "we evaluated" to "here's what differentiates"
- Removed all meta-commentary about what the system isn't doing
- Copy now starts from what the visitor gets, not what they should accept

**Files touched:** evaluator-adapter.js, evaluator-experience.js, fit_score.py

### The Fit Narrative Is a Product, Not a Response
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** The fit narrative output should be designed as a product experience (with structured components, scannable layout, multiple entry points) rather than as a prose response to a user query. This means moving from streaming markdown text to structured JSON events that render as designed cards, callouts, and sections.

**Context:** Kiran reviewed the improved fit narrative output (after initiative wiring) and said it "still looks like a tool ate something and spat it out." The narrative content was stronger, but the presentation was still tool-like — one long block of prose that happened to answer the JD.

He wanted it to feel like a designed product page: headers that orient, evidence cards that scan, context sections that explain, and follow-ups that guide the visitor. The v3 prototype showed what that could look like.

**Reasoning:** The difference between a "response" and a "product" is intentionality. A response answers a question. A product anticipates needs. In this case, the visitor doesn't just want to know "how does Kiran fit this JD?" They want to understand:
- Why this analysis is different from a resume (meta lead-in)
- What evidence maps to what requirement (scannable cards)
- What was considered but didn't make the cut (cutting room floor)
- What follows from this (next actions)

Each of these is a section in the v3 prototype. The prose narrative is one component (the evidence card descriptions), not the whole output.

This also changes how the backend should work. The fit_score endpoint shouldn't return prose — it should return structured data (selected initiatives, JD mapped requirements, cutting room floor items, key insight). The frontend renders that data as a designed experience.

**Impact:**
- Endpoint changes from streaming prose to streaming JSON events
- Frontend gains new components: evidence cards, cutting room floor grid, key takeaway callout, action pills, sharing features
- The product becomes more intentional about what it shows and in what order
- Kiran's work gets presented as designed thinking, not tool output

**Technical implication:** The narrative prompt engineering and streaming optimization from Session 8 becomes partially obsolete. We're moving to structured output instead of prose optimization. This is directional correction, not incremental improvement.

**Open Questions:**
- How much context should the cutting room floor show? Just title + how it maps to the JD, or full detail?
- Should the key takeaway be one sentence or multiple? Should it be a direct insight or a question the visitor should ask?
- Do action pills stay generic ("Schedule a call", "Learn more about Kiran") or become JD-specific?

### The Meta IS the Proof — Site Architecture as Hiring Proof
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Brand Identity / Product Philosophy

**Decision:** The fit narrative's approach to showing context, explaining what was chosen and why, and displaying what was left out becomes part of Kiran's hiring narrative. The transparency of the approach IS the evidence that Kiran builds systems with care.

**Context:** During the v3 prototype discussion, Kiran asked "are we adding friction with all the extra context?" Then he answered his own question: "Isn't showcasing the thinking the most powerful thing?"

The "friction" he was questioning was the cutting room floor section — showing all the relevant initiatives that didn't make the top 3. It would be simpler to just show three initiatives. But showing the thinking (what we considered, what we chose, what the constraints were) is more powerful than simplicity.

**Reasoning:** This principle cascades beyond the fit narrative. The entire site is built on the idea that "the medium is the message" — that how Kiran builds things is proof of how he thinks. The fit narrative applies that principle directly.

A visitor sees:
- Confidence in how the system is framed
- Intelligence in what evidence was selected and why
- Honesty about what was considered
- Design intention in how it's presented

All of that IS the hire signal. The visitor doesn't just learn "Kiran is qualified for this role" — they learn "Kiran builds systems that are transparent about tradeoffs, considers multiple options, and designs for clarity."

**Impact:**
- The fit narrative becomes a hiring demo, not just a tool output
- Future improvements should prioritize transparency over simplicity
- This principle should apply to other product decisions: what looks like "adding friction" might actually be adding proof
- Kiran's positioning becomes even more architectural — not just "I have this skill" but "I think about systems this way"

### Wire the Full Initiative Set Into the Narrative Engine
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Architecture / Data Strategy

**Decision:** Move the fit narrative from matching against a hardcoded 5-bullet KIRAN_PROFILE to matching against initiatives.json (all 32 initiatives). This includes both selecting the right initiatives for each JD and removing the "Breaking out of banking" career-transition framing that was poisoning every match.

**Context:** Kiran discovered that the fit narrative was generating against a 5-bullet hardcoded profile while the entire site contained 32 structured career initiatives with full problem/bet/shipped/outcome detail. He was legitimately angry: "The entire site is filled with my work history... Why is your context so limited?"

The KIRAN_PROFILE also contained "Target direction: Breaking out of banking" which was forcing every narrative into career-transition framing — even for roles where banking experience IS the core qualification.

**Reasoning:**
- **Data integrity:** Use the data you have. Don't create a redundant, stale profile when rich context is available.
- **Bias removal:** The hardcoded profile forced a narrative frame. The selection engine lets each JD determine which initiatives matter most.
- **Scale:** With initiatives.json as source, the endpoint can adapt to any JD without hardcoding. For new initiatives, just update the JSON. No code changes needed.
- **Quality:** A 5-bullet summary loses detail. The full initiative record (problem statement, bets made, shipped work, outcomes) gives Claude much richer context for narrative generation.

**Impact:**
- Single Claude call now does: extract JD requirements + select 5-8 most relevant initiatives
- Narrative prompt gets full initiative detail instead of 5-bullet summary
- No more "Breaking out of banking" frame on every JD
- Architecture is now data-driven instead of profile-driven

**Files changed:** api/v1/fenix/fit_score.py (endpoint), dependencies on initiatives.json

**Remaining gap:** The 32 initiatives are the "hero bullets" that survived resume compression. But 15 years of experience includes unsexy work (compliance, regulatory, infrastructure, operational support) that a specific JD might need. The gap analysis engine (Phase 4) will systematically identify what's missing for a given JD and surface it as a data opportunity. Current message: "These are the initiatives that map" (positive). Future message: "These map AND here's what you might want to know about that could be useful" (complete).

### Gap Analysis as a Systematic Product Feature
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Product Philosophy / Architecture (concept stage)

**Decision:** Build a gap analysis feature that systematically identifies which career evidence isn't captured in the initiative set for a given JD. This turns a limitation (32 initiatives might not be exhaustive) into a product insight (here's what might matter that we haven't surfaced yet).

**Context:** When Kiran discussed the initiative set's coverage, he noted that it captures the "hero bullets" but misses the unsexy work — compliance frameworks, regulatory navigation, operational support — that specific JDs might require. This wasn't framed as "our system is incomplete" but as "here's a vault we can interrogate."

The question: What if the fit narrative said "these five initiatives map your experience to the key requirements, AND here's what might be worth discussing that we haven't surfaced"?

**Reasoning:**
- Limitations become assets if you're transparent about them
- The visitor learns "we selected 5-8 from 32" which signals depth
- The gap analysis surfaces not missing information but hidden information ("here's what we didn't surface because it wasn't in the structure, but it might matter")
- This positions Kiran as thinking systematically about evidence — not just what he's shipped, but what the hiring context specifically needs

**Impact:** (Conceptual for now)
- Endpoint could run a second pass: "Given the JD and the 5-8 selected initiatives, what evidence gaps exist?"
- Output could include: suggested discussion topics, reference materials to share, deeper dives available on request
- This turns the limitation of a finite initiative set into a feature: the vault is rich enough to surface gaps

**Technical approach:** (Next phase)
- After selecting initiatives, run Claude prompt: "Given these initiatives and this JD, what experience is implied but not explicitly surfaced?"
- Generate gap analysis output (2-3 suggested topics)
- Integrate into the fit narrative as "what might be worth exploring"

**Open Questions:**
- Should gap analysis be automatic or triggered by user request?
- How specific should suggested topics be? ("regulatory frameworks" vs. "DODD-FRANK implementation experience")
- Should there be a way for Kiran to add gap bridging content (like video interviews or case studies) that address common gaps?

## Session 10 Decisions

### Gap Coverage as a Unified System, Not Scattered Features
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Architecture / Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Consolidate all gap-related functionality (Mind the Gap, Career Initiatives vault, Resume Gap Analysis, Gap Discovery Engine) into one coherent system with a single spec, shared schema extensions, and a defined feedback loop.

**Context:** Seven different features built across different sessions each touched some aspect of gap discovery and evidence management. Mind the Gap had 37 items in Supabase. Career Initiatives had 32 entries in JSON. Resume analysis produced gap reports that went nowhere. The fit narrative matched JDs without reporting what was missing. None of these systems talked to each other.

**Reasoning:** Individual features solving parts of the same problem create data silos and cognitive overhead. When Kiran looks at a gap in Mind the Gap, he can't see which JDs surfaced it. When the fit narrative runs, it can't tell him what's missing. When the resume analyzer finds a gap, it doesn't check if it's already tracked. A unified system turns these isolated outputs into a compounding loop: discover gaps, close them, grow the vault, strengthen the narrative.

**Impact:**
- New spec: GAP-COVERAGE-SYSTEM-SPEC.md (the system blueprint)
- Schema additions to `evidence_gap_items`: discovered_from, source tracking, fill strategy fields, vault linkage
- Career initiatives vault gets its own CC page (not nested in add-skills)
- Build sequence: Vault View → Gap Discovery Engine → Resume Pipeline Connection → Aggregate Intelligence
- Deferred: JD scraping, Fenix RAG, bulk import

### The Vault Gets Its Own Page
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Architecture / Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Career Initiatives (the "vault") gets a dedicated Command Center page, not a tab buried in the Add Skills feature.

**Context:** Kiran described the vault's history: career-highlights cards from an abandoned page that he didn't want to lose. He envisioned a place to see those cards, add more as gaps closed, and watch the collection grow. The current implementation stores them in initiatives.json with a CRUD API but no dedicated view — the data is consumed by the fit narrative backend but invisible as a standalone asset.

**Reasoning:** The vault is the backbone of the gap coverage system. Every closed gap becomes a new initiative. Every initiative makes the fit narrative stronger. If the vault is buried in a tab, this feedback loop is invisible. The vault view should make the trajectory visible — showing domain coverage strength, thin areas, and the growth over time. It's not just storage; it's the system's heartbeat.

**Impact:**
- New CC dashboard page with card grid, filter sidebar (domain/company/era), expand to full detail
- "Coverage map" header: visual showing strong vs thin evidence domains
- "Add from gap closure" workflow: completed Mind the Gap item → new vault initiative
- Phase 1 of build sequence (1-2 sessions)

### Prescriptive Fill Strategy Over Option Menus
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** The gap discovery engine must assign exactly ONE fill tier per gap and generate a specific, actionable recommendation — not present a menu of options.

**Context:** When I first described fill strategies, I presented options: "you could take a course, build a prototype, write a teardown." Kiran pushed back immediately. He wanted the system to make the call.

**Reasoning:** Kiran's philosophy is that good systems have opinions. A system that presents four options and waits is passing the decision cost to the user. A system that says "here's what to do and here's how long it takes" is doing the actual work of analysis. The four tiers (Articulate → Build Proof → Certify → True Gap) aren't categories to choose from — they're a decision tree the system traverses. "You led compliance modernization at First Republic but it's not in the vault. Write up the initiative — 2 hours. Done." Not "consider the following approaches."

This connects to the Session 9 confidence reframe. Stop presenting options. Start making recommendations.

**Impact:**
- Four-tier fill strategy built into gap classification logic
- Each gap gets: fill_tier, fill_action, fill_time_estimate, fill_output
- True Gap tier is explicitly NOT a task — it's a positioning decision ("is this role family worth pursuing?")
- System outputs look like "Here's what to do" not "Here are some ideas"

### Product Guides Absorbed into Knowledge Library
**Date:** 2026-04-13
**Category:** Architecture

**Decision:** Product Guides (structured YAML-metadata CC docs) should be absorbed into Knowledge Library as a category filter, not maintained as a separate navigation item.

**Context:** Kiran showed me the CC sidebar — it was crowded. He asked if Product Guides was defunct and covered by Knowledge Library. Both features serve the same fundamental need: structured reference documentation accessible through search or browse.

**Reasoning:** Product Guides had YAML metadata (version, section completeness) that Knowledge Library didn't. But that's a metadata problem, not a separate-feature problem. Absorbing Product Guides as a "Guides" category filter in Knowledge Library preserves the metadata while removing a navigation item. One fewer thing to maintain, one fewer thing for Kiran to remember exists.

**Impact:**
- Sidebar gets one fewer nav item
- Knowledge Library gains a "Guides" category filter
- YAML metadata (version, completeness) surfaces within KL, not lost
- Separate task, not part of gap system scope
