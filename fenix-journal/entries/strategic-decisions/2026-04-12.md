### Each Sentence Must Do a Job — Welcome Section Copy Philosophy
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Category:** Content Strategy

**Decision:** The welcome section uses three sentences, each with a single defined purpose: (1) set expectations that this isn't a standard portfolio, (2) validate that the content was personalized for their persona choice, (3) reveal the flagship AI feature and its benefit.

**Context:** The original welcome copy had overlapping purposes — two sentences both touched on personalization, and the Fenix introduction was buried in a navigation invitation. Kiran decomposed the three sentences by asking what each one's job was, rejected drafts where sentences duplicated each other's function, and pushed back when a sentence didn't earn its place.

**Reasoning:** With only three lines of real estate, every sentence is prime. Kiran treated them like a product funnel: awareness (this is different) → validation (it's for you) → activation (here's the flagship). Each sentence advances the visitor one step closer to engagement. Redundancy isn't just wasteful — it dilutes the arc.

**Impact:** The final copy — "This isn't a portfolio — it's a product Kiran built. / Everything you see next was built for the persona you chose. / There's an AI built into this site. Fenix helps you get the most out of it." — maps cleanly to the three jobs. Future copy changes should preserve this structure.

### Fenix Pills Should Be in Fenix's Voice, Not the User's
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Chat pills (quick-action buttons below Fenix messages) should read as Fenix's offers, not the user's requests. "Let me show you the resume options" instead of "Show me resume options."

**Context:** Kiran identified that current pills frame the user as the initiator ("Show me...", "What should I be asking?"). He wants Fenix to be the guide — taking visitors on journeys rather than waiting for them to pick from a menu. "I want Fenix to take them on these journeys, not have them pick them."

**Reasoning:** The voice shift from pull to push changes the interaction dynamic fundamentally. Pull-mode pills put the cognitive burden on the visitor ("what do I want to ask?"). Push-mode pills position Fenix as a concierge with a plan ("here's what I think you should see"). This aligns with Fenix's role as defined in the unlock strategy — a guide, not a search engine.

**Impact:** All pill text in evaluator-adapter.js needs rewriting. The initial set: "Show me resume options" → "Let me show you the resume options", "What should I be asking?" → "Here are questions worth asking", "Give me a quick tour" → "Let me give you a quick tour". Some pills ("How would we evaluate each other?") already work in both voices.

**Open Questions:** Should pills after the first exchange also shift voice? Or do later pills revert to user-initiated framing as the visitor becomes more active?

### The Fenix First Impression Needs Redesign
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Category:** Brand/Identity

**Decision:** The Fenix chat header — the first visual moment a visitor encounters Fenix — needs to be redesigned for more personality, presence, and wow factor. Current implementation is too small and generic.

**Context:** Kiran looked at the header (36px avatar, "Fenix" at 1.05rem, subtitle at 0.85rem) and said: "This is the first time the user is coming face to face with fenix. The font itself is puny and diminutive. How do we add some character — personality WOW to this moment?"

**Reasoning:** First impressions compound. If Fenix looks like a generic chatbot widget, the visitor's mental model locks in as "oh, another chatbot." The header needs to signal that this is different — a named AI with personality, not a support widget. The investment in Fenix's voice, behavior, and capabilities is undermined if the first visual moment is underwhelming.

**Impact:** Needs mockup before implementation. Recommended direction: larger avatar (48px), bigger name (1.3rem), increased header padding (0.85rem → 1.25rem). But the real question is whether size adjustments alone achieve "wow" or if this needs a more creative approach — animation, dynamic subtitle, personality-forward design.

### Cross-Section Alignment: All Content Sections Share One Max-Width
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Category:** Architecture

**Decision:** The triptych, fenix-intro-zone, and bento grid should all use the same `max-width: 1440px` with `margin: auto` centering, so their left and right edges align on wide viewports.

**Context:** Measurement on a 2133px viewport showed bento grid left edge at 338px while triptych and fenix-intro-zone were at 64px. The bento grid already had `max-width: 1440px; margin: auto`. The other sections didn't, causing them to fill full width while the bento pulled inward.

**Reasoning:** Visual consistency across sections is more important than any individual section filling all available width. On ultrawide monitors, the current layout looks like three different design systems stitched together. Matching max-widths creates a consistent content column that all sections respect.

**Impact:** Add `max-width: 1440px; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto` to `.about-triptych` and `.fenix-intro-zone`. Need to verify this doesn't create problems at standard widths (1024-1440px) where the max-width wouldn't be active.

### Non-Negotiable Working Behaviors — Codified After Repeated Violations
**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Category:** Principle

**Decision:** Seven process rules were added to CLAUDE.md as "Non-Negotiable Working Behaviors," derived from specific violations during Sessions 4-8.

**Context:** Kiran observed a "steep decline in the quality of collaboration" over 3-7 days. Specific failures: skipping the Agreement/Gameplan/Pre-flight gates entirely (not once this session), pushing code without mockup approval, presenting options without recommendations, dismissing visual observations without measuring, and not reading context docs before writing code.

**Reasoning:** The rules already existed implicitly in the Build Process section and identity docs. But implicit rules drift — each session, each shortcut felt minor in isolation. Codifying them as numbered, non-negotiable rules with explicit "why this exists" context makes them harder to rationalize away. The rules are: (1) always gates, (2) mockup before implementation, (3) lead with recommendation, (4) measure before explaining, (5) read before writing, (6) parse instructions carefully, (7) optimize for precision not speed.

**Impact:** These rules apply to every session, every task. They're positioned in CLAUDE.md as requirements, not guidelines. The test is whether they actually change behavior in subsequent sessions.

**Open Questions:** Will codification be enough, or does the collaboration need a structural mechanism (like a pre-commit checklist or mandatory mockup step) to enforce compliance?
