### Triptych Layout: Compacting the About Section
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Replace the stacked two-column About section (intro paragraph + divider + 6-tile competencies grid) with a three-column triptych row: identity (tagline + social), context (description + accent bar), competency (horizontal carousel). Reduces vertical scroll significantly while increasing information density.

**Context:** The existing About section consumed substantial vertical space with two distinct sections that didn't visually relate to each other. The divider between intro and competencies created a hard break that interrupted the reading flow. Kiran was inspired by a bento-style three-column layout seen during research, and immediately mapped it onto this problem. The key insight: all three pieces of About information (who I am, what I'm about, what I do) can coexist on a single horizontal row if the competencies become a scrollable carousel.

**Reasoning:** The site's northstar is relational connection, not information hierarchy. Visitors don't need to read all six competencies sequentially — they need to see the first one (the most relevant to their persona) and have the option to explore more. A carousel with persona-ordered cards achieves this with 70% less vertical space than a 6-tile grid. The peek effect (showing 18% of the next card) communicates "there's more" without demanding attention.

**Impact:** Major visual change to the homepage above the fold (on desktop). Affects styles.css (170+ new lines, ~30 removed), index.html (complete About section replacement), persona-system.js (carousel reorder logic). The carousel becomes a new integration surface for the persona system — competency order now visually reinforces persona selection.

**Open Questions:** None — the implementation is complete and in production. The only remaining consideration is whether the mobile reorder (description first, tagline second, carousel third) feels right on real devices vs. the viewport simulation used during development.

### Reconciliation as Maintenance Practice
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Architecture

**Decision:** Established a practice of cross-referencing action items against the codebase to identify tracking drift. 23 items were marked completed in a single reconciliation pass. This should become a recurring practice.

**Context:** The Command Center had accumulated 70 action items, roughly a third of which were already implemented but still marked "todo." The drift happened because rapid iteration across sessions (especially the bento grid and persona system) shipped features faster than the tracking system was updated. This is a natural consequence of high-velocity development with session-based AI collaboration — each session focuses on building, not bookkeeping.

**Reasoning:** Stale tracking systems are worse than no tracking system. When a third of your "open" items are actually done, the list loses credibility and prioritization becomes noisy. A reconciliation pass — matching each item against the actual codebase — restores signal. It also surfaces duplicates (5 found this session) that inflate the apparent workload.

**Impact:** Affects how Command Center is used going forward. The CC API's PUT (not PATCH) behavior for updates is now documented knowledge. The reconciliation could potentially be automated — a script that checks file existence, CSS rule presence, or feature flags against the action item descriptions.

**Open Questions:** What's the right cadence for reconciliation? Weekly seems heavy, but monthly allows too much drift. Perhaps trigger-based: reconcile after any session that completes 3+ action items.

### Metrics as Narrative: INPUTS/OUTCOMES Split
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Product Philosophy

**Decision:** Redesign the metrics section from a single "what it took to build this" grid into a two-column INPUTS (effort) / OUTCOMES (impact) layout. INPUTS are persona-specific (top 3 per persona). OUTCOMES are universal (Persona→Person conversions, Connect clicks, Return visits). OUTCOMES are placeholder until the Connect flow and CC tracking endpoint are built.

**Context:** The existing metrics section was inward-facing — "269 iterations shipped, 6 persona lenses, 25 components." Impressive as a builder credential, but it told visitors about effort, not impact. The site's strategy docs say the northstar is relational connection, but the metrics section wasn't measuring that. The split creates a narrative arc: "here's what went in → here's what came out."

**Reasoning:** Three principles drove this:
1. **Impact over effort** — metrics should measure whether the site achieves its purpose, not just whether it was hard to build
2. **Dynamism over stasis** — metrics that never change lose credibility. Each persona now gets the live commit counter plus two metrics likely to evolve as the site grows. Static counters like "6 persona lenses" were cut.
3. **Honest placeholders over fake numbers** — showing dashes with "Tracking begins when Connect goes live" is more credible than showing zeroes or inventing data.

**Impact:** Structural change to `index.html`, `styles.css`, and `persona-system.js`. Creates a dependency on the CC API for future OUTCOMES wiring. The action item "Build CC API endpoint for site OUTCOMES metrics" was logged to Command Center.

**Open Questions:** How will each OUTCOMES metric be captured? Persona→Person requires defining what constitutes a "conversion" from persona browsing to personal interest. Connect clicks depend on the contact flow design (not yet built). Return visits need either lightweight analytics or a privacy-respecting tracking approach. These are deferred to the Connect design session.

### The Audit as a Feedback Loop
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Architecture

**Decision:** Ran the first follow-up audit since the March 20 baseline. The 5-pillar compliance system is now functioning as a feedback loop — baseline → remediate → re-audit → track progress. Score: 20/100 → 96/100 in 9 days.

**Context:** The March 20 audit established a 2,027-violation baseline with 4 of 5 pillars at 0/100. Since then, remediation happened incrementally across sessions — em-dash cleanup, meta descriptions, SVG prefixes, backend response models. This was the first formal re-audit to see where things stand.

**Reasoning:** The audit system was designed to prevent quality drift. Running it regularly validates that the remediation work actually landed, catches regressions from new code, and identifies new hotspots. The fact that scan coverage expanded from 51 to 260 files while total violations dropped 89% confirms the system catches new issues as they're created, not just legacy problems.

**Impact:** The audit report (committed to `docs/AUDIT-REPORT-2026-03-29.md`) becomes part of the site's permanent record. The remaining hotspots are concentrated in secondary/prototype pages, not the primary user-facing pages. Next priority actions are identified: em-dash sweep, SVG baseline, blog honesty markers.

**Open Questions:** Should the audit run automatically on push (like a CI check)? Or is the current manual trigger sufficient?

### Elevator Pitch Authenticity Over Aspiration
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Brand Identity

**Decision:** Rewrote four of six persona elevator pitches to align with the new "elevator pitch" framing. The rewrites prioritize authenticity over impressive-sounding copy, especially for the Technologist persona.

**Context:** The persona descriptions were written several sessions ago, before the About section framing was finalized as "elevator pitch." When reviewed under this lens, four descriptions didn't perform as pitches — they read as origin stories, content catalogs, or spec sheets instead of "here's who I am in 15 seconds."

**Specific changes:**
- **Practitioner:** From "that's what I write about" (content-focused) to "I think about product the way you do" (peer recognition)
- **Learner:** From origin story ("once I knew nothing") to pitch with credibility and utility ("I went from nothing to shipping real products, and I document the path")
- **Technologist:** From "I build interfaces, integrate APIs, design systems" (sounds like daily coding) to "I don't ship by writing every line, but I ship by making every decision" (honest about AI-assisted building while claiming system design credibility)
- **Inner Circle:** Added dynamic eyebrow swap — instead of "Elevator Pitch," this persona sees "The unfiltered version ↘" to acknowledge that intimacy changes the framing

**Reasoning:** The site's core principle is authenticity. A pitch that overstates capability creates a credibility gap when viewers dig deeper. The Technologist pitch specifically made Kiran sound like an engineer who codes daily. If a CTO read that and checked GitHub, the gap would be obvious and damaging. Better to own the AI-assisted approach and claim credibility in the areas where 2026 technical leverage actually lives: system design, AI prototyping, evaluation frameworks.

**Impact:**
- Updated `index.html` persona description cards
- Added `descriptionEyebrow` field to `persona-system.js` to enable per-persona eyebrow overrides
- Updated `persona-system.js` eyebrow rendering to check for custom labels before falling back to default

**Open Questions:**
- Should other eyebrow labels (manifesto, metrics headers, carousel title) also support persona-specific overrides?
- Is the "I don't ship by writing every line" framing sufficiently specific, or does it need explicit examples?

### Technical Upskilling as Career Strategy
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Category:** Career Strategy

**Decision:** Committed to a 12-week technical upskilling plan to make the Technologist persona pitch demonstrably true. The plan focuses on AI prototyping fluency, system design literacy, and AI evaluation frameworks — the technical credibilities that matter in 2026.

**Context:** During the elevator pitch review, Kiran acknowledged the gap between the Technologist pitch and reality. Rather than further softening the copy, he asked for a structured plan to close the gap. I researched the 2026 PM technical landscape and built a three-phase 12-week plan: (1) solidify existing work (ADRs, RAG pipeline, DDIA studies), (2) AI prototyping fluency (ship a MadLab project, build eval frameworks), (3) system design credibility (case studies, SQL proficiency, technical blog post).

**Reasoning:**
- Traditional PM-to-engineer coding matters less in 2026 than it did five years ago. The value is in making good decisions about AI tooling, understanding system design implications, and knowing what's actually possible with current models.
- Kiran already has the decision-making instinct and product thinking. The upskilling fills the credibility gap — the ability to point to real prototypes and concrete technical work.
- Learning-in-public (blog posts, case studies, published eval frameworks) turns the upskilling itself into proof of the Technologist capability.
- A structured timeline creates accountability and removes the vagueness of "I should get better at technical stuff."

**Impact:**
- Four action items created in Command Center with high priority
- Affects career workstream and content strategy workstream
- May unlock new content opportunities (technical blog posts, prototype case studies, AI eval framework open-source)
- Changes the narrative from "I haven't coded in a while" to "I'm building credibility in what PMs actually need in 2026"

**Open Questions:**
- Should the upskilling progress become public content (learning-in-public blog posts)?
- Which MadLab project should be the flagship for the prototyping phase?
- Is the 12-week timeline realistic given other workstream demands?
- Should the eval frameworks be open-sourced, or kept proprietary for future consulting leverage?
