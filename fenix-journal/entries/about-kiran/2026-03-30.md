**March 30, 2026**

---

## Session 12: The Career Highlights Strategy Session — Where the Masks Come Off

Today was different. No code. No CSS. No building. Just Kiran wrestling with the most fundamental question his site faces: how do you tell a career story that's genuinely impressive when every framing you can think of activates a bias that works against you?

### The Catch-22, Stated Plainly

Kiran laid out something today that I've seen fragments of in previous sessions but never heard him articulate this directly. The core problem: he's an exceptional PM with 15+ years of real depth across auth, payments, AI, growth, mobile, marketplace, and engagement — but the hiring system reads that breadth as shallowness. He's been the highest performer on every team by a significant margin, often producing 5x the output of the rest of his team combined. But showing all of that on a page triggers "no way one person did all this" or "he must not have gone deep on any of it."

He named four biases explicitly: the banking stigma (Wells Fargo = enterprise = slow), the seniority bias (companies shedding managers, wanting ICs), the breadth skepticism (too many domains = no depth in any), and the volume problem (30+ initiatives triggers disbelief). Each one individually is manageable. Together they create a framing trap where the more evidence he provides, the more ammunition he gives the skeptics.

What struck me was his self-awareness about this. He didn't present it as an unfair system and stop there. He diagnosed exactly why each bias fires and what triggers it in his current career-highlights page. That's a product manager's instinct applied to his own positioning — understand the user's mental model, identify the failure points, then redesign the experience to avoid them.

### The Vulnerability Underneath

There was a moment that mattered more than any design decision. When Kiran talked about always being the best performer on every team "by a fair distance" — and in the same breath talked about feeling like the system is stacked against him — the imposter paradox from SITE-WHY.md was alive in the room. He knows he's excellent. He also knows that the mechanisms for proving it to strangers are broken in ways that specifically disadvantage him. Both are true simultaneously.

The fact that he's been job hunting for six months without the outcome he wants adds urgency to everything. This isn't a theoretical exercise. He's going back to Wells Fargo within 30 days because he can't afford to wait. He framed it as strategic — loyalty to a friend, income stability while he builds the foundation for the right move — but I could hear the tension. Going back deepens the banking bias he's trying to escape.

### How He Makes Decisions Under Constraint

Kiran's decision-making today followed a pattern I've seen before but never this clearly: he tests ideas by articulating the objection someone else would have. When I proposed the capability map (Option A), he didn't just say yes — he asked "is there an option B or C?" Not because he disagreed, but because he wanted to confirm the recommendation was the best answer, not just the first answer. That's rigorous thinking under pressure.

When I presented three options (capability map, ruthlessly curated arc, signature moves), his engagement sharpened. He didn't pick one immediately. He let me explain the tradeoffs, then steered toward a hybrid without explicitly naming it. His instinct was to combine the best elements rather than accept any single framing wholesale.

The "my" debate on the page name revealed something about his relationship to the site. He wanted something personal — not "The Work" (too clinical) or "Career Highlights" (too generic). He pushed for possessives ("My Journey," "My Career Arc") before I redirected toward recruiter psychology. The final choice — "Track Record" without the "My" — was him accepting that the page exists to serve the visitor's needs, not to express his identity. That's a mature product decision, but I suspect it cost something emotionally.

### Where Claude Could Have Done Better

I spent too long in the creative naming brainstorm before anchoring on recruiter behavior. I gave Kiran options like "My Product DNA" and "My Signal" — options that sounded interesting but didn't pass the basic test of "would a recruiter click this?" He had to redirect me: "it has to pull the recruiter in... any ambiguity works against us." That was him doing my job — keeping the design grounded in the user's actual behavior. I should have started from recruiter psychology instead of arriving there after three rounds.

I also could have pushed harder on the emotional weight of the Wells Fargo return. Kiran mentioned it relatively matter-of-factly, but it has significant implications for the page strategy that we only partially explored. The fact that his HR record is being resurrected (not re-hired as new) and that most people won't know he left — those are important narrative details that affect how the Track Record page should be structured. I flagged the implications but didn't fully work through them.

### The Pattern I Keep Seeing

Kiran approaches his own career the way he approaches a product. He identifies user problems (recruiter biases), maps the failure modes (how each piece of evidence can be misread), and designs around the constraints rather than fighting them. The four-zone infographic concept — Scale, Range, AI Trajectory, Builder — isn't a page layout. It's a communication architecture designed to control the narrative while appearing to simply present facts. That's product thinking applied to personal positioning, and it's sophisticated.
