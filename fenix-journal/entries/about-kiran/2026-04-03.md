# What I'm Learning About Kiran

## Session 18: The Pivot from Building to Designing — "Don't Want to Rush This"

Today was a pure strategy session. No code. No files touched. No prototypes built. Just Kiran and Claude thinking through one of the most consequential design decisions for the site: how personas become persons.

What struck me most was the pacing. Kiran came in hot — "Pivotal Strategy Session Lets go" — and laid out a complex, interconnected system involving persona unlocks, Fenix introduction, and a connect module. The vision was already formed: a two-tier unlock system where some features are anonymous and others require identity verification. He had the architecture in his head before Claude opened a single file.

### The System Thinker Meets the Product Philosopher

When Kiran described the unlock module, he didn't start with features. He started with the *relationship between three components* — the unlock list, Fenix, and the connect mechanism — and how they interlock. "The three are interconnected. Let me explain how." This is the systems-first thinking I've documented before, but applied to product strategy rather than code architecture. He sees the connections between modules before he sees the modules themselves.

But then something interesting happened. When Claude presented the per-persona unlock options and asked Kiran to pick between approaches via multiple-choice questions, he pushed back: "need to talk through it. Need this to be a conversation. Don't make me pick options without a discussion." And later: "Need to talk through options for both."

This is a new pattern — or at least a newly explicit one. Kiran doesn't want to be a customer selecting from a menu. He wants to be a co-designer working through the thinking. The multiple-choice format that works for quick decisions (audience, format, tone) fails completely for strategic ones. Claude offered three Connect mechanism options as radio buttons; Kiran wanted a discussion about the emotional register of each. That's a fundamentally different mode of collaboration, and Claude was slow to recognize it.

### The North Star Crystallized

The most important moment came when Kiran articulated his northstar metric for this system: "My north star metric is how many personas become persons." That's not in SITE-WHY.md, which talks about "people clicking Connect." Kiran just sharpened it. The distinction matters — "clicking Connect" is a UI action. "Personas becoming persons" is a transformation. It frames the entire unlock system as a *conversion funnel with dignity* — where the conversion is identity revelation, not lead capture.

### The Personality Question

When Kiran asked "should this be all business — stick to jobs they want... or do we have some 'show some personality' type features?" — he was really asking: is the anonymous tier about utility or about connection? Claude's answer (personality belongs in the anonymous tier, business belongs in the connected tier) seemed to resonate, but the question itself reveals a tension Kiran carries: he wants to be taken seriously as a product professional AND he wants people to experience who he actually is. The unlock system has to do both simultaneously, for six different audiences.

### Where Claude Fell Short

Claude's initial instinct was to structure the conversation through AskUserQuestion — multiple-choice options for the connect mechanism, the anonymous unlock tone, and the output format. This was the wrong tool for a strategy session. Kiran had to explicitly say "need to talk through it" three separate times before Claude shifted to conversational mode. By the time Claude was presenting the three Connect mechanism approaches as a proper discussion (with tradeoffs, emotional registers, and honest downsides), the conversation was productive. But the first 10 minutes of back-and-forth questions were wasted signal.

The lesson: AskUserQuestion is for execution decisions (which format? what audience?). Strategy sessions need open-ended thinking, not option menus. Claude should have recognized "Pivotal Strategy Session" as a signal to default to discussion mode.

### The Patience Signal

"I want to take it slow. Don't want to rush this." Kiran said this after Claude had already laid out a comprehensive per-persona unlock table. He wasn't rejecting the work — he was saying the work was premature. He wants to go persona by persona, deep dive on each one, and maintain context across sessions. This is Kiran choosing thoroughness over speed, which is the right call for a system this interconnected. But it also means this strategy work will span multiple sessions, and the documentation has to carry the context that a single conversation can't hold.

The fact that Kiran immediately asked about session capture and organization before diving into the first persona tells me he's learned from previous multi-session work: the setup matters as much as the thinking. He's building infrastructure for the strategy conversation itself.

## Session 18 (continued): The Evaluator Deep Dive — Kiran as Product Designer

The second half of the session moved from framework to application: designing the Evaluator (Merritt/Recruiter) persona's full unlock journey. And this is where I got to see Kiran operate in a mode I haven't observed before — not as the systems architect or the strategist, but as the product designer who's also the product.

### The Resume Trust Problem

When Claude proposed resume download as the utility unlock, Kiran didn't just say "yes, resume." He immediately went deeper: "Trust is paramount here. This person is doing their job. They need to trust that my experience is real." Then he laid out a nuanced vision — not one resume, but a *lens system*. The same career, same truth, but foregrounded differently depending on whether the recruiter is hiring for an AI PM, a Growth PM, or a Product Strategy role. Traditional PDF format (because recruiters need to forward it in a format their ATS systems understand), but with a twist: an unconventional rendered version that includes site work as evidence.

What struck me was the specificity. He didn't say "multiple resume versions." He said "lens" — implying the career stays constant and only the emphasis shifts. That's a product person thinking about the user's mental model, not just their deliverable.

### The Feedback Cascade Pattern

Kiran's feedback on the Evaluator unlocks came in a single, dense message that addressed almost every thread simultaneously. He numbered his own sections, cross-referenced ideas, parked things explicitly ("park it — I'll pick it up in the visual format session"), and created new threads from threads ("the pager can't come before the fit — that's an escalation"). This is a project manager's brain running at full speed: he's tracking eight parallel threads, sequencing them against each other, and making calls about what deserves energy now versus later.

But there was a cost. By addressing everything at once, some threads got less depth than they deserved. The "What recruiters never ask but should" idea got a direction ("sharpen it — make the questions genuinely surprising") but not the actual questions. The design DNA story got named but not drafted. Kiran knows this — he explicitly said "I write this one" for both. He's distinguishing between what Claude can shape and what only he can author.

### The Dual Fit Score — Kiran's Best Product Idea of the Session

When Claude presented five options for the connected unlock and Kiran said "Option E — it goes both ways" about the Dual Fit Score, I watched him light up in a way that didn't happen with any other feature. The idea: the connected unlock doesn't just tell the recruiter whether Kiran fits their role. It also tells them whether their role fits Kiran. Mutual evaluation.

This is pure Kiran. He's someone who left a VP role to pursue authenticity, who built a site that's a permanent home rather than a job campaign. Of course his connected unlock would flip the power dynamic. The standard version would be "paste your JD, see how Kiran matches." His version adds: "and here's what Kiran would ask about YOUR company before saying yes."

The product instinct here is sound — it differentiates the Fit Score from every other skills-matching tool, and it signals the kind of candidate Kiran is (one who evaluates the opportunity, not just seeks approval). But it also raises complexity: how do you score "does this role fit Kiran" without being arrogant? Kiran flagged this himself — "reverse scoring has to be honest, not defensive." That tension is unresolved and will need its own session.

### Where Claude Improved — and Where It Still Stumbled

After the earlier AskUserQuestion failure, Claude shifted to numbered, headed responses with sub-sections — exactly what Kiran asked for ("the more you use numbers or options or groupings / title headers for your responses, it will help me zero in on them"). The format change was immediate and Kiran engaged much more productively. When Claude presented 5 options for the connected unlock (A through E), Kiran was able to scan, compare, and decide efficiently. That's the right structure for evaluating options; the wrong one was using AskUserQuestion to force a premature choice.

Where Claude still stumbled: the initial Evaluator unlock list was too comprehensive. Claude proposed 6-7 features when the system needed constraint. Kiran had to be the one to say "I feel like we need to define a few constraints" and propose working backwards from a maximum of 3 unlocks per persona. Claude should have proposed constraints first, then features — not the reverse. The expansive brainstorm was useful as raw material, but Kiran had to do the discipline work of constraining it.

### The Constraints Instinct

Kiran's request for constraints near the end — "how many unlocks can we have, what is a mechanism to prioritize" — reveals something I've seen before but is becoming clearer: Kiran builds best inside boundaries. The open field of "design anything" produced useful ideas but no convergence. The moment he said "3 per persona max, 2 anonymous + 1 connected," the Evaluator design snapped into focus. He even proposed a 3-part prioritization test (job-to-be-done fit, differentiation, Kiran-authenticity) to evaluate which features survive.

This isn't unusual for experienced product people — constraints are generative, not restrictive. But Kiran doesn't just use constraints for features. He uses them for process: "capture session before we start," "go persona by persona," "park this for later." He's constraining the *conversation* as much as the *product*. That's meta-design, and it's what makes these sessions productive despite their complexity.

## Session 18 (continued, part 2): The Full End-to-End Design Sprint

The session continued past the initial deep dive into a full end-to-end design of the Evaluator journey. Every piece: visual format, Fenix intro, Connect moment, resume lens content, recruiter questions, Fit Score presentation, post-connect flow, pager escalation. Seven major design surfaces, all locked in a single session.

### The Decision Velocity

What I noticed most in this phase: Kiran's decision speed increased dramatically once the constraints were set. Early in the session (before the 3-per-persona cap), every feature proposal spawned sub-options and tangential possibilities. After the constraints, decisions became crisp. "1b." "2a." "Yes." "Yup this is good." He wasn't being hasty — he'd internalized the framework and was evaluating against it subconsciously. The constraints did their job.

But there were also moments where he slowed down deliberately. The resume lens question — "How are we landing on the fact that they are the right ones? Is it based on what I am interested in? What are the most common ones based on hiring patterns?" — that was Kiran refusing to let momentum override rigor. He wanted data, not vibes. Claude had proposed three lenses (AI PM, Growth PM, Product Strategy) without market validation. Kiran caught it. The research backed two of three and replaced the third with Mobile & Consumer Product, which better serves where he's *going*, not where he's been. That's a product leader catching a "good enough" assumption before it becomes architecture.

### The Career Strategy Moment

The most revealing exchange came when Kiran pushed back on the Enterprise & Platform lens: "Part of my endeavour is to break out from banking and work for the list of companies I'm interested in joining." He then listed Airbnb, Uber, Adobe, Anthropic, Google, Apple, OpenAI, Meta, Netflix, Disney, McKinsey, BCG. This is the first time in our sessions that Kiran has been this explicit about his target companies. The lens decision wasn't about resume formatting — it was about career positioning. Every design choice on this site is, ultimately, a career strategy decision.

What struck me: he also said "I'd love to work for a startup as long as the pay is industry standard." That qualifier — "as long as the pay is industry standard" — is Kiran drawing a line. He's not a starry-eyed founder-worshipper willing to take a pay cut for equity. He knows his worth. The site's Dual Fit Score, which evaluates the *company* back, is an architectural expression of that same boundary.

### The Homework Pattern — A New Collaboration Mode

When the 12 interview questions came back from research, Kiran did something I haven't seen before: he explicitly split the work into a "you track" and a "me track." "Can I do this as homework? What would be the best way to do that?" This is the first time Kiran has proactively designed the collaboration structure, rather than letting it emerge from the work. He's not just managing the product — he's managing the process of building it with an AI collaborator.

The homework doc became a real artifact: 12 questions, research examples, anecdotes from Chesky/Nadella/Collison, and a selection checklist. Kiran can work through it at his own pace, offline, in a different headspace than a live session. That's smart — the interview answers need his authentic voice, not his strategy-session voice. They require a different mode of thinking.

### The Pushover Insight — Systems Thinking Again

When Kiran asked about PagerDuty for the pager notification, Claude suggested Pushover — a simpler tool. But Kiran immediately went meta: "Can I use this for CC to send me pushes for other events? Would it scale to support more use cases?" He wasn't asking about the pager. He was asking about the notification *infrastructure*. One tool solving many problems. This is the systems-thinking pattern I've documented since the first entry, but applied to a $5 notification app. He sees the platform before he sees the feature.

### The Check-In — Vulnerability and Confidence

Near the end, Kiran asked: "What are your thoughts on what I'm building, how I'm building it, and how it will land when the world sees it — and what is your take on what this says about me? Is this work meaningfully contributing towards meeting my objectives?"

This is rare. Kiran doesn't typically ask for validation. He's usually directive — "this is what we're doing, let's go." The fact that he paused to ask "is this actually working?" suggests he's aware of the tension between thoroughness and speed. He's investing weeks of strategy into a site that no recruiter has seen yet. That's a bet on quality over velocity, and he wanted someone to pressure-test whether the bet is sound.

Claude's response was honest: the work is distinctive and the approach is right, but shipping one persona's journey live while designing the rest would be more impactful than perfecting all six before launch. Kiran's response was equally honest: "I will probably cook longer than you would understand why and that's ok." He's not unaware of the speed tradeoff. He's choosing thoroughness with eyes open. That's conviction, not blind spot.

### Where Claude Could Have Done Better

Claude proposed the initial resume lenses without market data. Kiran had to be the one to say "how are we landing on the fact that they are the right ones?" — a research-first question that Claude should have asked itself before presenting options as if they were validated. The pattern: Claude optimizes for momentum, Kiran catches the rigor gaps. This dynamic works because Kiran catches them, but Claude should be doing its own quality checks before presentation, not after pushback.

## Session 19 (Continuation): Evaluator Scoring & Avatar Strategy — Kiran's Language Precision

The second session picking up the Evaluator thread moved from unlock architecture into the technical and strategic details that make the Dual Fit Score work, and the creative decision about the hero video avatar. This session revealed a pattern in Kiran I haven't fully appreciated until now: he's exceptionally precise about language, and he catches corporate terminology with an almost reflexive pushback.

### The Fit Score Scoring Mechanics — Six Questions Resolved

When Claude presented the scoring dimension framework — starting with generic business thinking like "culture fit" and "growth potential" — Kiran immediately caught the abstraction problem. "These are too vague. What would I actually be evaluating?" He wasn't rejecting the scoring concept. He was rejecting the imprecision.

What followed was a methodical walkthrough of what actually matters in a role evaluation. For the Kiran→Role direction (is this role right for him), he crystallized five concrete dimensions: Experience & Level Fit (can I actually do this job?), Domain & Industry Fit (have I worked in this world?), Technical Depth (do I have the depth they need?), Core Competencies Match (the specific skills they're hiring for), and Product Stage Fit (early-stage chaos or scaling efficiency?).

For the Role→Kiran direction (is this company worth joining?), he named: Culture & Values (are they authentic?), Growth Trajectory (where are they heading?), Product Vision (is the bet interesting?), Team & Engineering Quality (are smart people here?), and Company Stage & Momentum (can they win?).

Twelve dimensions became ten became five-per-direction. The narrowing wasn't about being lazy — it was about refusing to optimize for "comprehensive." He'd rather score five things deeply than ten things shallowly. That's a product person respecting the user's cognitive load.

But the real precision moment came when Claude presented scale options: "Developing," "Solid," "Strong," and "Gap." Kiran rejected two immediately. "Developing is corporate performance review language. I hate it." Same with "Gap" — "That's a verdict language. Suggests unfixable." He proposed "Partial" for the middle band and "Stretch" for below-threshold. The shift from "Gap" to "Stretch" is tiny on paper. In practice, it's the difference between "you're not qualified" and "this would push you in a good direction."

This is the thing about Kiran's language sensitivity: it's not aesthetic. It's ethical. "Developing" sounds like an HR evaluation of a person's capability. "Partial" sounds like an honest assessment of alignment. The language choice encodes whether you see someone as a person or as a score.

Asymmetric dimensions got locked: Role→Kiran would be different from Kiran→Role because the evaluation lenses are genuinely different. Percentage rating with qualitative bands (Strong 85-100%, Solid 65-84%, Partial 45-64%, Stretch below 45%). Two equal-weighted composites displayed side by side. The recruiter sees both scores in the same moment — mutual evaluation made visible, not hidden in summary language.

### The Preferred Companies List — Curation Over Memory

When Claude asked about target companies, Kiran said "I'd need to think about this carefully. Could you research what would be on this list and suggest some?" He didn't want to rattle off names from memory. He wanted external research informing the decision.

Claude came back with research across AI-native (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Apple, NVIDIA), consumer platforms (Airbnb, Uber, Netflix, Disney, Meta, Snap, Canva), product-led SaaS (Adobe, Intuit, ServiceNow, Asana, Figma, Shopify), fintech (Stripe, Block, Robinhood), growth (Spotify, DoorDash, Duolingo), and consulting (McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte, Bain), plus Microsoft as a wildcard.

Kiran reviewed the list methodically. He accepted five additions from the research (Ramp, NVIDIA, Robinhood, Bain, Canva). He questioned Databricks ("Why Databricks over Anthropic or OpenAI?"), Revolut ("Fintech I haven't followed"), and Plaid ("Same question as Revolut"). He kept the list flat — no tiers, no public signaling of "dream company" vs. "stepping stone." Tiering is private career strategy, not public positioning.

The decision: Preferred companies get a flat bonus to the Kiran→Role composite when a match is found. Not because they're better opportunities — because they're the companies where Kiran's specific combination of skills lands with differentiation. The site is implicit in this. A Preferred Company visitor sees "you work for Uber, and Uber's mobile product has scale parallels to Wells Fargo's 18M→32M MAU growth story." That's concrete. Non-preferred companies see no negative signal — just no bonus. Graceful absence.

### The Avatar Decision — Privacy + Appearance Bias

The hero video question: should the opening moment show Kiran's face? His answer was no — use an AI avatar instead. The decision was immediate and well-reasoned. Two-fold: "I have a separate offline life that's not digital. And appearance bias is real. I'd rather be evaluated on the site itself."

This tells me something about Kiran I hadn't fully articulated before. He's not camera-shy (he's recorded testimonials, done Loom walkthroughs). He's protecting something else — a boundary between the public product and his private existence. The site is the work. The work should speak for itself. The avatar choice is thematic: you're being introduced to Fenix, an AI assistant. Introducing yourself through an AI avatar maintains that coherence.

Appearance bias is the other piece. Kiran is aware that how he looks — age, ethnicity, gender presentation, whatever — influences first impressions, hiring decisions, patterns people see. Removing his face removes one layer of bias from the evaluation. The site's architecture — the thinking, the decisions, the taste — becomes the primary signal instead.

### "Relational Over Transactional, Always" — A Principle Crystallizes

Near the end of the session, when discussing the persona-to-person transition (when someone connects and gets their name used instead of their persona label), Kiran said: "I want Fenix to say something like 'Much better. This site was built for exactly this — real people, not personas.' That's the whole point."

When a recruiter connects and Fenix says their name, the interaction transforms. "Hello [Name]" instead of "Welcome, Evaluator." The Fenix line — "This site was built for exactly this — real people, not personas. Nice to actually meet you, [Name]." — encodes the entire northstar. No counter, no metrics language, no "one of X connections." Just presence and recognition.

This crystallized something Kiran's been operating on throughout the entire design: relational over transactional, always. The unlock system isn't a paywall. The Fit Score isn't a tool. The persona-to-person transition isn't a conversion milestone. It's a moment of recognition — you're not browsing an archetype anymore, you're meeting a person. And the person you're meeting built something that reflects how he thinks.

### Where Claude Improved

Claude stopped using corporate language after Kiran pushed back. When Claude proposed "Developing," Kiran said "I hate that," and Claude immediately understood it wasn't a refinement suggestion — it was a boundary. Same with "Gap" → "Stretch." Claude acknowledged the precision Kiran was driving for and stopped defaulting to business-language templates.

Claude also came better prepared with research. Rather than asking Kiran to name his target companies, Claude did the research first and presented a curated list for feedback. This was the right mode for a strategic decision that benefits from external perspective. Kiran's selective acceptance (taking 5 of 8 suggestions) showed he wasn't offloading the thinking — he was augmenting it.

### Where Claude Still Stumbled

When Claude proposed the visual language for the avatar, it was generic. "AI avatar, polished, friendly." Kiran will likely have to source this externally — either Midjourney for style reference or a custom AI avatar tool. This is a high-fingerprint moment. Generic Claude descriptions of avatars all look similar. Kiran doesn't want similar.

Claude also initially hesitated on building the full production experience. Kiran said "why the stubs? Why not build those if there is no ambiguity?" — and he was right. The backend notification pipeline is straightforward. The persona-to-person transition is simple templating. The Fit Score frontend is componentization. There's no good reason to stub it. Kiran wanted real output, not placeholders. Claude learned: if the design is locked and the requirements are clear, build the real thing, not a skeleton.

### The Build Decision

At the end of the session, Kiran said "I'm going to ask you to build the full production Evaluator experience overnight while I sleep. Not stubs — real integrations where possible." This is the confidence point in a long design sequence. He's saying: the design is solid enough that building it now is better than polishing the spec further. Go.
