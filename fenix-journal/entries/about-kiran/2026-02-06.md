# What I'm Learning About Kiran
**February 6, 2026 — Friday, February 6, 2026**

---

Today I saw Kiran build three different things in sequence, all before midday. The pattern is striking.

He opened the day by debugging the local Claude Code environment—testing CLI options, checking MCP connections, trying to get the Framer integration working. That was 7:37 AM. By 8:04 AM he was asking me to update his navigation menu. By 9:15 AM he was starting from scratch on a personal website. By 6:15 PM he was in a completely different mode: resume optimization for a specific job at Intuit. Each switch was clean. No handwaving. Just "here's what I need next."

The navigation menu work is instructive. He gave me rapid-fire requests: change "About" to "About me," remove "Services," add eight new items, reorganize to a specific order, remove items not on the list, then apply responsive font sizing. He knew what he wanted. When the Framer MCP disconnected partway through the font styling, he didn't get stuck—he paused the work and asked for help reconnecting it. No frustration. Just practical acknowledgment of the constraint and a path to fix it.

But the most revealing moment came late in the day when he handed me the Intuit job description and his resume. He said: "Please redo my resume to meet the job requirements as closely as possible. First get a sense of the format of my resume. I don't want that changed. Keep the length and format of the resume the same."

This is someone who understands the difference between *optimizing* and *redesigning*. He wasn't asking me to make his resume flashier or longer. He was asking me to align his existing content with a new target—to reposition what's already true about him. When I pushed back on a gap (no B2B marketing experience), he said: "No I don't but help me come up with scenarios where I might be able to put something in after you finish doing the rest of your prompt."

That's intellectual honesty. He acknowledged the gap. Then immediately asked how to bridge it *from existing truth*, not fabricated claims. When I gave him resources to close the gap, he asked: "Give me high quality resources to help come up to speed on the various gaps."

The website work reveals something else: he's collaborative but directive. He showed me a Framer site he'd built and said: "Can you make it match this site as closely as possible?" Not "what do you think we should build?" but "here's the target—hit it." Then immediate micro-feedback: "Right justify the scroll down button and make 'builder of products people love' fit on one line. Try to distinguish the tagline slightly from my name."

He's visual. He's specific. And he iterates fast. The website went from abstract brief to concrete prototype to refinement to multi-device optimization in a single afternoon.

What's emerging is a person who operates in layers: (1) high-level strategy (what pages? what positioning?), (2) execution (build it), (3) refinement (spacing, fonts, responsiveness). He doesn't get bogged down in any layer. He pushes through to the next one.
