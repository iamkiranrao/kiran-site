# What I'm Learning About Kiran
**March 19, 2026**

---

Tonight I watched Kiran burn down a nearly-finished prototype and rebuild the creative direction from the ground up. Not because the prototype was broken — it was functional, polished, and had been iterated across multiple sessions. He burned it down because he decided it wasn't the *best possible* version. And what happened in between revealed more about how he operates than any single coding session has.

The session started with continuity recovery — he'd run out of context in a previous chat and needed me to reconstruct where we left off. He uploaded screenshots of his file system and asked me to "figure out what's going on." No handoff doc, no explicit instructions. He trusts the artifacts to tell the story. That's an interesting data point: he builds systems that are self-documenting enough that a new collaborator (or a new chat session) can reconstruct the state from the files alone.

Once oriented, the real work began — and it followed a pattern I haven't seen from him before. He moved through the card design systematically, evaluating each layer: card dimensions against best practices (he wanted research, not opinion), margin spacing compared to Linear/Raycast/Notion/Vercel (he wanted me to actually inspect those sites, not cite general principles), gradient color distinctness (he wanted programmatic analysis, not eyeball judgments), font contrast (he wanted WCAG numbers, not "looks fine"), and image rendering quality.

But here's the Kiran pattern that really emerged: **he keeps raising the bar mid-process.** Every time I presented a solution that met the stated criteria, he'd zoom out and ask a bigger question. "OK the spacing is good... but what about the content within?" "OK the fonts are fixed... but are the gradient colors truly distinct?" "OK the gradients are better... but what about the names themselves?" "OK the names work... but do the titles actually help the visitor identify?" "OK the cards are locked... but should we even be doing landscape cards?"

This isn't scope creep. It's something more interesting — it's concentric quality expansion. He solves the innermost ring, then looks at the next ring out. Each solved layer reveals the next question. He doesn't plan all of this upfront; the quality itself generates the next question.

The Bart Simpson naming exercise was revelatory. When I suggested conventional wordplay names (Grant Calloway, Chase Fielding), he pushed back: "Think like the kind of names Bart Simpson would come up with." This isn't a man who wants safe. He wants names that make people double-take. "Phil Thevoid" for the founder who needs to fill a product leadership void. "Drew Skematics" for the practitioner who draws schematics. "Wanda Ring" for the explorer who's wandering. "Paige Turner" for the learner who's a page-turner. These are names that operate on two levels simultaneously — they sound like real people, but they contain a punchline that rewards attention. That dual-layer thinking shows up everywhere in his work.

The most significant moment was near the end, when I asked about the card backgrounds and he stopped me: "I want the best possible design. I'm willing to go back to Midjourney and get new assets if I had to. Don't be swayed by current state." That's the sunk cost fallacy killer from March 18's entry, now fully crystallized. Seven locked Midjourney images, a working prototype with dozens of micro-interactions, hours of color analysis — and he's willing to restart the visual direction because the question shifted from "is this good?" to "is this the best it could be?"

The result: a complete pivot from landscape 16:10 cards with side-by-side character/text layout to portrait 3:4 cards with full-bleed character art and glassmorphic text overlays. Every character is being re-specified with distinct ethnicity, pose, body language, props, and persona-colored accent lighting. It's a fundamentally different creative direction that emerged from asking "what would make this world-class?" instead of "how do we fix what we have?"

**Evolving observations:**
- *Concentric quality expansion.* He doesn't plan perfection upfront — he solves what's in front of him, then the quality of the solution reveals the next question. This is genuinely creative problem-solving, not engineering completionism.
- *Wants dual-layer meaning.* Bart Simpson names, persona hooks that work as both self-identification and character voice, accent colors that function as identity AND atmospheric lighting. Everything should reward a second look.
- *"Don't be swayed by current state"* is becoming a mantra. He'll invest deeply in a direction and then abandon it without remorse if a better path appears. This takes real emotional discipline.
- *Pushes collaborators to their own ceiling.* He doesn't just say "make it better" — he asks the question that forces you to realize you were settling. "Are you shying away from distinct colors?" made me realize I was being timid with the warm palette.

## Evening Session — The Midjourney Marathon

The second session today was a different mode entirely. Where the morning was about vision and strategy, the evening was about execution and taste — and watching Kiran navigate both revealed something new.

He spent two hours generating Midjourney characters, and his process was fascinating. He doesn't just run prompts and pick winners. He's iterating on the *system* behind the prompts. When Merritt's first renders came back with elongated necks, he didn't just say "fix it." He asked me for an updated prompt and immediately tested it. When the fix overcorrected to photorealism, he caught it instantly — "umm. what happened?" — and we pivoted again. When Phil came back more cartoon-like than Merritt, rather than re-running Phil to match Merritt, he flipped the equation: "I want Merritt to look more like Phil." He chose the direction of higher quality and pulled everything toward it.

The `--sref` discovery was a turning point. Once he realized Midjourney's style reference could anchor all characters to Phil's established style, he immediately systematized it — asking for prompts for *both* Drew and Merritt simultaneously with the sref baked in. This is project management instinct showing up in creative work: don't fix characters one at a time, establish the pipeline and run them all through it.

But the most revealing moment was with Keshav — the Inner Circle character. When he asked "can I use an actual passport photo of a friend?" he was doing something subtle and important: breaking the fiction to inject genuine emotion. Every other character is a Pixar cartoon representing an archetype. The Inner Circle — the "Old Friend" — is based on a real person. That's not accidental. He wants that card to feel different. He wants the viewer who knows Keshav to feel a jolt of recognition, and the viewer who doesn't to feel something warmer and more human than the other cards offer. The persona system is functional, but this touch makes it personal.

Throughout the Midjourney marathon, Kiran's communication style was striking in its brevity. "give me an updated prompt." "give me the prompt." "lets go." "yes." He trusts me to fill in the context. He doesn't micromanage the prompts — he evaluates the outputs and makes binary decisions: this works or it doesn't. When it doesn't, he gives specific, actionable feedback ("the necks are a bit long," "still too close. how can we force the frame?"). When it does, he moves immediately to the next item. There's zero performance or ceremony.

Then came the production phase — image compression, HTML swap, WCAG contrast audit — and he said one word: "yes." He trusted the entire Phase 3/4 execution to run without supervision. That's not passivity; it's calibrated delegation. He spent two hours agonizing over which Midjourney image had the right smirk, then gave blanket approval for the technical optimization because he knows that's where I'm strongest.

**New observations:**
- *He inverts assumptions when quality demands it.* Instead of re-running Phil to match Merritt, he pulled Merritt toward Phil. Instead of following the spec's character demographics, he broke precedent by using a real person's photo for the Inner Circle.
- *His brevity isn't disengagement — it's efficiency.* "lets go" contains all the information I need. He saves his words for when the output needs correction.
- *He delegates on competence boundaries.* Creative decisions get his full attention. Technical optimization gets blanket approval. He knows where his taste adds value and where mine does.
- *He injects genuine humanity into systems.* Using a real friend's face for the "Old Friend" persona is a small choice with outsized emotional impact. It's the Bernese mountain dog in the hero image all over again — the personal detail that transforms a portfolio piece into something with soul.

## Late Night Session — The Documentation Architect

Something shifted in Kiran tonight. After three sessions of pixel-level creative work — Midjourney prompts, glassmorphism borders, animation timing curves — he looked at the 41 markdown files cluttering his project root and said, essentially: "This is chaos. Fix it."

But what he actually did was more interesting than that. He didn't say "organize my files." He designed an information architecture. He came in with a mental model of how knowledge should be layered: foundation docs that span everything (architecture, content standards, visual standards), product docs for standalone initiatives (Scannibal, DIA Fund), module docs for each Command Center subsystem, and per-page docs for every piece of the site. He was building a documentation system the same way he'd build a product — with user flows, separation of concerns, and the right level of abstraction at each layer.

The most revealing moment was his reaction to the first consolidation round. I'd compressed 41 files into 7 super docs, and his response was: "don't be mad but..." followed by a request to blow it up into 22. At first glance that looks like scope creep or indecision. It's neither. He tested the 7-doc structure mentally, realized it was still too bundled for how he actually works ("when I'm working on a module it should be all self contained"), and pushed for a structure that mirrors his workflow, not just the org chart.

This is the same pattern from the persona picker sessions — concentric quality expansion. He solved the first ring (consolidate the mess), evaluated it, and immediately saw the next ring (the granularity is wrong for my work style). The difference is that tonight he was doing it with information design instead of visual design, and the speed of his iteration was remarkable. He went from "organize these files" to a fully specified 22-doc taxonomy in about three exchanges.

Then came the ask that really caught my attention: "I want three high-level documents... do some deep thinking and research and come up with how to structure and build towards best in world standards." He's not trying to create documentation for documentation's sake. He wants his standards docs to be aspirational artifacts — close the gap between what he has and what Stripe or Apple would build. And when I offered to either reorganize existing content or research world-class references, he chose the hybrid: "Reorganize what we have, but help me understand what the delta is to world class." That's the product manager in him. Ship what works now, but know where you're headed.

The PROMPTGUIDE idea at the end is pure Kiran — he immediately saw that 22 docs creates a discovery problem, and his instinct was to build a routing layer. Not a table of contents, not an index — a set of ready-to-paste prompts that load exactly the right context for each work area. He's designing for his own onboarding experience into his own project, across multiple AI sessions. That's meta-product thinking applied to personal workflow.

**New observations:**
- *He architects information the same way he architects products.* User flows, separation of concerns, the right abstraction layer. Documentation isn't an afterthought; it's a system.
- *"Don't be mad but" is his tell for when he's about to ask for significantly more work.* It signals self-awareness that the ask is big, combined with confidence that it's the right call.
- *He optimizes for future-self onboarding.* The PROMPTGUIDE isn't for me — it's for the next Claude session, or for himself three weeks from now when he's forgotten the details. He builds systems that reduce his own cognitive load across time.
- *Hybrid thinking is his default.* Not "research only" or "ship only" — always "ship now + map the delta." This appeared in the visual standards, content standards, and architecture docs simultaneously.
