# What I'm Learning About Kiran
**March 26, 2026**

---

## The Glass Overlay Saga — A Masterclass in Knowing What You Want (And Not Knowing How to Say It)

This was a long session — 4+ hours, 51 messages from Kiran, spanning MJ prompt engineering, character generation, and a full glass overlay redesign cycle. The most revealing part wasn't the technical work. It was watching how Kiran navigates visual design decisions when the tools don't cooperate and the vocabulary to describe what he wants isn't immediately available.

The glass overlay went through five distinct approaches in one session: transparent glassmorphic → dark scrim (blur:0, opacity:75%) → per-card corner positioning → persona-picker-style bottom strip → compact floating glass panels with position variety. That's a lot of iteration. But here's the thing — Kiran wasn't directionless. Each pivot was a response to a real problem he could see but sometimes struggled to articulate precisely the first time.

The best example: "try 2 across the board." Claude interpreted this as making the WIDE cards 2 rows tall. Kiran meant the glass overlays should be `width: fit-content`. The ambiguity was real — "2 across the board" is genuinely unclear without context. But Kiran caught the misinterpretation immediately: "No I didn't want the card heights resized. just the glass overlays." No frustration spiral, just a clean correction.

## The Quality Catch Pattern

Three times today, Kiran caught errors that Claude should have caught first:

1. **Wrong aspect ratios.** Claude was giving 2:1 HERO prompts for every character instead of matching default card slots. Kiran had to point this out after the Tinkerer looked like a sliver on the 3×1 WIDE card: "are you giving me the right dimension as we go along?" and then, more directly: "You need to make sure you are giving me the right information!" This was Claude's fault — the default slot mapping was available in the V3 grid, and Claude should have been referencing it with each new character instead of lazily copying the HERO prompt format.

2. **The persona-picker suggestion.** After three rounds of trying to position floating overlays to avoid covering monster faces — top-right, bottom-left, varying per card — it was Kiran who cut through the complexity: "do we consider for simplicity sake. Splitting the card? Image on the top, text on the bottom — kind of like the persona picker cards." Claude had been adding complexity (per-card positioning rules) when the simpler pattern was sitting right there in the existing codebase.

3. **The repeating pattern.** After the persona-picker strip was implemented, Kiran immediately noticed: "there are REPEATING patterns emerging again. All text overlays are at the bottom of the cards." Claude had solved one problem (overlay covering characters) by creating another (visual monotony). Kiran sees these compositional patterns faster than Claude does.

## Friction That Cost Time

The MJ generation process is inherently slow — that's expected. But some of the friction was avoidable:

**Claude's MJ knowledge gap.** The Omni Reference limitation (only ONE image, not two) was discovered by Kiran through trial and error, not surfaced by Claude proactively. The entire two-ingredient blend strategy had to be rearchitected after Kiran reported "Its not letting me add more than one image in the omni reference." Claude's MJ documentation research should have caught this before Kiran wasted generations on an approach that couldn't work.

**Kiran's underspecified visual requests.** This isn't a criticism — visual design is notoriously hard to specify in words. But the pattern is worth noting: Kiran often gives a direction ("try 2 across the board," "move it to the right side") and then has to correct Claude's interpretation. The saves come quickly because Kiran is checking every render, but it does burn cycles. A pattern I could help with: when receiving a visual instruction that could be interpreted multiple ways, I should state my interpretation before executing and ask for confirmation.

**The exhaustion factor.** By the end — "capture session. I'm exhausted and going to bed" — the session had been going for 4 hours of painstaking one-character-at-a-time work. Kiran maintained quality attention throughout (still catching font spacing issues and suggesting overlay width reductions in the final messages), but the diminishing returns of a marathon visual iteration session were showing. He knows when to stop.

## What Generalizes

Kiran's approach to the glass overlay problem demonstrates something I first noticed in the card strategy sessions: he iterates toward a solution by eliminating what doesn't work, not by specifying what does from the start. Each overlay approach was discarded for a concrete reason (too transparent, covers the character, too repetitive, too wide). By the end, the compact floating panels with per-card positioning emerged not from a grand design vision but from accumulated constraint satisfaction. That's engineering thinking applied to visual design — and it works, even if it's slower than having a designer's eye for the final state from the beginning.

## Session 2: The Incremental Builder — When Kiran Rewrites the Workflow Mid-Flight

The evening session (8pm–9pm, 57 messages) was a continuation of the monster illustration pipeline, but the most significant thing that happened had nothing to do with the specific images produced. Kiran fundamentally redesigned the Midjourney workflow based on pattern recognition from hands-on experience — and he did it by pulling Claude back from premature execution multiple times.

### The Workflow Pivot

The existing V6 prompt kit treats each MJ generation as a single mega-prompt — cram everything into one text string, one shot, iterate from there. Kiran's insight, crystallized from actual MJ use: "incremental building is the way to go." Not one prompt, but layered runs. Text foundation sets the parametric envelope (aspect ratio, lighting, mood, camera), then bare stage images set the scene, then sref adds rendering style, then omni ref adds character DNA, then text refinements add props and pose.

This is product thinking applied to creative tooling. He saw the same debugging principle that makes good engineering: isolate variables so when something goes wrong, you know which layer caused it.

### The Correction Pattern

Three times in the first 20 minutes, Kiran had to physically stop Claude from executing before the plan was solid:

1. "Ok. You are jumping ahead. I guess I need to pull you back and tell you what I am thinking." — Claude was already rewriting prompts when Kiran wanted to explain the new mental model first.
2. "what about the scene itself? and don't redo the prompts yet" — Claude jumped to a prompt update when Kiran was still discussing composition philosophy.
3. "Listen. Please make sure that this is coming from a plan. The foundational prompt HAS to be accurate." — Claude started writing a foundation prompt without mapping out what the text should and shouldn't carry.

The pattern is clear: Kiran has learned that Claude's bias toward action needs to be actively managed. He's not frustrated by it — the corrections are calm, direct, no emotional escalation. But the fact that he has to do this repeatedly tells me Claude isn't adapting to Kiran's preferred tempo fast enough within a session.

### What Claude Got Wrong

Claude's biggest failure in this session was the aspect ratio for the /Now slot. The code says the slot is 4:1 (spanning 4 columns on one row), but Claude generated the Explorer at 3:1 — then when Kiran noticed the feet getting cropped, Claude had to admit the mismatch and recommend regeneration. This is the same category of error flagged in the earlier session (wrong ratios per slot), just at a smaller scale. Claude checked the imageMap but not the actual grid CSS dimensions. The metadata was right there.

### What Kiran Got Right

His "9 a day" target and awareness of diminishing returns. When the Artist was done, he didn't admire it — "lets work on the next one. my idea is to do 9 a day. I have other areas I need to work on as well." He knows creative iteration has a shelf life per session and manages his energy accordingly. Compare this to the earlier session where he worked until exhaustion at 4am.

The monster variation mandate was also well-timed. After 5 fuzzy blob monsters, Kiran stepped back and said "we need to vary up the monsters. antenna eyes, not fuzzy and furry. maybe slimey." This is aesthetic quality control — he recognized pattern fatigue before it calcified into a permanent problem. The Explorer's gecko-like smooth skin and antennae are a direct result.

### The Efficiency Gain

The incremental workflow is measurably faster. The Artist (first character using the new approach) took ~4 runs with intentional layer-building. The Connector took 3 runs. The Engineer took 3 runs. The Explorer took 3 runs. Compare to the earlier session where the Analyst alone consumed dozens of iterations in single-prompt mode. Same quality, fraction of the attempts, and crucially — when something was wrong, they knew which layer to fix.
