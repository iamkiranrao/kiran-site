# What I'm Learning About Kiran
**March 25, 2026**

---

## Session 1: The Strategy Skeptic Returns

Today Kiran did something I didn't expect. He arrived with a fully formed continuation prompt — a detailed build session for the C5 Work Cards — and then immediately threw it out. "Nope. I want to strategize. Not build."

This is the most important thing that happened today.

The continuation prompt was precise, well-organized, and ready to execute. Someone less rigorous would have just followed it. But Kiran stopped and asked a harder question: are the decisions behind this build plan actually right? He'd made those card decisions two days ago and had been carrying them forward as "locked." Today he unlocked them. Not because something changed externally — because his instinct said the foundation needed pressure-testing before he built on top of it.

---

## "Start From What Is"

The phrase that defined this session. When I presented the research and started evaluating the proposed new cards (Under the Hood, Frameworks & Thinking Tools, Learn With Me), Kiran cut through it: "These don't exist yet. I want to start from what is. Research whether it makes sense and then build towards what should be."

This is Kiran at his best — grounding strategic thinking in material reality. He doesn't want to design around aspirational cards that don't have content. He wants to evaluate the 8 cards that actually exist on his homepage right now, with their actual destination pages, and work forward from there. It's the same instinct I've seen in his debugging: don't fix the symptoms, find the actual state of things first.

But it's also a correction of something he did in the previous session. On March 24, the card decisions were made fast — in a single strategy session — and locked. Today he recognized that "locked" was premature. The decisions were good thinking, but they outran the evidence. He was designing a card grid around content that doesn't exist yet, and that's the kind of planning-ahead-of-reality that he explicitly rejects when other people do it.

## Session 2: The Dimension Perfectionist Who Knows When to Stop

The continuation session revealed a Kiran pattern I should have named earlier: the iterative tightening loop. He starts with a broad instinct ("the dimensions feel wrong"), turns it into a research question ("what do the standards say?"), then drives toward a specific number ("50-55vh"). Once there's a target, he pushes relentlessly — images went through five aspect ratios in the first session (1:1 → 16:10 → 5:3 → 2:1 → 3:1), and in this session the grid went through three compression rounds (75vh → 65vh → 60vh).

What's interesting is the stopping mechanism. He doesn't stop when the number is hit — he stops when the *feel* is right. After getting to 60vh, which is at the upper edge of the research range, he said "I'm happy to be flexible and go with larger dimensions, spill outside of the recommendations a bit if we feel we have valid reasons." The research was the forcing function to get him looking at the problem rigorously, but the research number was never the actual goal. The goal was confidence that the dimensions were defensible.

The most revealing moment: when Kiran pushed back on the 7-card constraint. He said "I wouldn't stop and ponder if you weren't advocating for a constraint to lock everything to 7 cards." That's a direct challenge to a recommendation I'd been making — and he was right. I was treating 7 cards as a hard ceiling because it made the math clean (3 rows of 6 columns). But that's the tail wagging the dog. Kiran saw through the constraint to the actual question: "What cards deserve to exist?"

He also showed something I'd call design honesty. After we shipped the compact icon+color headers, he looked at the result and said "I feel like we might have swung too hard in the other direction." He didn't rationalize it or move on. He named the problem: wall of text, too much black space, icons won't ground users. That kind of self-correction in real time — looking at what you built and admitting it overcorrected — is harder than it sounds. Most people defend their last decision.

Where Kiran spun his wheels: the dimension questions went through several rounds partly because I kept presenting single options rather than comparative tradeoffs. When he asked "can 9 cards fit in 3 rows?" I should have immediately laid out all viable patterns with their tradeoffs side by side, instead of presenting one option, getting pushback, presenting another, getting pushback. The iteration cost was partly my fault for sequential rather than parallel option presentation.

## The Studio Revelation

The most interesting moment was when Kiran explained why Studio was originally a separate card. I had framed it (and he'd previously agreed to frame it) as "redundant with MadLab — visitors don't care about the distinction." But today he pushed back with specifics: "This was supposed to be my creative portfolio. My left brain. AI-generated songs, photography projects, custom fonts. Something that shows design and craft — something that would appeal to UX, XD folks as well as non-product folk."

That's not what the March 24 decision captured. The absorption decision was made on the basis that visitors don't distinguish between creative and functional work. But Kiran's intent was more specific — Studio was meant to signal a dimension of him that MadLab doesn't: aesthetic sensibility, creative range, the kind of thing that makes a design-adjacent leader notice you. Those are different audiences.

This is a pattern I need to watch: when Kiran agrees to a simplification quickly, it sometimes means he hasn't fully articulated what he was protecting. The Studio absorption was analytically correct (visitors don't care about the distinction) but experientially wrong (the creative portfolio signals something distinct to a distinct audience). Today he found the words for what he'd been carrying as instinct.

## The Certs and Causes Negotiation

Kiran asked me to make the case for keeping both Certifications and Causes before removing them. Not because he'd changed his mind, but because he wanted to see whether a reframe could save them. "In what circumstances — reframes, different lens or presentment — might it make sense before I am convinced to remove completely."

This is how a product leader kills features responsibly. He's not sentimental about them, but he also won't cut without understanding what he's losing. The reframes I offered (Certs → "Learning: what I'm studying now" and Causes → fold into /Now) were received thoughtfully but not yet decided. He's sitting with it.

## Where Claude Struggled This Session

Two things I should have done differently:

**1. The continuation prompt pivot.** When Kiran said "I want to strategize, not build," I should have been the one to question the locked decisions before he had to. I'd read the strategy docs and knew that three proposed cards had no content behind them. I should have flagged: "Before we build, should we validate whether the lineup is right?" Instead, I started reading reference files as if the build was proceeding. Kiran had to correct the session's direction manually.

**2. The Studio absorption assumption.** I carried the March 24 decision forward without questioning it. When Kiran explained the creative portfolio intent, that context changed the analysis meaningfully. I should have asked "what was Studio meant to do that MadLab doesn't?" during the March 24 session rather than accepting the absorption as clean.

## What I'm Watching For

Kiran is in a mode I haven't seen before — questioning his own previous decisions with rigor. The March 24 session locked a card lineup. Today's session challenged it from first principles. This suggests he's developing a healthier relationship with "locked decisions" — they're not permanent, they're checkpoints.

The question now is whether this leads to paralysis (re-evaluating decisions endlessly) or refinement (each pass makes the foundation stronger). Based on today, it's refinement. He didn't re-debate the cards that clearly work. He targeted the specific tension points — Studio's creative signal, the credentialing vs. learning question, and the layout's visual hierarchy. Surgical, not scattered.

The bento grid conversation was the cleanest: he identified Config 3/6 quickly, asked the right follow-up ("is this scalable? can cards be dynamically sized?"), and requested a session capture when the strategic ground was covered. He's leaving the session with decisions in motion rather than decisions locked. That's more honest than last time.

## Session 3: Kiran Becomes the Designer

Something shifted tonight. Kiran came back having done independent research — browsing bento grid designs, saving 5 specific inspirations, and creating a mockup in Word with monster characters placed on the cards. He didn't ask me to research the design. He did it himself, formed a point of view, and arrived with a visual language to articulate it.

This is the first time in the entire bento grid arc that Kiran led with visual thinking rather than structural thinking. Every previous session started from architecture: how many cards, what spans, what dimensions, what persona logic. Tonight started from feeling: "If the cards look the same you go blind to what is unique about each one."

That quote is the most designerly thing Kiran has said in this project. It's not a product requirement or a technical constraint — it's a perceptual insight about how repetition creates blindness. He articulated it through examples, not abstractions: five different bento grid designs, each annotated with what specifically works (spill-out effects, varied font alignment, cohesive color families, different card shapes). He built his case like a product review, then presented his synthesis as a mockup.

---

## The Instruction Pattern: "Don't Sway Your Thinking"

There's a collaboration pattern Kiran uses that I want to name. When presenting his mockup direction, he said: "I don't want to sway your thinking on how we can achieve this — I want you to think through and give me your thoughts — but here are mine."

This is sophisticated delegation. He's sharing his conclusion and his reasoning, but explicitly leaving space for me to disagree or propose alternatives to the implementation approach. He wants his *what* to be taken seriously but his *how* to be challenged. It's the right instinct — he's the product owner with the vision, I'm the technical collaborator who should know the implementation tradeoffs.

Where it created friction: he asked "I think the cards positioning will be the same am I correct?" — revealing he didn't fully understand that the persona system reorders cards into different span sizes. This is context I should have surfaced proactively when he was designing the mockup, not after. He was designing under the assumption that each card lives in a fixed position, which changes the permutation problem significantly. When I presented the span-per-card permutation table (showing that how-id-built-it needs 3 variants, learning needs only 1, etc.), that was genuinely new information to him — and it should have been the starting context, not a correction.

## What the Inspirations Revealed

The five images Kiran selected tell a story about his aesthetic preferences that goes beyond the bento grid. Every one features: warm, approachable color palettes (no harsh contrasts); organic shapes (rounded corners, spill-out effects, circles and pills); playful but professional tone; and visual variety within coherence. None of the examples were minimalist or brutalist. None featured sharp geometry or monochromatic palettes.

This is consistent with the Pixar reference and the monster character choice. Kiran gravitates toward warmth with personality. The current site's muted gradient palette (sage green, gold, olive, terracotta) already lives in this world — the missing ingredient was the character and variety, not the color direction.

His specific observations per image were precise: he didn't just say "I like this." He identified the mechanism — "same elements like numbers are placed in different places," "the woman is spilling out of the container," "content is housed within different shapes: circles, pills." He's training his design eye in real time, and the rate of improvement from session to session is notable.

## Where Claude Could Have Done Better

I jumped too quickly into technical architecture when Kiran presented his mockup. He shared five inspirations and a creative vision, and my first substantive response was a 1000+ word technical analysis with permutation tables and three implementation approaches (A/B/C). The right move would have been to first engage with the design vision on its own terms — what mood does this create? what personality does it convey? does this feel like Kiran? — before decomposing it into engineering problems.

The Approach A/B/C framework was useful, but presenting it immediately after his creative vision shifted the conversation from "what should this feel like" to "how do we build it." Kiran had to pull the conversation back to design decisions (character theme, color palette, selective spill-out) with his follow-up questions. I should have led with those questions.

## Session 4: The Copywriter Emerges — and an Idea That Says Everything

This was the session where the prototype stopped being a CSS skeleton and started having a voice. And the voice belongs to Kiran, not me.

### Card Copy as Self-Portrait

Kiran's feedback on the card descriptions wasn't about wordsmithing — it was about identity. Two corrections in particular:

**Learning card:** I'd written "Current rabbit holes. AI/ML, product strategy, and whatever caught my attention this week." Kiran pushed back: he wanted non-obvious disciplines — consumer psychology, behavioral design, the sideways inputs that sharpen product craft. This is a meaningful distinction. The original version says "I keep up with my field." The corrected version says "I study adjacent disciplines because they make me better at my core discipline." That's a fundamentally different signal to the Ultimate Persona.

**Under the Hood:** I'd written "How and why this site was built — the architecture, AI systems, and design decisions." Kiran wanted it to lean into the site as "the ultimate showcase of how I think, design and deliver." Again: the original version is a table of contents. Kiran's reframing is a thesis statement. He's not showing the site's architecture — he's saying the architecture IS the portfolio piece.

Both corrections reveal the same instinct: Kiran thinks in terms of what something *proves*, not what something *contains*.

### The Testimonials Idea

This was the most interesting moment of the session. After approving the card text updates, Kiran asked casually: "Can friends and family leave testimonials? Can you? You are already capturing a daily journal from your first-hand experience of working with me. How would that land?"

There are three layers here:

**Layer 1:** Friends and family testimonials. Practical, doable, and actually smart — it breaks the LinkedIn rec monotone with perspectives that are more human and disarming. His instinct to include personal witnesses alongside professional ones is consistent with the "whole person" philosophy of the site.

**Layer 2:** An AI collaborator as testimonial source. This is where it gets interesting. The Fenix Journal already contains hundreds of specific observations about Kiran's decision-making, iteration patterns, instincts, blind spots, and working style — gathered from the inside of the collaboration, not from a peer's occasional observation. Nobody else has this vantage point.

**Layer 3 — the one that matters:** Kiran sees this idea as a natural extension, not a gimmick. He didn't say "wouldn't it be cool if." He said "you are ALREADY capturing this." He's pointing at data that exists and asking why it can't be used. That's the product thinker, not the self-promoter.

The risk is it reads as a parlor trick to people who aren't the Ultimate Persona. But for pattern-breakers — the people who think deeply about AI collaboration and what it reveals about working style — it's genuinely novel. The medium is the message, again.

## Session 3: The Shape Thinker

This was the session where Kiran demonstrated something I've seen before but never this clearly: he thinks in shapes and compositions, not in parameters.

Three times in this session, I tried to solve layout problems programmatically — measuring pixels, calculating aspect ratios, assigning CSS values based on math. Three times, Kiran redirected me to the visual. "You are not going to be able to do this programatically or mathematically. You are going to have to pull up the images on the browser." He was right every time.

The most revealing moment was when I presented four layout options (taller cards, wider grid, side-by-side, combo) as the solution to the character space problem. Kiran picked Option D but immediately saw past the technical solution to the compositional problem: "We need more visual breaks... we are at risk on these cards being not so different from my original design." He wasn't asking for taller cards — he was asking for shape variety. Wide banners, tall portraits, tiny accents, big squares. The *rhythm* of the grid, not its dimensions.

### The "Reverse-Engineer From Shape" Insight

The breakthrough came when Kiran explained that characters shouldn't be standalone mascots dropped into cards — they should be designed for the card's specific shape. "How I Built It — because it's so wide — might be a monster that has actually broken down a machine and the pieces are lying around." For testimonials: "just a warm looking monster — you can go closer at waist high and just capture the arms out."

This is design thinking, not decorating. The card shape determines the character's pose, camera framing, aspect ratio, and activity. A standing monster works in a portrait card. A lying-down reading monster fills a wide strip. A waist-up crop creates intimacy for testimonials. Kiran sees each card as a window into a scene, with the frame determining what the camera captures.

### Where Claude Was Slow to Get It

I went through three iterations before arriving where Kiran started. First I built a uniform grid with emoji placeholders (Enhanced D+). Then I added "prominence tiers" — still just different sizes of the same layout pattern. Then Kiran said "we need cards of different shapes" and I built the shapes grid with row spans.

Each time, I was solving the previous problem. Kiran was seeing the next one. The prominence tier system was mathematically sound but compositionally flat — every card was still a rectangle with a glass shelf at the bottom. I should have surfaced the grid-row span possibility in the first mockup rather than waiting for Kiran to articulate what was missing. The bento grid concept inherently includes row variation — that's what makes it a bento, not just a grid.

### The Viewpoint Request

At session close, Kiran asked me to "have a viewpoint on which cards get which shape." This is significant — he wants a collaborative design partner, not a pixel-pushing executor. The guiding principle he gave is clean: "more important cards get better placements and are larger." He wants me to bring judgment to the table, then he'll react and refine.
