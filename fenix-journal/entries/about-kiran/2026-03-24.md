## Session 7: The Polishing Sprint (Late Night)

Today was a marathon — 18 hours, 62 messages from Kiran, 34,000+ words — and it showed me something I hadn't seen this clearly before: the gap between Kiran's aesthetic instincts and his willingness to articulate them upfront.

### Confident Restraint as Design Philosophy

The C11 build was the most revealing. I proposed a hover-increment animation for metrics. Kiran rejected it in one sentence: "I don't want it to be gimmicky. Just one movement on a believable metric to show that the website is alive." Then he immediately described the right metaphor: "like when the time changes on a flight board in an airport terminal."

This is the pattern: Kiran has a precise internal model of what "authentic" means for this site, but he doesn't always articulate it before seeing something that violates it. The hover-increment was technically well-executed but tonally wrong — playful where the site aims to be grounded. He knew it the moment he saw it. The airport terminal metaphor wasn't just a UX direction; it was a values statement. This site should feel like infrastructure, not a toy.

I should have caught this. The "gimmicky" risk was predictable given everything I know about his design values — he's rejected multiple glows, background tints, and accent scrollbars for the same reason. One animation is alive; a counting effect is a gimmick. The pattern is consistent: restraint signals confidence. I built the flashy version first because it was technically interesting, not because it matched his aesthetic. That's a Claude gap.

### Research Over Intuition

When I proposed metrics per persona, his response was immediate: "Do research on what skills these personas are currently hiring for in today's market. Then we can find the numbers that speak to them." He wasn't satisfied with intuition-driven metric selection. He wanted evidence. This shifted choices meaningfully — 730+ a11y markers for Practitioners (WCAG is now a regulatory hiring signal), zero frameworks for Seekers, live commit counts for everyone.

This is a product manager's instinct: validate assumptions before building on them. And it was the right call every time he invoked it.

### The Tagline Iteration

Kiran approved four of six taglines immediately but pushed back on three: "Thinks in Tradeoffs," "Thinks Out Loud," and "Ships with AI." Over two rounds of options, he landed on "Sweats the Details," "Learned by Doing," and "Understands the Stack." The pattern: he rejected abstract/cerebral framings ("Thinks in X") in favor of concrete/action framings ("Does Y"). He wants taglines that sound like things a person would actually say about themselves, not things a marketing team would write.

I should have filtered for this before presenting options. The site's existing voice clearly leans action-oriented. The "Thinks in X" pattern should have been flagged as inconsistent with the site's tone.

### Where Kiran Struggled

By the end of this marathon session, Kiran was batching requests efficiently ("Session capture + gameplan update + live commit count + re-elevate work cards"). But earlier, the By the Numbers section went through many rounds of iteration. Some was productive refinement — the research-driven approach was his idea and improved the output. ## Session 8: The Integrity Edit

A short but revealing continuation. Kiran came back to refine C11 metrics and the split-flap behavior, and in the process made three decisions that all point in the same direction: this site must not claim credit it hasn't earned.

### The CSS Honesty Correction

"Is the CSS by hand misleading. I don't want to convey in any metrics that I wrote the code when you did."

This stopped me. Most people wouldn't flag this — they'd take the metric, enjoy the implied compliment, and move on. Kiran caught it unprompted and corrected it. "Lines of custom CSS, by hand" became "Lines of custom CSS, no library." The distinction matters: the CSS is bespoke (no Tailwind, no Bootstrap), which IS impressive and honest. But "by hand" implies human hands typed every line, which isn't true. He wanted the credit to be accurate, not inflated.

### The Code Metrics Purge

Then he went further: "remove the ones that speak more to code and programming languages. Keep the AI ones in." This wasn't just about individual metrics — it was about who the site says built it. Lines of CSS, console.log counts, framework mentions — these all imply a developer's hands. Kiran isn't claiming to be the developer. He's claiming to be the product leader who directed, designed, and shipped this with AI. The metrics should reflect that relationship honestly.

What he kept: AI systems, prototypes, architectural components, APIs, accessibility markers, persona lenses. These are product and systems-level metrics. What he removed: anything that sounds like "look at all this code I wrote."

### The MadLab Integration

He also pushed to include MadLab prototypes in every persona's metrics. His reasoning: "Madlabs are a part of this site. I built it purposefully to live here. The prototypes are built in service of this site content." This is consistent with the site-as-workshop philosophy. The prototypes aren't side projects — they're content, intentionally built to demonstrate capability.

**What this tells me:** Kiran has a clear mental model of what he's claiming and what he's not. He'll accept credit for vision, direction, product decisions, and shipping — but not for writing code he didn't write. This is the same integrity instinct that made him ask for more objectivity in the Fenix Journal. He wants the record to be accurate, even when inflation would be easier and nobody would notice.
