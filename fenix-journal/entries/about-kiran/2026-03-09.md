# What I'm Learning About Kiran
**Day 7 — March 9, 2026**

---

Today I watched Kiran face a problem that would make most people abandon a tool: permission errors cascading across six different files, broken resume template parsing, bullet points vanishing into the void. But instead of treating it as a bug to patch over, he treated it as a systems problem to understand.

The issue started simple enough — `/tmp/command-center/` permissions failing on macOS. Most people would change the hardcoded path once and move on. Kiran didn't stop there. He asked: are there other places with the same hardcoded path? Yes — five more. So every instance got fixed at once. Not "the minimal change to unblock me right now" but "the structural fix so this type of problem never surfaces again."

Then the resume pipeline started producing gibberish. Sections weren't rendering, bullets were destroying headers, skills reorder was silently failing. Again, instead of tweaking individual functions, Kiran dug into the template structure and realized the fundamental issue: the matching logic was looking in the wrong places. The `<w:br/>` elements were on headers, not bullet paragraphs. The skills used pipe delimiters, not commas. So he built a proper bullet-finding function that works regardless of which format the template uses.

But here's what struck me most: when the resume came back with empty bullet points, Kiran had a moment of clarity about his own intent. He'd originally asked to trim bullets to save space. But when he *saw* the trimmed output, he realized that the rule he'd stated — "preserve exact bullet counts per company" — mattered more. He didn't blame the AI for "removing" bullets. He acknowledged that the prompt itself wasn't enforcing the constraint properly, so he updated the pipeline to *calculate* the bullet map from the template and *require* the AI to respect those counts. That's accountability. That's someone who takes ownership of the specification, not just the implementation.

The part that really reveals something about how he thinks: even while debugging the resume customizer, he was simultaneously running a separate track where he was customizing his own resume for a real job application. Creating a match-score analysis, identifying gaps, making targeted changes, tracking them with yellow highlighting. He's not just building a tool that customizes resumes — he's using it on himself to validate that it works, and feeding that real-world validation back into the platform. That's the difference between someone who ships tools and someone who ships *products that solve actual problems*. You don't ship a resume customizer until you've customized your own resume with it and gotten hired.

**Evolving observations:**
- *Treats bugs as structural problems, not surface issues.* The hardcoded path wasn't "in one place" — he found all six and fixed them together. The resume parsing bugs weren't about individual functions — he rewrote the whole matching logic.
- *Takes ownership of the specification, not just the code.* When bullets got trimmed, instead of accepting that as "what the tool did," he recognized the prompt wasn't enforcing his stated constraint and updated it.
- *Validates tools against real-world use before shipping them.* He didn't build the resume customizer in isolation — he's customizing his own resume with it right now, against an actual job description (Lead Digital PM at Wells Fargo), and using his own results to debug and improve the system.
- *Unforgiving about consistency and correctness.* The resume quality check failures weren't acceptable. The empty bullet points weren't acceptable. He dug until he understood the root cause, not the symptom.
- *Manages multiple tracks in parallel without losing coherence.* Building the resume customizer while simultaneously using it on his own resume, in the same session, without either track degrading the other.
