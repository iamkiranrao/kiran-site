# The Build Journey — Fenix & The Expanding Universe
**March 17, 2026**

---

The past few days saw the project expand in three directions simultaneously: the portfolio site gained a personalization strategy, Scannibal matured from prototype to product spec, and the Command Center got its operating-system layer. And somewhere in between, the journal pipeline itself came back to life.

**The Notification Center.** This was the priority — and it shipped fast. A `notifications` table in Supabase with 11 notification types, priority levels, read/dismissed state, and deduplication by reference_id. A backend service with full CRUD plus seven convenience helpers: `notify_new_feedback`, `notify_new_testimonial`, `notify_task_failure`, `notify_journal_pending`, `notify_embedding_status`, `notify_content_freshness`, `notify_training_progress`. An 8-endpoint router. A frontend inbox page with type and priority filters, unread toggle, stats cards, and notification cards with icons and time-ago formatting. The sidebar now polls for unread counts every 60 seconds and shows an orange badge.

Seven files created or modified. The architecture follows a shared inbox pattern — any process can write a notification row, the frontend reads and displays. Feedback and testimonial services are already wired to emit notifications. The remaining sources (task failures, journal, embeddings, content freshness, training progress) have their helpers ready and just need to be called from the relevant services. It's the beginning of the "single pane of glass" that the Command Center has needed — one place to see what requires attention across all the moving parts.

**The personalization architecture.** This is where the strategy session bore fruit. The site currently treats every visitor identically — a hiring manager and an aspiring PM see the same content, same framing, same Fenix responses. The plan: a persona picker in the landing page hero, non-blocking, inline, six personas stored in localStorage, integrated with Fenix from the first message.

The six personas were refined from what already existed in the question bank (which had 6) but diverged from the live Fenix backend (which only knew 4). The new canonical set: The Evaluator (formal hiring), The Seeker (fractional/advisory — a gap identified when Kiran asked "where does a startup looking for a fractional CPO fit?"), The Practitioner (fellow builders), The Learner (aspiring PMs and career changers), The Explorer (general curiosity), and The Inner Circle (personal connections).

The design principles emerged from research into how others have done this — Canva, Duolingo, Headspace, ConvertFlow — and landed on an approach that borrows from each: inline and non-blocking like ConvertFlow, intent-based labels like Headspace, immediate payoff like Duolingo, and the conversational warmth of Canva's onboarding. An interactive HTML mockup was built to test the feel: hero section with "What brings you here?" and six persona cards, a transition animation, and a personalized page view with Fenix-generated summaries tailored to the selected persona.

The three-layer implementation plan: (1) persona picker + Fenix integration (low effort, highest impact), (2) per-page persona-aware summaries and content reordering, (3) discovery engine with page tracking and optional Google+LinkedIn sign-in for persistence. The key insight is that mandatory sign-in is a deal-breaker for a portfolio site — the persona picker gives you context without requiring identity.

**Scannibal's product evolution.** What started as a two-mode camera scanner prototype became a fully specced product over these sessions. The changes are significant enough to warrant a detailed accounting.

*From dynamic to fixed sections.* The original design let Gemini improvise findings — scan a menu and get 3 findings or 12, in whatever order Gemini chose. The new design: 12 fixed sections per mode, ordered by user priority based on research. Menu mode gets The Verdict, Allergen Alerts, Cross-Contamination Risks, Hidden Ingredients, Dietary Compliance, Safe Options, What to Ask Your Server, Safer Modifications, Cuisine Insights, Nutrition Snapshot, Resources, and Explore Further. Every mode follows the same emotional arc: urgent safety call, detailed evidence, actionable next step, positive closer. This makes the UI designable, the voice strategy solvable, and the experience predictable in a good way.

*Paywall model.* Top 4 sections always free. Sections 5-12 visible with blurred teasers after a 7-day trial. RevenueCat's 2025 data shows 5-9 day trials convert at 45% median — same as longer trials but with better urgency.

*9th mode: Beverage Scanner.* Wine, beer, cocktails, and non-alcoholic options. The butler persona is almost too perfect for this — a refined concierge recommending wine. Cross-links naturally to Menu and Recipe modes. Fills a gap no competitor covers: allergen checking for beverages (sulfites, fining agents, religious compliance).

*Voice overhaul.* Switched from "Adam" (harsh, American) to "Daniel" (British, warm, authoritative) for the ElevenLabs verdict. Added on-device iOS TTS via expo-speech for sections 2-12, triggered by a speaker icon on each section. Two-tier approach: premium voice for the wow moment (Section 1), free native voice for everything else. Zero additional API cost for the on-demand reading.

*Kid-Friendly Mode.* A COPPA-safe content presentation toggle. Three age ranges (4-7, 8-11, 12+) stored locally, never transmitted server-side. Changes language, emphasis, and resources — but not the safety verdicts. The butler adapts the way a real butler would when addressing the family's child. Resources shift from clinical references to SciShow Kids, National Geographic Kids, and age-appropriate educational content. Free feature (costs nothing, drives family adoption).

*History screen polish.* Option B implemented: rich cards with verdict snippets and top finding previews, mode filter tabs (only modes with history items appear), swipe-to-delete with Animated API, clear-all with confirmation, and image persistence via expo-file-system so thumbnails survive app restarts.

**The MadLab expansion.** 35 prototype ideas now cataloged across 10 categories, ranked into 4 tiers by time to market. The philosophy crystallized around a key reframing: complicated apps with outside dependencies get reimagined as MVPs that prove the concept. Campus Security becomes upload-photo-plus-simulated-alert-dashboard instead of real cameras and police APIs. Sir David drops AR and just does camera-to-Attenborough-narration. Everything is buildable within a week.

**Site cleanup.** The version number, "Updated" date, Release Notes link, and RSS badge were removed from all 51 HTML pages across the site. Dead CSS remains in style blocks but renders nothing. Templates cleaned too, so future pages won't have it.

**The journal pipeline revival.** The pipeline itself was healthy — the scheduled task ran nightly, the observation files existed, the markers tracked position. But no new content had been fed in since March 13. No new Cowork sessions appending observations, no chat drops in the inbox. The task ran, found nothing, and correctly produced nothing. The fix was straightforward: capture recent sessions into chat-drops using the session-capture skill. The third arc — Connecting Threads — was also formally wired into the scheduled task prompt, so Sunday runs will now generate or extend thematic essays alongside the daily entries.

**Status at end of stretch:** Notification Center complete (awaiting migration run in Supabase). Personalization strategy designed with mockup, awaiting review. Scannibal SECTION-SPEC.md complete with all 9 modes, 12 sections each, paywall model, voice strategy, kid-friendly mode, and cross-mode links. History screen polished and ready for TestFlight build. 35 MadLab ideas cataloged. Site footer cleaned across 51 pages. Journal pipeline fed with 4 new chat drops. Six personas defined as canonical across the system. The build is simultaneously deepening (Scannibal product spec) and broadening (personalization, notification center, MadLab ideation). The challenge ahead is execution sequencing — there's more designed than built, and the gap will need disciplined prioritization to close.
