# The Build Journey — Fenix & The Expanding Universe
**March 18, 2026**

---

The persona picker went from design spec to working prototype today. Real images, real interactions, real problems. And the biggest problem — the one that ended the session unresolved — turns out to be a fundamental tension between the hero image's aspect ratio and the container it lives in.

**From wireframe to working prototype.** The session opened with Kiran returning from Midjourney with his first character generation. He immediately spotted that portrait cards (3:4) would create excessive vertical scroll, asked for a wireframe comparison, and chose Option B: landscape cards with the Midjourney character on the left ~42% and a gradient-to-dark background with text overlay on the right ~58%. This was a pivotal decision because it changed the image spec from portrait to square (1:1 bust shots), which Midjourney handles much better for character work — tighter framing, no wasted space.

The card composition uses a CSS gradient overlay: the character image sits on the left, a gradient fades from transparent to the card's dark background color, and the persona text (tag, name, hook) sits on the right against that solid dark field. Each persona gets an accent color — sapphire for The Evaluator, amber for The Seeker, teal for The Practitioner, violet for The Learner, coral for The Explorer, copper for The Inner Circle — stored as a CSS custom property (`--persona-accent`) that threads through the entire page.

**Seven Midjourney assets locked.** Over the course of the session, Kiran generated and evaluated dozens of Midjourney variations across all seven assets (1 hero + 6 characters). Key lessons from the Midjourney workflow: V7 does not support `::` prompt weighting (causes an error — use word placement and repetition instead). The `--sref` flag with `--sw` weight control is powerful but can pull the style away from Pixar toward whatever the reference image's aesthetic is — when the hero had a painterly quality, the characters started looking photorealistic. The fix was aggressive Pixar-specific language ("NOT photorealistic, NOT a photograph") and `--no` negative prompts. The WW2 plane easter egg made it into the final hero on the fourth attempt.

**The hover state evolution.** Kiran's first feedback on the prototype was that hover states were too subtle — "the image only shifts slightly, there is no flow or scaling." The fix layered multiple concurrent animations: card lifts 12px with 1.04x scale, character image zooms 8% with brightness boost, an accent-colored border ring fades in via `::before` pseudo-element, a double-layer glow (tight + wide) via `::after`, tag letter-spacing expands from 2.5px to 3.5px, the divider line grows from 30px to 50px, and the hook text brightens. All on a spring-like cubic-bezier curve. The result is that each card feels alive on hover — multiple elements moving at slightly different rates creates organic depth.

**Character image quality.** The Midjourney 1:1 images are large (5-6MB each) being displayed at roughly 200×200px in the cards. The aggressive downscaling caused visible artifacts — Kiran described the hair as looking "pixelated or like it has dandruff." The fix: `image-rendering: crisp-edges` for sharper downscaling plus subtle `filter: contrast(1.02) saturate(1.05)` to restore the Pixar pop that compression was washing out.

**The lead-in redesign.** The original tagline ("Everyone sees something different here") wasn't directive enough. Kiran's feedback: "For someone who doesn't know what personas are, I don't think it is clear enough that they need to pick one." The redesign landed on a three-layer composition: a dramatic statement ("This site *reshapes itself* around you") at 38px Playfair Display with the key phrase in the persona's accent color, a directive subtitle explaining what happens when you choose, and a "Who are you?" scroll hint with a bouncing accent-colored arrow. The accent color on "reshapes itself" dynamically updates when a persona is selected — a small detail that makes the page feel responsive before you've even scrolled to the cards.

**The unsolved hero image problem.** This is the open wound. The hero image (`hero-scene-final.png`) is 2912×1632 pixels — a 16:9 aspect ratio. At a typical 1440px-wide viewport with 65vh hero height (~630px), the image scales to 1120×630px, which means the image must be cropped from 1632px tall down to 630px — losing 61% of its height. Even at 75vh (~810px), you lose 50%. The dog sleeping under the desk sits in the bottom ~20% of the image. The Golden Gate Bridge is in the top ~15%. Showing both simultaneously in a container that's wider than 16:9 is geometrically impossible with a single `object-fit: cover` rule.

Five approaches were tried and failed: `object-position: center 40%` (dog invisible), `center 70%` (dog semi-visible but top cropped), `center bottom` with reduced fade (lamp became focal point), 75vh with 120% height for parallax room (still hidden), and `object-fit: contain` (shows everything but betrays the cinematic intent — Kiran rejected this as "giving up").

The real solutions that need to be evaluated: regenerating the hero at a wider aspect ratio (21:9 or even 3:1) that fits the container naturally with the dog visible; a split-layer approach where the dog is extracted and positioned as a separate element; a taller hero (85-90vh) that accepts being nearly full-screen to show more of the image; or a scroll-reveal composition where the image starts zoomed to the top and slowly reveals the full scene as the user scrolls toward the persona cards. Each has tradeoffs. The Midjourney regeneration path is the most reliable because it solves the problem at the source rather than fighting it in CSS.

**Status:** Persona picker prototype is functional with real images, hover states, selection logic, parallax, and the redesigned lead-in. All 7 Midjourney assets are locked. The hero image display is the critical blocker — it needs to be solved before this can ship. The answer likely lives in Midjourney (wider aspect ratio) rather than CSS (fighting the geometry).
