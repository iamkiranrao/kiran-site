# The Build Journey — The Unlock Architecture Takes Shape
**April 3, 2026**

---

## No Code, All Architecture

Today's session produced zero lines of code and zero file modifications. What it produced was something more valuable for the project's trajectory: the strategic framework for how the site's persona system converts anonymous visitors into identified connections.

This is the first session dedicated entirely to product strategy for a net-new system. Previous sessions have been build-heavy — skills page, Command Center modules, persona picker production. Today was pure design thinking.

---

## The Three-Module Architecture

The system Kiran described has three interconnected components that need to work as a unified experience:

**Module 1: Persona Unlock (Left Column)** — A visual list of what the visitor gets as their selected persona. Divided into two tiers: "Explore freely" (anonymous, immediate value) and "Connect to unlock" (identity-gated, higher-value features). This replaces the current undifferentiated unlock list in the PERSONA-PLAYBOOK.

**Module 2: Fenix Introduction (Right Column)** — The visitor's first encounter with Fenix. Not the floating action button or the subpage banner that was removed yesterday — a proper introduction with persona-aware framing. Fenix serves as the concierge: explains each unlock, has pills that map to the unlock options, and guides the visitor through both anonymous and connected flows.

**Module 3: Connect (Embedded Conversion)** — Not a separate page. The conversion mechanism lives inside the Fenix conversation. When a visitor chooses a connected unlock, Fenix initiates the identity flow inline.

The key architectural insight: these three modules are visually adjacent but functionally sequential. The unlock list is the menu, Fenix is the waiter, and Connect is the moment you commit. The visitor experiences them as one coherent section, not three separate components.

---

## The Connect Mechanism Decision Space

Three approaches were discussed, each with different tradeoffs:

**OAuth (LinkedIn/Google):** Lowest friction, highest identity verification, but feels like SaaS onboarding. The "Sign in with LinkedIn" button carries product associations that clash with the site's warm, personal tone.

**LinkedIn Connection Request:** Most relational, creates an actual human connection, but highest friction (leave site, go to LinkedIn, click connect, come back). Verification is also hard — how does the site know the connection happened?

**"Introduce Yourself" Form:** Middle path — name and company in a simple form, no OAuth popup. Feels human (you're introducing yourself to Fenix), low friction, but lower identity verification (someone could type a fake name).

The direction agreed on: **offer both LinkedIn OAuth and "introduce yourself" as parallel options.** Fenix presents the choice: "You can sign in with LinkedIn for instant access, or just tell me who you are." This respects both the efficiency crowd and the warmth crowd. The technical implementation will need to support both flows feeding into the same backend notification pipeline.

---

## The Anonymous / Connected Split Principle

The strategic principle that emerged: **the gate must be functional, not artificial.** If someone asks "why do I need to connect for this?", the answer should be "because the feature needs your context to be useful" — not "because I want your email."

This means connected unlocks aren't premium versions of anonymous ones. They're *different capabilities* that genuinely require knowing who the visitor is. The Fit Score for a recruiter needs their specific job description and company context. The "Bring Your Problem" mode for a founder needs to know what they're actually building. The pairing session for a technologist needs to know who they're pairing with.

Anonymous unlocks, conversely, should be genuinely generous — complete and valuable on their own. The visitor should walk away thinking "that was worth my time" even if they never connect. The emotional pull toward connecting should come from *wanting more of this quality*, not from feeling tricked by an incomplete teaser.

---

## The Personality Layer

An important design question surfaced: should anonymous unlocks be purely utilitarian (resume download, frameworks, case studies) or include personality-driven features (something unexpected, delightful, human)?

The principle proposed: every persona should get at least one anonymous unlock that's pure personality — something that makes Kiran feel like a real person, not a portfolio — alongside the utility features. The personality features are what create emotional residue. They're the thing that makes someone think "I want to know more about this person" rather than "I got what I came for."

Specific personality features weren't designed yet — that's for the per-persona deep dives. But the architectural slot is reserved: each persona's anonymous tier must include a utility feature AND a personality feature.

## The Evaluator Deep Dive — First Persona Takes Shape

The second half of the session moved from architecture to application. The Evaluator persona (Merritt Hunter — recruiters and hiring managers) was the first deep dive, chosen because: highest stakes for Kiran's career goals, the Fit Score is the most technically complex connected unlock, and getting the hardest persona right first makes the remaining five easier.

### What Landed

**Resume as a lens system.** The utility unlock isn't a single resume — it's a set of lens variants. Same career, same truth, different emphasis. An AI PM lens foregrounds the Fenix build and AI product experience. A Growth PM lens foregrounds user acquisition and metrics. A Product Strategy lens foregrounds the portfolio thinking and cross-functional leadership. Traditional PDF format (recruiters need ATS compatibility), plus an unconventional rendered page version that includes site work as portfolio evidence.

**"What recruiters never ask but should."** The personality feature — the thing that makes Kiran feel human, not portfolioish. The concept: surface the interview questions that would actually reveal whether someone is the right fit, but that recruiters never think to ask. Needs a research pass and Kiran needs to author the actual answers, but the concept is locked.

**"What's not on my resume" — the design DNA story.** A specific narrative about Kiran's college experience building design systems that never appears on a resume but explains how he thinks about product. Kiran identified the story himself; he'll write it.

**Dual Fit Score (connected unlock).** The standout product idea of the session. Not just "does Kiran fit your role?" but "does your role fit Kiran?" Mutual evaluation. The recruiter pastes a job description, Fenix analyzes the match in both directions. This differentiates it from every skills-matching tool and signals Kiran's values (evaluating opportunities, not just seeking approval). Technically complex — needs a scoring session to design dimensions, weighting, and the reverse-evaluation logic.

**Post-connect escalation.** The pager service (direct notification to Kiran) was repositioned as a post-connect escalation, not an unlock. Sequence: connect → fit score establishes mutual interest → THEN offer the pager. This prevents the pager from feeling transactional.

### What's Still Cooking

**Constraints.** Kiran proposed a 3-per-persona maximum (2 anonymous + 1 connected) and a 3-part prioritization test: (1) Does this match the persona's job-to-be-done? (2) Is it differentiated from what they'd find on any portfolio? (3) Is it authentically Kiran? These are proposed, not locked — need explicit confirmation.

**Visual format.** The unlock component's visual design is unresolved. Is it text, menu options, cards? What makes someone pause and think "THIS is something interesting"? This needs a prototyping session.

**Interview anti-pattern game.** Concept is "spot the dysfunction" — common interviewing mistakes that signal organizational problems. Kiran likes the idea but needs a better name and will author the real answers from experience.

### Architectural Observations

The deep dive revealed something about the system's complexity that wasn't apparent during the framework session: each persona's unlock design is essentially a *mini product design* with its own user journey, emotional arc, and conversion psychology. The Evaluator alone produced 8+ feature ideas before constraints were applied. If all six personas have similar depth, the total design surface is larger than the site itself.

This reinforces Kiran's instinct to go slow. Rushing through all six would produce surface-level thinking. Going deep on the Evaluator — understanding that a resume download isn't just "download a PDF" but a lens system with trust framing and format decisions — sets the quality bar for the remaining five.

### The Container Decisions

Cards have: icon, title, one-line hook, state indicator, hover reveal. Open cards: full opacity, accent border, CTA on hover. **Fenix intro voice for Evaluator:** "Fenix, at your service" heading. Positioning line: "I know Kiran's work better than his resume does." Pitch bridges to pills and invites both structured and open-ended exploration. Register: sharp colleague who respects your time.

**Connect moment:** Fenix explains the Dual Fit Score, why identity is needed (functional gate — needs JD and identity to generate personalized report), presents dual path (LinkedIn OAuth and introduce yourself form as visual equals). After identity: both paths converge on "paste your JD." Bail-out: silent redirect to anonymous unlocks. Re-engagement: one gentle nudge after anonymous unlocks explored.

### The Content Decisions

**Resume lenses validated against market data.** Initial proposal was AI PM, Growth PM, Product Strategy. Market research showed Product Strategy is table stakes at VP level, not a differentiating lens. Kiran pushed further: the lenses should serve where he's going, not just where he's been. Final set: AI Product Leader (Anthropic/OpenAI/Google), Growth & Experimentation (Uber/Airbnb/Meta), Mobile & Consumer Product (Netflix/Disney/all of the above). The third lens — Mobile & Consumer — is the strategic choice. It breaks Kiran from the banking anchor and speaks to the consumer-first companies on his target list. No generic fallback: three lenses, no catch-all.

**Recruiter questions: 12 researched, 5 to be selected.** Research produced questions validated against First Round Capital interviews, Hacker News discussions, and executive coaching literature. Questions span values revelation, intellectual flexibility, accountability, and power-dynamic flipping. Kiran will narrow to 5 and author answers as homework. Format: hybrid (Fenix intro line + scannable content panel). All 12 questions also logged to Fenix training bank via CC API.

**Dual Fit Score presentation: Unified layout.** Each dimension shows both directions stacked (Kiran → Role score + explanation, then Role → Kiran score + explanation). Tension visible within each row: "Strong AI experience" AND "unclear AI ownership in the JD" for the same dimension. Narrated processing (Fenix describes what she's doing during the LLM call). Key addition: "What would increase these scores" — inline notes per dimension below threshold + summary checklist at bottom. This turns the Fit Score from a static report into actionable intelligence the recruiter can bring to their hiring manager.

### The Post-Connect Architecture

After Fit Score renders: Fenix bridge line → follow-up questions from training bank → save/share (PDF download + email). Pager escalation uses Option C: mutual threshold — both forward AND reverse scores must be above threshold. When triggered: "Looks like there's real alignment here." When not triggered: Fenix doesn't mention it. Graceful absence.

**Pager notification mechanism: Pushover**, not PagerDuty. $5 one-time, purpose-built for app-to-human push notifications. But the real decision: build this as a CC notification service, not a Fit Score feature. Generic `notify_kiran()` function that any CC event can trigger with priority levels. Future use cases: persona conversions, testimonials, resume downloads, engagement thresholds, scheduled task completions.

### Fit Score Architecture — All Six Questions Locked

The scoring framework became concrete: asymmetric dimensions, each direction unique.

**Kiran→Role (Can I do this job well?):**
1. Experience & Level Fit — Does my career stage and depth match?
2. Domain & Industry Fit — Have I worked in this world?
3. Technical Depth — Do I have the specific technical capabilities?
4. Core Competencies Match — Am I strong at what they're hiring for?
5. Product Stage Fit — Does my experience match their product stage (early chaos vs scaling)?

**Role→Kiran (Is this company worth my time?):**
1. Culture & Values — Are they authentic about what they stand for?
2. Growth Trajectory — Where are they heading as a company?
3. Product Vision — Is the bet intellectually interesting?
4. Team & Engineering Quality — Are smart people here doing real work?
5. Company Stage & Momentum — Do they have momentum toward their vision?

Each dimension: percentage score (0-100%) with qualitative language bands.
- **Strong:** 85-100% — Clear alignment, confident recommendation
- **Solid:** 65-84% — Real match, can work well
- **Partial:** 45-64% — Mixed signals, room for growth
- **Stretch:** Below 45% — Would require significant adaptation

The band language was finalized after Kiran rejected corporate terminology. "Developing" got cut (sounds like HR performance evaluation). "Gap" got replaced with "Stretch" (reframes misalignment as growth opportunity, not verdict). This matters: the language choice signals whether you see someone as a broken system to fix or as a person with different strengths.

**Scoring mechanics:**
- Two composites, equal-weighted (50% each direction)
- Displayed side by side so recruiter sees both scores simultaneously
- Dimension-level notes: what score is driven by, what would increase it
- Summary checklist at bottom: "What would move both scores into Strong range?"
- **Vague JD handling:** Minimum 3 of 5 dimensions must be scorable. If JD doesn't provide enough context, Fenix declines and diagnoses what's missing. "I'd love to score this, but I need more detail on [X, Y, Z]." Transparent deficit, not judgment.

**Input layers for scoring:**
1. **Required:** Job description (pasted by recruiter)
2. **Autonomous enrichment:** Fenix researches the company — Crunchbase, recent news, investor updates, team compositions — building context for the Role→Kiran evaluation
3. **Conversation context:** Fenix uses conversation history if the recruiter shared additional context ("We're bootstrapped and lean" vs "Series C well-funded")

**Narrated enrichment (critical decision):** Fenix shows his research in real time. "Cross-referencing against Kiran's target companies... match found. Checking public financial data... Series B, 2 years old, 45 people. Reading their last blog post on their technical approach..." The research IS the performance. The recruiter sees the thinking, not just the output.

**Compensation excluded:** Deliberately kept out of dimensions. Compensation is a negotiation, not a fit signal. It gets addressed separately: "If there's alignment on direction, we talk numbers."

### Preferred Companies Integration — The Bonus Pattern

The 25-company list (AI-native, consumer, SaaS, fintech, growth, consulting, Microsoft) feeds into the Fit Score logic.

**When Fenix sees a Preferred Company match:**
- "Cross-referencing against Kiran's target companies... match found."
- Flat +5 point bonus to the Kiran→Role composite (not a multiplier, just a boost)
- Communicates: "You work for a company Kiran is genuinely interested in."

**Non-preferred companies:**
- No penalty, no negative signal, no mention
- Graceful absence — the absence of the bonus says nothing about quality
- Treats every opportunity as potentially valuable, just not on Kiran's active target list

This design respects both the recruiter's opportunity and Kiran's strategic focus without creating a public hierarchy of "dream companies" vs. "backup options."

### Three-Layer Video Strategy — Avatar + Fenix Frame + Persona Option

The video architecture spans three distinct moments and purposes.

**Layer 1: Hero Video (Homepage, pre-persona picker)**
- **Content:** 4-beat narrative about the site's philosophy and what visitors will experience
- **Avatar:** AI-generated avatar, not Kiran's face (see strategic decision below)
- **Purpose:** Set tone before personas are visible; tease the relational, non-transactional nature
- **Tone:** Conversational, direct, no performance — addresses the visitor
- **Duration:** 15-20 seconds (autopay, no click required, mutes after 3 seconds)

**Layer 2: Fenix Opening Frame (Per-persona, immediate)**
- **Trigger:** Immediately after persona selection, before unlocks are shown
- **Content:** Text-based per-persona introduction (locked in final iteration)
- **Purpose:** Fenix's first moment with the visitor, sets conversation tone
- **Framing:** Explains what this site is, what Fenix brings, why it's different
- **No friction:** Text, not video; immediate and scannable

**Layer 3: Optional Persona Video (Persona-specific, gated)**
- **Trigger:** Offered by Fenix as an enrichment option (e.g., "Want to see a short video on how I'd approach your specific needs?")
- **Purpose:** Deeper engagement for visitors who want to go further
- **Not required:** Graceful absence if visitor doesn't engage
- **Format:** Per-persona variations (different script/tone for Evaluator vs. Seeker vs. Practitioner)

**Avatar Decision (Strategic):**
The hero video uses an AI-generated avatar instead of Kiran's face. Two-fold reasoning:
1. **Privacy boundary:** Kiran has a non-digital life outside the website. The site is the public product. The avatar maintains that boundary — introduces visitors to Fenix (an AI) through an AI interface, maintaining thematic coherence.
2. **Appearance bias elimination:** Face-based first impressions introduce unconscious bias (age, ethnicity, gender presentation, attractiveness, etc.). Removing the face removes one layer of implicit evaluation. The site's architecture and thinking become the primary signal instead of appearance.

This is a deliberate creative choice, not a cost-saving decision. The avatar needs to feel intentional and cohesive with the site's visual language, not generic.

### Fenix Opening Frame — Locked Text (Evaluator)

The exact text that renders immediately after a recruiter selects their persona:

> "Quick context before we start. This isn't a portfolio site. It's a product Kiran built. The resume comes in three versions, each tuned to a different kind of search. There's a Fit Score that evaluates both directions — whether Kiran fits the role and whether the role fits Kiran. And I'm not a template chatbot. I've been trained on Kiran's actual work, his decisions, and how he thinks. This site isn't designed for a 30-second skim. But every minute you spend here will surface insights you'd otherwise spend weeks piecing together. The more you experience the uniqueness of this site, the more you understand about how Kiran thinks and works. I'm here to help you focus on what matters to you."

Key moments in this text:
- **"It's a product Kiran built"** — Reframes the site itself as the evidence, not a marketing asset
- **"Evaluates both directions"** — States the mutual evaluation concept upfront
- **"I'm not a template chatbot"** — Sets Fenix apart from generic AI
- **"Every minute you spend here will surface insights"** — Respects time, promises value
- **"The more you experience the uniqueness..."** — Contributed by Kiran himself in the session; this is the key line
- **No "pitch" language** — The word "pitch" was explicitly rejected as too salesy; the framing is informational, not persuasive

This frame was drafted by Claude, refined through dialogue, and the closing line was authored by Kiran. It represents the collaborative voice: Claude structures, Kiran authors authenticity.

Five more personas need their own opening frames, designed in subsequent sessions with similar structure: explain what this persona will experience, how Fenix serves that archetype, why spending time here matters.

When Claude proposed stubbing out some backend work ("placeholder notification, mock Fit Score calculation, etc."), Kiran pushed back: "Why the stubs? Why not build those if there is no ambiguity?" He's right. The requirements are clear:

No ambiguity means no stubs. Build the real thing. This became the commitment: full production Evaluator experience, not a prototype. Ship with the real functionality on day one.
