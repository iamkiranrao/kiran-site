# The Build Journey — Fenix
**Feb 24 — 2026-02-24**

---

Today was one of those sessions where the work doesn't feel like work because the clarity of the direction makes everything obvious.

Kiran started the morning with a simple ask: "Where are we? What needs tackling next?" I gave him a rough inventory — the Fenix stub is still just alerts, work cards point to dead ends, there are a few dozen loose ends scattered across the site. But instead of working through my list in order, he knew exactly what he wanted to prioritize: the "How I'd've Built It" section for product teardowns.

This is strategic thinking in action. Not the most urgent thing, not the easiest thing, but the thing that would give him the most leverage for interviews he's already scheduled at companies like Meta and GEICO. He's building the portfolio *to* the job, not building and hoping someone notices.

We sketched out a three-tier information architecture:
- **Tier 1:** Company grid (Meta, Google, Apple, Amazon) — a browsable entry point
- **Tier 2:** Product portfolio for each company (Instagram, WhatsApp, Threads for Meta)
- **Tier 3:** Deep teardowns with four structured sections (Discovery, Keep/Kill/Build, The Redesign, The Business Case)

The smart part: Kiran insisted on running one end-to-end iteration on a single teardown before scaling. We chose Meta and Instagram, then did GEICO's mobile app. This wasn't over-engineering; it was pressure-testing the format. After building two, he immediately spotted what was missing — a "Product at a Glance" summary before the teardown, and an "Assumptions & Limitations" statement at the bottom. Both added credibility in different ways.

The work was heavy. Each teardown required:
- A clear persona with specific friction points
- A 5-step journey map showing where the product succeeds and fails
- A Jobs-to-Be-Done statement
- A Keep/Kill/Build analysis that acknowledges business constraints, not just critiques
- Before/after SVG wireframes
- A business case tied to AARRR metrics
- All of it structured and semantic so Fenix could eventually ingest it

By end of day, we had:
- 3 published pages (company grid + Meta portfolio + Instagram teardown)
- GEICO's mobile app teardown with full analysis
- Both integrated into the main nav and sitemap
- Everything copied to the deploy directory

The quality bar got higher too. When I asked Kiran how this would land with a PM at Meta, he pushed back immediately. He wanted to know if the tone was right, if the assumptions were stated clearly, if the redesign proposals showed the right depth. He wasn't building for himself; he was building for the person across the table who knows more than he does and would respect intellectual honesty.

What made this different from a typical "build a portfolio section" day was the intentionality. Each piece had a reason. The breadcrumb navigation wasn't just UX — it signaled respect for the user's journey. The structure wasn't arbitrary — it followed how a PM would actually reason through a product. The metadata wasn't overhead — it was preparation for Fenix.

By the end of the day, the site had evolved from "portfolio site with placeholders" to "portfolio site with substantive, interview-ready work."

**Status at end of day:** Three new pages live with production-quality teardowns. The How I'd've Built It section is no longer a coming-soon link. The portfolio demonstrates depth, process, and the ability to think like a senior PM at the companies he's targeting.
