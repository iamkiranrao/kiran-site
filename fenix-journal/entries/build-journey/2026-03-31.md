# The Build Journey — From Zones to Timeline
**March 31, 2026**

---

## The Shift: Reframing Happens When You Question the Frame

No production code was shipped today. Four working prototypes were built (tr-zones-prototype.html, tr-options-prototype.html, tr-parallel-v2.html, tr-pingpong-v3.html) — but they're research artifacts, not deployed features. The real work was strategic: fundamentally restructuring how the career narrative gets presented.

---

## The Architecture Before: Four Self-Proclaimed Zones

The previous direction organized the career into four labeled categories:
- **Scale:** Peak user numbers and high-stakes financial impacts
- **Range:** Domain breadth across seven major product areas
- **AI Trajectory:** Continuity of AI work from 2016–2025
- **Builder:** Hands-on proof via the site and projects

Each zone had its own visual personality (observatory dashboard, transit map, accelerating timeline, workbench artifacts). The structure was architecturally clean. It also had a fundamental problem: it put Kiran's interpretation *first*. The reader had to trust his framing that these four things matter. The page said "here's how I want you to understand me" instead of "here's what happened in the world while I was working."

---

## The Reframing: Parallel Timeline as Structural Principle

Around the 07:54 timestamp in the session, Kiran shifted the question from "how do we visually distinguish zones?" to "what if we organize by what was happening in the world, and show my work in response?" That's not a design question. That's a narrative architecture question.

The new structure runs world events down the left (Touch ID ships, COVID closes branches, Zelle launches, ChatGPT's moment hits) and work items on the right (biometric login shipped, digital channels, payment platform scaled, AI integration prioritized). The reader's eye bounces left-to-right and *draws the connection without being told*.

Three specific architectural wins in this approach:

**First, company names move from headers to footnotes.** Wells Fargo and First Republic don't appear as organizational anchors. They show up in small italic text below each era ("AVP, Digital Product — Auth & Mobile"). This breaks the banking stigma that was baked into the previous structure.

**Second, the organizing principle is transparent.** The page says "The world" on the left and "What I was building" on the right. The reader immediately understands the structure's logic. No interpretation required.

**Third, the content becomes curated by era, not by claim.** Instead of "Here's my scale" (which demands defense), it's "Here's what happened, here's what I shipped in response." The evidence organizes itself.

---

## Four Prototypes, Three Iterations of Direction

**Prototype 1 — tr-zones-prototype.html:** Four distinct visual zones testing whether differentiation would carry the narrative. Observatory (hero-sized numbers), transit map (domain connections), accelerating timeline (AI progression), workbench artifacts (hands-on projects).

*Assessment:* Beautiful visual variety. Still reads as categories. Still self-proclaimed.

**Prototype 2 — tr-options-prototype.html:** Three competitive structures explored in one page.
- Option 1 (Pure Eras): Chronological organization with world events as headers, work items in a grid, company names minimal
- Option 2 (Parallel Tracks): World events left, work items right, colored thread tags showing patterns, center divider
- Option 3 (Eras + Synthesis): Compact scanning with synthesis cards at the bottom

*Assessment:* Option 2 (Parallel Tracks) immediately felt strongest because it makes the juxtaposition *visual*. But it was dense. Thread tags added noise. Four work items per era competed with the world events.

**Prototype 3 — tr-parallel-v2.html:** Refined Option 2, leaning into the parallel concept.
- Column headers ("The world" / "What I was building") for clarity
- World events breathe — bold headlines followed by dated sub-events
- Work items are tighter — title, description, one metric
- Company names are present but footnoted
- Synthesis cards at the bottom for cumulative payoff

*Assessment:* This version works. The magic is the horizontal juxtaposition — the reader's eye bounces left to right and the connection forms in their head.

**Prototype 4 — tr-pingpong-v3.html:** Adding directional relationship system.

Three connector types between world events and work items:

- **→ Shipped the answer:** Solid arrow pointing right. Kiran responded fast to market shift. (Touch ID → biometric login, Zelle launch → payments platform, Apple Pay adoption → digital strategy)

- **← Already there:** Solid arrow pointing left. Kiran was building it before the world caught up. (DOC AI in 2016, years before the ChatGPT moment; digital channels during COVID; Fargo AI scaling when ChatGPT made AI mainstream)

- **··· Built before the world noticed:** Dashed line with glowing dot. No predetermined response — the thing was built on its own trajectory. (Device fingerprinting, cardless ATM, Avatour pivot)

Each work item gets an explicit label so the relationship is transparent.

The payoff: "Of 12 major technology moments over 13 years: 5 times already there. 4 times shipped the answer. 3 times built before anyone noticed. The pattern isn't luck. It's positioning." The reader watched the pattern accumulate as they scrolled. They counted it themselves.

*Assessment:* This works structurally and narratively. It turns a career timeline into an argument about positioning.

## Technical Decisions and Tradeoffs

**Decision: Content-first over visual-first.** The prototypes skip production design (icons, illustrations, custom SVG shapes). They focus on content layout and narrative structure. This is correct — the architecture has to work before we design it. But it means the visual identity work is still ahead.

**Decision: Edit world event copy for editorial voice.** The first version of the parallel timeline used factual event names ("COVID-19 pandemic," "Zelle launches"). Those work, but they're flatter than they could be. "COVID closes every branch in America" lands harder and makes the juxtaposition sharper. This voice work needs to happen per-era.

**Decision: Curate work items per era, not show everything.** The prototypes show 2-4 items per era. The full career-highlights data includes 30+ initiatives. Showing all of them would kill the parallel timeline structure — too much on the right side, it competes with the world events. The curated set (strongest 2-3 per era) lets both sides breathe. This means editorial judgment calls about what "counts" as illustrative.

**Unresolved: Cloud migration framing.** Kiran mentioned AWS/Google Cloud migration work at First Republic that he doesn't typically highlight because he wants to position for client-facing PM roles. This is meaningful infrastructure work, but if it appears, how? As a work item in its own era? As a note about enabling? The session surfaced it but didn't lock the decision.

---

## What's Still Ahead

The prototypes proved the structure works. The build from here needs:

1. **World event copy refinement** — sharper, more editorial voice per era (currently 30% done)
2. **Work item curation** — which 2-3 items per era make the cut? (30% done)
3. **Connecting arrows** — implementing the three connector types with animation (not started)
4. **Visual identity** — icons, typography treatment, color system (not started; Claude fingerprint risk is high here)
5. **Synthesis cards** — final design and messaging for the cumulative payoff at the bottom (drafted, not visualized)
6. **Mobile responsive behavior** — how the parallel structure collapses on small screens (drafted conceptually, not tested)

The narrative architecture is locked. The execution is still ahead.

---

## Lessons That Generalize

When a structure feels right but the problem definition is wrong, the quality of the structure doesn't matter. Kiran had the wisdom to sense misalignment before committing to a build. That's rarer than it sounds.

The parallel timeline structure works because it inverts the burden of proof. Instead of "trust my framing," it's "here's what happened, you draw the conclusion." That's a narrative move that carries beyond this one page — it's a principle about how to present evidence without appearing to sell.

The three-arrow system (shipped the answer / already there / built before noticed) is elegant partly because it came from a specific question ("Does it need a ping-pong effect?") rather than from a desire to add complexity. The constraint made it stronger.
