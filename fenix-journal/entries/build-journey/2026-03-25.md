## The Layout Decision: Bento Grid

After evaluating five layout options against UX research (NNGroup on cards, Material Design 3, paradox of choice, cognitive load), Kiran gravitated toward the bento grid (Option D). The key insight: variable card widths communicate hierarchy without needing the visitor to sort 8 equal-weight options.

The layout uses a 6-column CSS grid base where cards can span 2, 3, 4, or 6 columns. Row patterns are flexible — any combination that adds up to 6 per row works. This makes the grid composable: cards can be added, removed, or resized without rebuilding the layout.

Kiran specifically asked about dynamic sizing based on interaction patterns. Three approaches are viable:

1. **Persona-driven** — hero card swaps based on selected persona (infrastructure exists today)
2. **Analytics-driven** — high-engagement cards get wider spans over time (needs click data)
3. **Freshness-weighted** — recently updated cards get promoted (needs metadata system)

The persona-driven approach is implementable immediately.

## Xbox Metro as Design Reference

Kiran raised the Xbox 360 Metro dashboard as a reference for the bento grid. The connection is apt: Metro used variable-sized tiles where size communicated importance, and the whole grid scrolled as a composition. What worked: clear hierarchy, bold visual language. What failed: the tiles got cluttered with ads, and the grid couldn't scale past 20-30 items.

The lesson for this site: every tile earns its space by serving the visitor, not the site's aspirations. The Store card was the "ad tile" — taking up grid space with no visitor value. Removing it is the equivalent of the Xbox Series X redesign that reduced tile clutter and centered personalized content.

## Session 2: The Build — And the Overcorrection

This session was execution. Four commits shipped, moving from strategy to live code. The 7-card bento grid went live, then expanded to 9 cards, then underwent aggressive visual compression. Here's the arc.

### What shipped

Commit 1 (6fc0191): Replaced the old 7-card layout with 9 cards — adding Under the Hood and Testimonials. Swapped all photographic images for 48px color header strips with unicode placeholder icons. Updated BENTO_SPANS from [4,2,3,3,2,2,2] to [4,2,3,3,2,2,2,3,3]. Updated all 6 persona cardOrder arrays and cardConfigMap.

Commits 2-3 (2332d9c, 9f8dd48): Progressive compression. Header strips reduced from 48px to 32px. Descriptions hidden on 2-span cards, then also on 3-span cards (only the hero shows its description). Gap tightened from 1.5rem to 0.75rem.

Commit 4 (8c59d9a): Cache-busting query param on the CSS link — Netlify CDN was serving stale CSS, so measurements kept reporting old values even after deployment.

### The dimension hunt

The research target was 50-55vh. The grid started at 75vh after the 9-card expansion. Through three rounds of compression (header height reduction, description hiding, gap tightening), it landed at 60vh. The math: row 1 (139px, hero with desc) + rows 2-4 (117px each, no desc) + 3 gaps (36px) = 527px / 876px viewport = 60vh.

### The overcorrection

Here's where I failed the session. In optimizing for the dimension target, I stripped too much visual identity from the cards. The 32px color strips are too thin to carry meaning. The hidden descriptions leave cards as title + tags in a dark box with too much negative space. Kiran saw it immediately: "looks like a wall of text... I'm not sure the icons will be able to ground the user right away."

This is a classic engineering overcorrection. The photographic images were too loud (cropped badly at 3:1, composed for different aspect ratios), so the "fix" was to remove images entirely. But the actual problem wasn't "images are present" — it was "these specific images don't work at this size." The solution should have been "better visuals at the right scale," not "no visuals."

The session ended with Kiran correctly identifying the middle ground: tasteful visuals integrated into the card body, not as a header strip on top. Research links to bento grid inspiration were compiled for him to browse overnight.

### Technical lessons

1. **CDN caching bit us twice.** The first push appeared to have no effect because Netlify served stale CSS. Adding `?v=20260325` to the stylesheet link forced cache invalidation. This should be automated — either hash-based filenames or a build step that appends a git commit hash.

2. **CSS Grid row height is determined by the tallest item in the row.** This meant hiding descriptions on 2-span cards in row 1 saved nothing — the hero card's height set the row. Understanding this earlier would have saved an iteration round.

3. **The persona system survived the expansion cleanly.** Going from 7 to 9 cards required touching only the BENTO_SPANS array, the persona cardOrder arrays, and the cardConfigMap. The architecture held. That's a sign the earlier refactor (index-based → ID-based lookup) was the right call.

### Where it stands

9 cards, 4-row bento, 60vh, placeholder visuals. The structural skeleton is solid. The visual skin needs rethinking. Tomorrow's session starts with a design direction for card visuals — informed by the bento grid inspiration links compiled tonight.

## Session 3: The Visual Direction Crystallizes

Kiran came back with research and a mockup. The design direction is now clear: illustrated monster characters as card identities, with a three-layer compositing approach and glassmorphism text overlays.

### The Design Vision

Each card gets a unique character illustration (Pixar-style monster) that serves as the card's visual identity. The characters are thematically linked (all monsters) but individually distinct — different shapes, colors, postures, personalities. Text content overlays on a frosted glass panel, letting the illustration breathe through.

Select cards have characters that "spill out" of the card boundary — a tentacle extending past the edge, a character peeking over the top — creating depth and breaking the grid's visual uniformity. This was the strongest recurring pattern across all five inspirations Kiran studied.

### The Three-Layer Architecture (Approach C)

After evaluating three implementation approaches, the recommended architecture is layered compositing:

1. **Layer 1: Gradient background** — CSS-driven, adapts to any card size automatically. Colors shift from the current 9-color palette toward a unified warm neutral family (sandy tones, warm grays) so the characters become the color, not the background.
2. **Layer 2: Character illustration** — Transparent PNG, absolutely positioned, repositioned/scaled via CSS per span class. This is the one layer that needs to be "aware" of card size.
3. **Layer 3: Glassmorphism text overlay** — Frosted glass panel containing the eyebrow, title, description, and tags. Light frosted in light mode, dark frosted in dark mode.

This approach solves the persona permutation problem cleanly. When the persona system moves a card from span-4 to span-2, Layer 1 adapts automatically (CSS gradient), Layer 2 gets repositioned via a CSS rule like `.card-span-2 .card-character { transform: scale(0.7); right: -10px; }`, and Layer 3 adapts via flex layout. No duplicate assets needed per size — just CSS positioning rules.

### The Permutation Map

The persona reorder analysis revealed the actual size variants each card needs:

- **how-id-built-it:** spans 2, 3, 4 (the only card needing all three)
- **my-work, blog-podcast:** spans 2, 4
- **my-sandbox, now:** spans 3, 4
- **creative-lab, testimonials, under-the-hood:** spans 2, 3
- **learning:** span 2 only

Total: 18 card-size combinations. But with the layered approach, this is 18 CSS positioning rules, not 18 separate image assets. The character illustrations themselves are just 9 files.

### The Color Decision

The current 9-color gradient palette (sage green, gold, olive, steel blue, purple, green, terracotta, slate blue, dusty rose) will likely shift to a unified warm neutral family. The reasoning: vivid card backgrounds compete with vivid character illustrations. With neutral backgrounds, the monsters become the visual identity — and persona accent colors remain the only "system colors," avoiding signal confusion.

This is still being discussed — not locked. The key constraints are: legibility of overlaid text in both light and dark mode, no friction with persona accent colors, and a warm approachable feel consistent with Kiran's stated aesthetic preferences.

### What's NOT Changing

The grid structure (6-column, 4-row, BENTO_SPANS pattern), the 9-card lineup, the persona reordering system, and the responsive breakpoints (6-col → 2-col → 1-col) all stay. This is a visual layer redesign on top of a structural skeleton that works.

### External Dependencies

The monster illustrations are the critical path item. They need to be generated externally — Midjourney with `--sref` for style consistency is the primary recommendation, with Ideogram as a fallback. Each illustration needs: transparent or clean background, consistent art style across all 9, personality that maps to the card's content (a studious monster for Learning, a tinkering monster for Under the Hood, etc.).

The responsive consideration: character illustrations need enough breathing room to be repositioned from span-4 (wide, cinematic) to span-2 (narrow, square) via CSS cropping/scaling. Full-width mobile is actually the easiest case — plenty of room.

### Open Questions Heading Into Implementation

1. **Monster character design:** How to generate 9 characters with consistent style via AI tools — needs prompt engineering and potentially multiple rounds
2. **Selective spill-out:** Which specific cards get the spill effect? Likely hero + one in row 2
3. **Text layout variation:** How much layout variation per card? Different alignment per card vs. 3-4 templates assigned to cards
4. **Font treatment:** Same family with varied weights confirmed, potentially a second typeface for stat headlines
5. **Card dimensions:** Still targeting 60vh with flexibility to 65-70vh if the illustration treatment warrants it

## Session 4: Glass Shelf Architecture — and the Spatial Honesty Check

The prototype got its voice this session. Descriptions on all 9 cards, per-card content-aware copy, and a structural fix that was hiding in plain sight.

### Content Lockdown

All 9 cards now have descriptive text. The interesting design constraint: descriptions were already in the HTML for most cards, but CSS `display: none` on `.card-span-2 .work-desc` and `.card-span-3 .work-desc` was hiding them. That rule made sense when the cards had no visual breathing room — stacking eyebrow + title + description + tags in a small card with a full-bleed gradient background created density overload. But with the three-layer architecture giving us a glassmorphism shelf, there's structured space for the text and visual room for the character. So the hide rule was removed and replaced with graduated font sizing (0.85rem → 0.75rem → 0.7rem by span).

**Eyebrow swap:** "Site Architecture" → "The Journey Behind the Build" on the under-the-hood card. Kiran proposed this and it's the right call. "Site Architecture" is a label. "The Journey Behind the Build" is an invitation. It reframes Under the Hood from documentation to narrative — consistent with how the entire site operates as portfolio-as-proof.

### The Glass Panel Problem (and Fix)

This was the most important technical discovery of the session. When Kiran asked "Is there space for the monster characters?" I had to give an honest answer: **no, not with the current CSS.**

The problem: `.card-glass` had `flex: 1`, which stretched the glassmorphism panel to fill the entire card. The character illustration at z-index 1 was entirely behind a `backdrop-filter: blur(16px)` layer. For spill-out cards, the portion outside the card boundary would be visible, but everything inside would be a blurry ghost. With descriptions now added to every card, the glass panel was even more content-dense, making the character even less visible.

**The fix — Glass Shelf Architecture:**
1. Removed `flex: 1` from `.card-glass` — panel is now content-sized
2. Changed `.card-overlay` to `justify-content: flex-end` — glass anchors to bottom
3. Increased card `min-height` per span (260px/280px/300px for span-2/3/4)
4. Repositioned all character placements to the open upper area

This creates a clear visual hierarchy: the top ~40-50% of the card is open space where the monster character lives unobstructed. The bottom portion is the frosted glass shelf with all text content. Your eye catches the character first (open space, high contrast), then drops to the text (glass shelf, organized content).

The layout counterbalancing also got formalized: Layout A (left-aligned text) positions the character upper-right. Layout B (right-aligned text) positions it upper-left. Layouts C/D (centered text) center the character above. This creates visual variety across cards while maintaining a consistent compositing logic.

### Responsive Adjustments

The taller cards required responsive updates. On tablet (≤1024px), all spans collapse to 1-column with 240px min-height, and spill characters normalize to upper-right. On mobile (≤600px), characters shrink to 70px and normalize to upper-right with all transform effects disabled. The glass panel gets tighter padding (0.8rem).

One concern I'm tracking: 260px min-height on span-2 cards is generous for desktop but might feel tall on tablet where they're full-width. This needs real-device testing once the illustrations are in.

### What This Means for the Monster Integration

The glass shelf architecture actually makes monster integration cleaner than the original full-glass plan:

- **Clear staging area:** Each character has a defined region (upper portion) with no blur competition
- **Spill-outs are more dramatic:** Characters spill from their clear staging area, not from behind frosted glass — the visual break is sharper
- **Size-responsive by construction:** The staging area scales with card height, so larger cards get proportionally larger character spaces
- **CSS-only tuning:** When real PNGs arrive, positioning is just `top`, `left`/`right`, `width`/`height` per card ID — the layering system is done

### Remaining Technical Debt

- Per-card character sizes are placeholder estimates (160/120/90px per span). Real illustrations will need individual tuning.
- The `.work-card:hover .card-character` transform needs per-card overrides for centered characters (translateX preservation). Two overrides are in place (learning, testimonials) but this could grow.
- Mobile character normalization (`transform: none !important`) is heavy-handed — might want selective effects on mobile when illustrations are in.
- The /Now vs /Newsfeed eyebrow question was raised but not resolved.

## Session 5: Shape Variety — The Grid Becomes a Bento

This was the session where the grid stopped being a grid and became a composition.

### The Problem That Drove It

Option D (wider + taller cards) was selected from the layout comparison page. The goal: make characters big enough to hold their compositional weight, vary the text containers to break visual monotony, and give important cards more prominence. But after implementing Option D with three prominence tiers (Hero/Important/Supporting), the result was still fundamentally uniform — every card was a rectangle of a different size, but the same *shape*. Kiran called it immediately: "we need more visual breaks... we are at risk on these cards being not so different from my original design."

The fix wasn't adjusting sizes within the existing grid. It was changing the grid itself to produce genuinely different card shapes — wide banners, tall portraits, tiny squares, and landscape rectangles coexisting in the same layout.

### What Was Built: bento-shapes.html

The final prototype uses explicit CSS Grid placement on a 6-column grid with `grid-auto-rows: 220px` and row-spanning cards:

```
Row 1-2: Analyst (cols 1-4, 2 rows) + Veteran (cols 5-6, 2 rows)
Row 3:   Tinkerer (cols 1-3) + Connector (cols 4-6)
Row 4-5: Engineer (cols 1-3, 2 rows) + Artist (cols 4-5, 2 rows) + Explorer (col 6) + Student (col 6)
Row 6:   Storyteller (cols 1-6, full width)
```

This produces five distinct card shapes measured in pixels:
- **WIDE LANDSCAPE (2.1:1):** Analyst at 956×452px — the hero, biggest stage for the most elaborate scene
- **PORTRAIT-ISH (1:1):** Veteran at 472×452px, Artist at 472×452px — tall enough for standing characters
- **LANDSCAPE (1.6:1):** Engineer at 714×452px — two-row span with generous scene width
- **ULTRA-WIDE BANNER (3.2:1):** Tinkerer and Connector at 714×220px — horizontal scenes, characters doing wide activities
- **TINY SQUARE (1:1):** Explorer and Student at 230×220px — intimate close-ups, single-activity scenes
- **MEGA BANNER (6.5:1):** Storyteller at 1440×220px — full-width, the widest possible framing

The variety is real. No two adjacent cards are the same shape. The grid reads as a composition, not a spreadsheet.

### The Auto-Placement Failure

First attempt used `grid-column: span N` without explicit start/end positions. CSS Grid's auto-flow algorithm left gaps wherever column counts didn't add to 6 within a row. Three empty cells appeared in the middle of the layout. The fix was switching every card to explicit `grid-column: start / end` and `grid-row: start / end` placement. No auto-flow, no gaps.

This is the kind of thing I should have predicted. Auto-placement works for uniform grids. For bento layouts with row spans, explicit placement is the only reliable approach. The lesson cost one iteration round.

### The Scene Paradigm Shift

The most important conceptual change wasn't in the CSS — it was in how character illustrations are conceived. Kiran articulated it through examples: "How I Built It — because it's so wide — might be a monster that has actually broken down a machine and the pieces are lying around." For Testimonials: "just a warm looking monster — you can go closer at waist high and just capture the arms out." For Learning: a monster lying on its belly reading a book — a horizontal pose that fills the wide strip naturally.

This is the shift from "design a mascot and drop it into a card" to "the card's shape determines the scene's camera framing, the character's pose, and the activity." The card is a window, and the window's aspect ratio determines what the camera captures. A 6.5:1 banner can't show a standing character — it shows a panoramic scene. A 1:1 square shows an intimate portrait. A 3.2:1 strip shows a character mid-activity with environmental context.

I updated `docs/BENTO-MONSTER-SCENES-V5.md` with full scene concepts and Midjourney prompts for all 9 cards. But the dimensions table in V5 is now outdated — it was written against the bento-enhanced-d.html layout, not the final bento-shapes.html layout. The scene concepts themselves still hold; only the aspect ratios and stage dimensions need updating.

### Where I Was Slow

Three iterations before arriving where Kiran started. First: Enhanced D+ with uniform rectangles. Then: prominence tiers with different sizes but same shape. Then: actual shape variety with row spans. Each time I was solving the previous problem while Kiran was seeing the next one. The bento grid concept *inherently* includes row variation — that's what makes it a bento, not just a grid. I should have surfaced the `grid-row: span 2` possibility in the first mockup.

The other failure: Kiran had to tell me explicitly that I couldn't solve the layout programmatically. "You are not going to be able to do this programatically or mathematically. You are going to have to pull up the images on the browser." He was right. The card dimensions that look right on paper don't necessarily look right on screen. Visual tuning requires visual feedback, not calculations.

### What's Next

The V5 scene prompt doc needs dimension updates for the new card shapes. Then the critical path item: generating the remaining 8 monster character illustrations in Midjourney (only Veteran exists). Once scenes are generated, they integrate as card background images behind the glass shelf — the compositing architecture is already built and waiting.
