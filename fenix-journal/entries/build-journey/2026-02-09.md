## What Got Built

**The GEICO Prep Package**: This isn't code, but it's a structure—a recruiter screening blueprint. Short answers to standard questions. Research talking points. Questions to ask back. The foundation of this is pattern recognition: we identified what GEICO is looking for (Conversational AI, scale, ambiguity tolerance, cross-functional leadership) and mapped Kiran's background against those dimensions. He didn't ask for a 50-page guide; he asked for *actionable answers he could personalize*.

The architecture here is important: I created a template, then Kiran requested completeness. He wanted the full coverage (main answers, recruiter questions, research talking points, compensation baseline). That tells me he thinks systematically about interview prep—not just what to say, but the *entire context* of what's being tested.

**The Payment Rails Learning Path**: Kiran asked for a plan to "upskill as a PM" *and simultaneously* publish content and build visibility. We designed a 12-week curriculum broken into four phases:
- Phase 1 (weeks 1-3): Foundations + publish 2 articles
- Phase 2 (weeks 4-6): Economics & risk + publish 2 articles
- Phase 3 (weeks 7-9): APIs & integration + publish 2 articles
- Phase 4 (weeks 10-12): Emerging rails & strategy + publish 2 articles

The design decision here: **learning and publishing are tightly coupled, not sequential**. Each phase is 3 weeks long. Each phase produces one build artifact (visual map, cost model, working Stripe integration, strategy memo) and two published pieces. This creates a compound effect—by week 12, Kiran is "the PM who writes about payments" *and* has done the deep technical work to back it up.

This is a product roadmap, not a learning plan. It has milestones, deliverables, and cadence.

**The 4-Week Interview Prep Framework**: This is the most ambitious architecture. Kiran wanted three tracks running in parallel:
1. **Build Track**: AI prototypes and portfolio pieces
2. **Publish Track**: Thought leadership on LinkedIn
3. **Interview Track**: Framework mastery, STAR stories, mock interview reps

The design decision: **all three need to feed each other**. The AI prototype he builds becomes a portfolio piece he can discuss in interviews and reference in his LinkedIn posts. The STAR stories from his resume become case studies for the interview track. The payment rails content feeds into demonstrating PM depth. It's not three separate timelines; it's one integrated program with three outputs.

I recommended Exponent ($12/mo) for structured learning, Yoodli (free tier) for delivery coaching, and 3-4 paid mock sessions ($75-150/session) in weeks 3-4. But the real infrastructure is internal: he's going to build a custom Claude-powered interview coach as part of his portfolio work. That kills two birds—he gets a tool to practice with, and he builds a portfolio piece that demonstrates he understands Claude, the API, and product design.

The math: 20+ hours/week across 4 weeks = ~80 hours of focused time. If he allocates ~20 hours to Build, ~20 to Publish, and ~40 to Interview prep, he can realistically:
- Complete Modules 5-6 of the LangChain course + build the interview bot = Build track
- Write 4-6 substantial LinkedIn pieces on PM frameworks, his background, and insights = Publish track
- Do 40+ practice questions, run 3-4 mock interviews, refine his Wells Fargo and Google Docs AI stories = Interview track

This is aggressive but not impossible for someone with his experience level and availability.

## Architecture Decisions

**Decision 1: Payload density**. Every artifact Kiran builds needs to serve multiple purposes. The AI interview coach isn't just for practice—it's a portfolio piece, a resume talking point, and a demonstration of product acumen. The payment rails articles aren't just knowledge building—they're establishing credibility in a domain that maps to Fintech PM roles (Intuit).

**Decision 2: Publication as validation**. Publishing on LinkedIn isn't separate from learning—it's part of the feedback loop. Writing forces clarity. Feedback from the network (likes, comments, follows) signals whether the ideas land. This is how Kiran builds confidence while building visibility.

**Decision 3: Compression without cutting corners**. We're fitting 12 weeks of depth into 4 weeks of prep. But we're not skipping the depth—we're just not doing *everything*. For LangChain, Kiran should focus on Modules 5-6 (LangGraph) and 8 (interview Q&A), skip Modules 1-4, and rely on hands-on learning from the interview bot build. That's a real tradeoff: less breadth, but the build work substitutes for lecture time.

## What Didn't Get Built

The site is secondary infrastructure right now—the real infrastructure is *the person* (his resume, his credibility, his interview readiness). Once he lands the job at Anthropic or Google, the site becomes more powerful as a post-hoc storytelling vehicle. Right now, it's a destination looking for an audience. Better to build the audience first.

## The Bet

Kiran is making a bet: **in 4 weeks, I can become interview-ready at top companies by doing three things in parallel: building visible artifacts, publishing credible work, and practicing until I'm fluent.**

That's different from "study harder" or "get a coach." It's **proof by action**. By publishing, he's proving he can think clearly about PM problems. By building the interview bot, he's proving he understands AI product development. By practicing with mocks, he's proving he can articulate his experience under pressure.

The risk: he's spread thin. If one track stalls (e.g., the interview bot takes longer to build than expected), the others compress further. Also, interview coaching and LangChain mastery are both high-cognitive-load activities. Doing both simultaneously while publishing is... a lot.

The upside: if he executes on even 70% of this, he'll have:
- 4-6 published pieces showing PM depth and voice
- One working AI product as a portfolio piece
- Fluency in interviewing frameworks and his own narrative
- Visible momentum (people can see him building and shipping in real-time)

That's a different signal than "I was a VP for 5 years." It's "I can still ship, learn, and communicate under pressure."
