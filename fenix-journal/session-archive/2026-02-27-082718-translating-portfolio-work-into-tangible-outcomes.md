### Claude `08:27`

Great question, Kiran. You've built up a solid portfolio of practical work — the LangGraph insurance chatbot, the job search management app, the payment systems content — and the key now is making sure each piece is doing double duty: **demonstrating capability** and **opening doors**.

Here's how I'd think about translating it all into tangible outcomes:

**Turn demos into conversation starters, not just portfolio pieces.** The GEICO chatbot demo is a perfect example — bring it into interviews as a live walkthrough, not just a talking point. When you can pull up a working agent and say "here's how I architected this and why," it collapses the gap between "PM who talks about AI" and "PM who builds with AI." For any company on your target list, consider whether a lightweight, tailored demo (even 30 minutes of customization) could differentiate you.

**Package the job search app as a case study in product thinking.** You identified a pain point, scoped an MVP, built it, and iterated. That's a product story. Frame it in interviews using the language hiring managers expect — user problem, hypothesis, solution, metrics, iteration. It shows you practice what you preach.

**Convert your technical learning into public artifacts.** You've been building with LangChain/LangGraph and studying payment systems deeply. Even short LinkedIn posts or a GitHub repo with annotated code ("Here's how I built a multi-step insurance agent with tool use") can establish credibility faster than a polished portfolio site. Hiring managers and recruiters increasingly check for this kind of signal, especially for AI-focused PM roles.

**Use the consulting work strategically.** The Avatour work gives you a current narrative — you're not "between roles," you're actively shipping product thinking. Make sure you can articulate a crisp story about impact there, even if it's early-stage or unpaid.

**Prioritize depth over breadth in your applications.** You're targeting 15/week, which is solid volume. But the demos and content you've built give you a weapon most candidates don't have — the ability to go deep on a handful of dream-role applications with customized materials that show you've already done the work. For your top 3-5 targets (Anthropic, Google, etc.), that extra investment likely has a much higher expected return than two more generic applications.

The throughline: you've already done the hard part by building real things. The leverage now comes from making sure the right people see them in the right context. Would you like to work on any specific piece — like packaging one of these for a particular role or company?

### Kiran `15:53`

How do I get people to discover this content on their own?

---

### Claude `15:53`

This is the right question — shifting from "I show people my work" to "people find my work organically." A few angles:

**GitHub as a discovery engine.** If you have repos for the LangGraph chatbot or the job search app, make sure they have strong READMEs — clear problem statement, architecture diagram, quick-start instructions. People search GitHub for LangGraph examples, insurance chatbot patterns, agentic AI demos. Well-documented repos with descriptive names rank surprisingly well. Add relevant topic tags (langchain, langgraph, conversational-ai, etc.) so they surface in GitHub's explore and search.

**LinkedIn posts that teach, not announce.** "I built a chatbot" gets polite likes. "Here's the non-obvious thing I learned building a multi-step insurance agent with LangGraph" gets engagement and shares. The pattern that works: specific insight → brief story of discovering it → what you'd do differently. Payment systems is another goldmine — there's a huge appetite for people who can explain complex fintech concepts clearly. Even a short series ("Things I wish I'd known about real-time payments") can build a following among exactly the PMs and hiring managers you want to reach.

**Write for the communities where your target audience already hangs out.** A Medium or Substack post titled something like "What building an AI insurance agent taught me about production-ready LLM architecture" hits a sweet spot — it's useful to engineers and PMs evaluating these tools, and it signals exactly the kind of thinking companies like Anthropic and Google want. Cross-post to relevant subreddits (r/LangChain, r/ProductManagement) and Hacker News if the content is substantive enough.

**SEO through specificity.** Broad content gets buried. Narrow content ranks. "LangGraph multi-agent tutorial" has far less competition than "AI chatbot tutorial." If you write about the specific technical choices you made and why, you'll attract exactly the people who are evaluating those same tools — which often includes hiring teams at the companies building them.

**Engage before you publish.** Comment thoughtfully on posts from PMs and engineers at your target companies. Not "great post!" but substantive additions that show your depth. This builds familiarity so that when you do share your own work, there's already an audience primed to pay attention.

The compounding effect is real but slow — the best time to start is now, even with rough first posts. Would you like help drafting a first LinkedIn post or structuring a technical write-up around one of your projects?
