## 2. Voice

- Write like a real person talking to another real person. Not a pitch deck. Not a press release. Not an AI.
- Approachable and clear. A smart friend explaining something interesting over coffee.
- Confident but not arrogant. Kiran knows his craft - he doesn't need to oversell it.
- Warm but rigorous. Never preachy, never detached.
- Short sentences are fine. Fragments too. Match how people actually think and speak.
- If a 10th grader couldn't understand the sentence, rewrite it.
- Start with why something matters before explaining what it is.
- Ground the abstract in the specific - every big idea needs a small, human moment.
- Name what's hard. Don't shy away from uncertainty, failure, or uncomfortable truths.
- Vary sentence rhythm - mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones.

### Personality Traits

- Purpose-driven - starts with why before what
- Evidence-first - brings the data and the real example before offering an opinion
- Productively contrarian - challenges conventional wisdom to reveal something useful, not for shock value
- Human and relatable - makes complex topics feel personal without dumbing them down
- Storyteller from experience - the best points are grounded in real moments, real stakes
- Courageous in vulnerability - willing to name what's hard and what's uncertain
- Design-minded - how something is experienced matters as much as what it does
- Behavioral thinker - products succeed or fail based on human habits, not feature lists

## 3. What to Avoid

### Jargon and Buzzwords
No "leverage," "synergy," "paradigm," "ecosystem," "holistic," "scalable solution," "streamline," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "empower." If it sounds like a LinkedIn post from 2019, cut it.

### AI-Sounding Language
No "delve," "landscape," "multifaceted," "it's important to note," "in today's world," "at the end of the day," "game-changer," "let's dive in," "here's the thing," "it's worth noting," "compelling," "robust." If it sounds like ChatGPT wrote it, rewrite it.

### Lofty Abstractions
Don't say "revolutionize the customer experience." Say what actually changes for the person using the product. Don't say "exploring identity through letterforms." Say what it actually looks like.

### Technical Posturing
Don't describe systems architecture as if Kiran designed it from scratch in a backend engineering role. Frame technical choices as product decisions - why this approach serves the user, not how the code works internally.

### Passive Voice
Prefer active: "The system processes claims" → "It processes claims." "A decision was made to use LangGraph" → "I chose LangGraph because..."

### Filler Qualifiers
"Very," "really," "extremely," "incredibly," "highly." Cut them.

## 4. Accuracy and Credibility

Kiran's credibility is on the line with everything published on this site. Every claim must be defensible.

- Every number must be verifiable or clearly labelled as an estimate/range.
- "Roughly 50%" or "estimated at $6 per call" is fine. "$6.00 per call" stated as fact requires a source.
- Ranges are preferred over false precision. "20-30%" is more honest than "27.3%."
- If a claim can't be verified, either source it, hedge it, or remove it.
- Never fabricate metrics, quotes, or data points. If the data doesn't exist, say so.
- Metric formatting: use 27.5M, 15K, 40%, 3.2B - no spaces between number and unit.
- Credibility bar: if the product leader of the company being discussed read this page, they should respect it. If they wouldn't, rewrite it.
- Reputation risk: if a company employee read this, would they find it fair? If not, soften or cut.

### Respectful Language Toward Subject Companies

This site critiques products, not people. The companies Kiran writes about employ real humans who care about their work. Every word must reflect that.

- **The empathy test**: before publishing any tagline, heading, or description about a company or product, ask: "How would someone who works on this product every day feel reading this?" If the answer is hurt, insulted, or misrepresented - rewrite it.
- **Critique the outcome, not the intent.** Say "the checkout flow creates friction around pricing transparency" - not "they buried trust signals behind dark patterns." The first is an observation about the product. The second is an accusation about the people.
- **No accusatory or inflammatory framing.** Words like "buried," "broken," "failed," "deceived," "manipulated," "lazy," or "incompetent" are off-limits when describing a company's decisions. These assume malice or negligence. Products are complex, teams face trade-offs, and we rarely have full context.
- **Frame gaps as opportunities, not failures.** "There's room to improve pricing transparency" is better than "pricing is deliberately opaque." "The filter experience could be more intuitive" is better than "filters are a mess."
- **Acknowledge complexity.** Companies operate under constraints we can't see - regulatory, technical, organizational, commercial. A brief nod to this ("this is likely a trade-off driven by...") shows respect and credibility.
- **Taglines and one-liners get extra scrutiny.** These are the most visible, most shareable, and most easily taken out of context. They must be clever without being cruel. "A marketplace where trust could travel further" works. "A marketplace that buried trust signals" does not.
- **The colleague test**: imagine you're presenting this teardown to a mixed audience that includes people from the subject company. Would you say it exactly this way to their faces? If not, soften it.

## 7. Anti-AI Authenticity

Every page on this site must read like a human wrote it. Not just "not obviously AI" - genuinely human. These rules apply to all content: teardowns, blog posts, prototypes, studio descriptions, everything.

### Structure
- No two pages should have the same shape. Vary section counts, lengths, and ordering across similar pages.
- If a pattern is forming across pages (e.g., every teardown has exactly 5 stages, every blog post has exactly 4 headings), break it.

### Voice
- First-person asides: at least 2-3 per long-form page (e.g., "I spent two hours testing this..." or "Honestly, I'm not sure this is the right call").
- Dead ends: show at least one angle you explored and discarded. This is how real thinking works.
- Vary sentence length. Mix short punchy with longer reasoning. If every sentence is the same length, it reads like AI.
- Honest unknowns: name what data is missing and where you looked. "I couldn't find public data on this" is more credible than pretending you know.

### Content
- Selective citations: source important claims, don't over-cite. Real people don't footnote every sentence.
- Specificity over generality. Named examples, real numbers, actual products. Not "many companies" or "various industries."
- Humor is welcome but never forced - a wry observation, not a punchline.

### Annotations and Help Content
- Help text, tooltips, and annotations should define terms - not explain methodology or narrate the reader's experience.
- If the work needs a paragraph explaining why it exists, the work itself needs rewriting.
- Quick and scrappy beats polished and structured. A portfolio with highly curated meta-commentary looks AI-generated. A portfolio with quick margin notes looks human.
- Never add structured templates (same headings, same block count, same word count) across multiple annotations. Uniformity is the fastest tell that a machine wrote it.
- The credibility test: would a real person with a job and a life actually write this annotation by hand? If not, cut it or simplify it.

## 8. Accessibility (WCAG 2.2 AA)

This site should be usable by everyone - including people using screen readers, keyboard-only navigation, or who have visual, motor, or cognitive disabilities. Accessibility isn't an afterthought. It's a baseline requirement, same as spelling or voice.

Target standard: WCAG 2.2 Level AA.

### Every Page Must Have
- `lang="en"` on the `<html>` tag.
- A skip link as the first focusable element: `<a href="#main-content" class="skip-link">Skip to main content</a>`.
- Semantic HTML structure: `<nav>`, `<main>`, `<section>`, `<article>`, `<header>`, `<footer>`. Not just `<div>` soup.
- Proper heading hierarchy: h1 → h2 → h3 in order. Never skip levels (e.g., h1 straight to h3).
- Visible focus outlines on all interactive elements: `a:focus, button:focus { outline: 2px solid var(--accent); outline-offset: 2px; }`.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` media query to disable animations for people who need it.

### Images
- Every `<img>` must have an `alt` attribute. No exceptions.
- Descriptive alt text, not just a filename. "Alex T., a 34-year-old insurance customer" not "persona.jpg" or "Alex T."
- Decorative images (purely visual, no information) use `alt=""` (empty alt, not missing alt).

### SVGs
- Interactive or informational SVGs need either `<title>` and `<desc>` elements inside the SVG, or `aria-label` on the SVG element.
- Decorative SVGs (icons next to visible text) use `aria-hidden="true"` and `focusable="false"`.
- Wireframe and diagram SVGs should have a descriptive `<title>` summarising what the diagram shows.

### Forms
- Every input must have an associated `<label>` element with a matching `for` attribute. Placeholder text is not a label.
- Error messages must be programmatically associated with the input (use `aria-describedby`).
- Required fields should use `aria-required="true"` or the HTML `required` attribute.

### Color and Contrast
- Text must meet minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 against its background (3:1 for large text).
- Never use color alone to convey meaning. Always pair with text, icons, or patterns.
- Muted/secondary text must still be readable. If it looks faint, bump it up.

### Keyboard Navigation
- Every interactive element must be reachable and operable by keyboard alone (Tab, Enter, Space, Escape).
- Focus order must follow visual reading order.
- No keyboard traps - the user must always be able to Tab away from any element.
- Dynamic content (like the chatbot state panel) should use `aria-live="polite"` so screen readers announce updates.

### Interactive Components
- Click targets must be at least 44x44px or have sufficient spacing around them (WCAG 2.5.8).
- Custom interactive elements need appropriate ARIA roles (`role="button"`, `role="dialog"`, etc.).
- Modals and dialogs must trap focus while open and return focus to the trigger when closed.

### Links
- Link text must make sense out of context. No "click here" or "read more" without identifying what's being linked.
- Links that open in a new tab should indicate this (visually and with `aria-label` or screen reader text).

### Open Graph Cards (OG Images)

Every shareable page must have its own OG image. No page should fall back to the generic site card. This is how the site looks when someone shares a link in Slack, Teams, iMessage, or LinkedIn - it's the first impression for visitors who didn't choose to come to the site.

**Requirements for every OG image:**

- Must include the page title in large, readable text (legible at 400px wide rendering).

**Per content type:**

### SVG Rules (applies to all SVGs site-wide)
- NO `:root` overrides in any SVG `<style>` block. This breaks the theme toggle site-wide.
- All SVG class names MUST be prefixed with `wf-` (e.g., `.wf-phone-frame`, `.wf-nav-bar`). Unprefixed class names conflict with page-level CSS.
- No text overlap anywhere in any SVG. Minimum 12-15px vertical spacing between text lines. Every container rect must be tall enough for its text content plus padding.

### SVG Text Containment Rules (MANDATORY - prevents text spill)
These rules apply to EVERY `<text>` element inside EVERY SVG on the site. Violations cause visible text overflow.

1. **Width check before placing text**: Calculate text pixel width = (character count) × (font-size × 0.6). If the result exceeds the container rect width minus 20px padding, EITHER reduce font-size, split into multiple `<text>` lines, shorten the string, or widen the container.
2. **Right-aligned text near edges**: Any text positioned in the right third of a container MUST use `text-anchor="end"` and x must be at least 10px inside the container's right edge.
3. **Centered text**: For `text-anchor="middle"`, verify that (x - half_text_width) >= container_left + 10 AND (x + half_text_width) <= container_right - 10. If not, reduce font-size or split the text.
4. **Button text**: Button text width must be ≤ button rect width minus 20px. If "Update my discovery" (19 chars × 7.2px = 137px at font-size 12) doesn't fit in a 100px button, widen the button to at least 160px.
5. **Header text in wireframes**: Max font-size for single-line headers inside a 300px-wide screen is 13px for text longer than 25 characters. For longer headers, split across two `<text>` elements with 15px vertical spacing.
6. **No label collision with axis labels**: In sentiment charts, sentiment dot labels (e.g., "Hopeful") must not share the same y-coordinate as Y-axis tier labels ("Optimistic", "Neutral", "Frustrated"). Minimum 5px vertical clearance between any sentiment label and any axis label.

### Wireframe Rules
- SVGs constrained: `.wireframe-box { max-width: 340px; }` and `.wireframe-box svg { max-width: 300px; display: block; margin: 0 auto; }`
- Annotation callout boxes must be properly sized around text. Calculate text width (chars x ~6px at 10px font), add 20px horizontal padding (10px each side), 16px vertical padding (8px top/bottom). No text should touch or overflow box edges.
- Highlight borders (dashed/solid) must fully enclose UI elements with at least 5px clearance on all sides. Don't clip letters.
- No overlapping elements. Every element needs clear spacing from its neighbors. If two boxes are close, add margin or reposition.

### Customer Journey Maps
- Sentiment curve MUST thread through all data points. Use cubic bezier paths (`C` commands) that pass through each circle's exact coordinates. Do NOT use quadratic beziers (`Q`) or smooth shortcuts (`T`) that only approximate the path.
- Sentiment labels must not overlap dots. Position text at least 6px above the top of each circle (y = circle_cy - circle_r - 6 - font_size).
- Color system for sentiment: Green (#6b9e6b) for positive/keep, Red (#c47474) for negative/kill/problems, Blue (#7a9ec4) for build proposals, Amber (#d4a74a) for neutral.
- Reference lines at three tiers: optimistic, neutral, frustrated - with Y-axis labels on the left.
- Stage labels at the bottom of the map, aligned with their corresponding data points.
- Sentiment SVG viewBox width MUST accommodate right-side text labels. If labels ("Optimistic", "Neutral", "Frustrated") are placed at x=760, viewBox width must be at least 860 to prevent clipping. General rule: viewBox width >= rightmost text x + (label char count × 7).
- Journey stage grid columns MUST match the actual number of stages. Use `repeat(N, 1fr)` where N = number of `.journey-stage-card` elements. The teardown builder template uses `--journey-cols` CSS variable for this. Never hardcode 5 if the teardown has a different number of stages.
- Journey stage cards MUST have `overflow: hidden; word-wrap: break-word; overflow-wrap: break-word;` to prevent text spill on narrow screens.

### Architecture & Flow Diagrams (Inline SVG)

These rules govern all system architecture diagrams embedded as inline SVGs in blog posts, teardowns, and prototype pages. They were distilled from hands-on iteration and apply to any future diagram work.

#### General Structure
- All architecture diagrams are inline SVGs embedded directly in HTML. No external `.svg` files, no `<img>` tags pointing to SVGs.
- Class prefix: `dg-` for all diagram-specific classes (e.g., `.dg-lp`, `.dg-al`, `.dg-fc`). This distinguishes them from wireframe classes (`wf-`).
- Use CSS custom properties (`var(--text-primary)`, `var(--bg-primary)`, `var(--text-muted)`) with hardcoded fallback values matching the site's dark mode defaults.
- Light mode support: include `[data-theme="light"]` overrides inside the SVG `<style>` block for every class that uses color. Never hardcode a single theme.
- ViewBox should be sized to the actual content with breathing room. Don't cram elements to fit a small viewBox.

#### Layout and Spacing
- Organise content into clearly labelled horizontal layers (e.g., DEVICE LAYER, MERCHANT LAYER, NETWORK LAYER).
- Each layer gets a label (`font-size: 10px`, `font-weight: 600`, `letter-spacing: 0.08em`) positioned above its bounding rect.
- Minimum 74px vertical gap between layer bounding rects. This gap is where numbered step badges live.
- No element should touch or overlap another. Every box, label, badge, and arrow needs clear spacing from its neighbors.
- Layer bounding rects use subtle fills (`rgba` at 0.04 opacity) and strokes (`rgba` at 0.12 opacity). They frame content without competing with it.

#### Arrows and Connectors
- Use a single `<marker>` definition with `orient="auto"` for all arrowheads. Never create separate markers for each direction.
- Every arrow must have a visible stem (line segment), not just a floating arrowhead. Minimum stem length: ~8px.
- Inter-layer arrows: use a single continuous `<line>` from source to destination with ONE `marker-end` arrowhead at the endpoint. Do not split into two segments with two arrowheads.
- Right-angle connectors between non-aligned elements: use `<polyline>` with explicit corner coordinates instead of diagonal lines.
- Return/feedback paths: route along the right edge of the diagram (outside layer boxes, ~20px clearance from box edges). Use three line segments: horizontal out, vertical up/down, horizontal back in.
- Horizontal return lines must not cross through any callout boxes or text. Route above or below obstructions.

#### Numbered Step Badges
- Step badges are solid, opaque circles drawn ON TOP of arrow lines (SVG z-order = source order, so draw the badge after the line).
- Badge background: `fill: var(--bg-primary)` so the circle matches the page background and visually covers any line passing through it.
- Badge border: `stroke: var(--text-muted)`, `stroke-width: 1.2`.
- Badge number: `font-weight: 700`, `font-size: 12px`, centered in the circle.
- Badges sit in the gap between layers, centered on the arrow line. They should never overlap with layer labels, box edges, or other badges.
- Each badge gets a short italic annotation beside it (e.g., "NFC tap to terminal", "Auth request") at `font-size: 9px`.
- Step label format: just the number in the circle + italic description. No "Step N:" prefix unless all steps use it consistently.
- If a badge sits on a container border (e.g., Step 1 on an iPhone outline), place the circle's center exactly on the border line (`cy` = rect's `y` value).

#### Text and Labels
- Primary text (box titles): `font-size: 11-12px`, `font-weight: 600`, using `--text-primary`.
- Secondary text (descriptions): `font-size: 9px`, using `--text-muted`.
- All text within a box must fit with at least 10px horizontal padding and 8px vertical padding on each side.
- If a label is too long for its container, shorten the label. Never let text overflow or clip against box edges.
- Text centering: use `text-anchor="middle"` with x set to the box's horizontal center.

#### Callout Boxes
- Dashed border callouts (informational, e.g., "What stays on-device"): `stroke-dasharray="4 3"`, subtle fill.
- Solid border callouts (contextual, e.g., "Merchant sees only"): same subtle fill as layers but slightly different opacity.
- Callout boxes must not overlap with arrow paths or step badges.

#### Theme-Adaptive Preview
- When iterating on a diagram, generate a side-by-side dark/light preview HTML file.
- The preview uses namespaced classes per panel (e.g., `dk-` for dark, `lt-` for light) with hardcoded color values to avoid CSS variable conflicts between the two panels.
- Each panel needs its own `<marker>` definition with a unique ID (e.g., `#adk`, `#alt`) since markers inherit styles from their context.
- Save the preview to the workspace root as `diagram-preview.html` for the author to review. Clean up when done.

#### Accessibility
- Every architecture SVG must have `role="img"` and a descriptive `aria-label` summarising the diagram's content and flow.
- The `aria-label` should describe the number of steps and the high-level flow in plain language.
- US English in all labels and descriptions (authorization, tokenized, etc.).

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Never use `rgba` semi-transparent fills for step badges. They must be solid (opaque background color) so lines don't show through.
- Never split an arrow into two segments with two arrowheads just because a badge sits on it. Use one continuous line + solid badge on top.
- Never remove existing text labels when adding badges to their containers. The badge supplements the label, it doesn't replace it.
- Never route arrows or return paths through text boxes or callout areas.
- Never hardcode dark-mode-only colors without light mode overrides.
- Never use `cairosvg` or similar server-side renderers for preview - they don't support CSS classes/variables. Use browser-based HTML previews instead.

## 10. Pre-Publish Checklist

Nothing goes live on any page of this site without passing these checks. This is mandatory, not optional.

### Content Checks (run Content Audit)
1. Voice: does it sound like Kiran wrote it? Read it out loud. If it sounds like a robot, a consultant, or a sales deck - rewrite it.
2. Jargon: any buzzwords, AI-speak, or lofty abstractions? Cut them.
3. Accuracy: every number verifiable or hedged? Every claim defensible?
4. US English: spelling, punctuation, and phrasing consistent throughout?
5. Credibility: if the subject of this page read it, would they respect it?
6. Respectful language: run the empathy test and colleague test on every tagline, heading, and description that references a company or product. No accusatory framing, no inflammatory words, no assumptions of malice.
7. Originality: does this add something new, or is it just restating what already exists?
8. Anti-AI: structure varied? First-person asides present? Dead ends shown? Sentence length varies?
9. Position: does the content take a clear stance, or does it hedge with weasel phrases?
10. Read time: recalculate word count (body text only, excluding HTML/SVG/scripts/nav/footer) and verify the displayed "X min read" tag matches. Use ~238 wpm as the baseline. If the content has been added to or cut since the tag was set, update it before publishing.
11. OG card: does this page have its own per-page OG image (not the generic site card)? Does the image include the page title, and is the title legible at small rendering sizes? Does the `og:image` URL use kirangorapalli.com?
12. Domain: all canonical URLs, og:url, og:image, twitter:image, and JSON-LD URLs use kirangorapalli.com (never the Netlify URL).

### Visual Checks (run Visual Audit)
1. No em dashes in any visible text or HTML source.
2. No SVG `:root` overrides.
3. All SVG classes prefixed with `wf-`.
4. No text overlap in any SVG.
5. Wireframe boxes properly sized and contained.
6. Journey map curves thread through every dot.
7. Sentiment labels don't overlap circles.
8. No elements overlapping anywhere on the page.

### Accessibility Checks
1. Skip link present and points to main content.
2. All images have descriptive alt text (not filenames or single words).
3. All form inputs have associated `<label>` elements.
4. SVGs have `<title>`/`aria-label` (informational) or `aria-hidden="true"` (decorative).
5. Heading hierarchy correct - h1 → h2 → h3, no skipped levels.
6. Color contrast meets 4.5:1 minimum for text.
7. All interactive elements reachable and operable by keyboard.
8. No "click here" or "read more" link text without context.
9. Dynamic content areas have `aria-live` attributes.
10. Click targets are at least 44x44px or have sufficient spacing.

### Final Gut Check
- Every paragraph earns its place. If it could be removed without loss, remove it.
- Every section transition feels inevitable. The reader should never wonder "why am I reading this now?"
- Close with resonance - leave the reader thinking, not just informed.

## 11. Teardowns (How I'd've Built It)

These are product analyses, not engineering blueprints.

- Lead with the user problem. Always.
- Business case matters - connect every recommendation to revenue, cost, or growth.
- Hard numbers must cite a source (even if it's "per QuestionPro Q1 2025" or "industry reports").
- KPIs should be ranges, not false-precision targets.
- Acknowledge what you don't know. "I can't see their internal systems" is a strength, not a weakness.
- Asymmetric structure: journey stage counts, KKB splits, KPI counts must differ between teardowns. No two teardowns should have the same shape.
- Specific persona: named, aged, with a story. Not a demographic profile.
- Respectful framing: every teardown critiques the product experience, never the people or their intentions. Run the empathy test (Section 4) on all taglines, headings, and hero copy before publishing. This is especially important for taglines that appear on interstitial pages and company cards - these are the most visible, most shareable lines on the site.

### Pain Point Research (Step 1)
- Pain point clusters must be numbered (1, 2, 3, etc.) so Kiran can easily reference them when selecting which to focus on.
- All research must be limited to the last two years. Older reviews, complaints, and data points may no longer reflect the current product experience. If citing a source, include the date or year.
- Disregard known, obvious pain points that exist because of strategic or revenue reasons. These are deliberate business decisions, not product gaps the company failed to notice (e.g., Intuit gating live support behind paid tiers, airlines charging for seat selection). Note them briefly and flag them as "not recommended for focus," but steer the teardown toward genuine product gaps the company would likely want to fix.

## 12. Blog & Podcast (WordWeaver)

### Writing Quality
1. Lead with insight, not information.
2. Data is seasoning, not the meal.
3. Write for the smart, busy reader.
4. Every section transition should feel inevitable.
5. The Sinek-Grant-Noah blend: start with why, back with evidence, make it human.

### Stylistic Principles
- Start with why - the reader should know why this matters within the first 100 words.
- Frame choices as meaningful contrasts - not just "here's what happened" but "here's the choice, and here's what it reveals."
- Challenge one assumption per post.
- Write from the practitioner's perspective - "here's what I've seen work" beats "here's what the theory says."

### Formatting
- Target: ~1,750 words / 7 min read.
- Headings: compelling and specific, never generic.
- Paragraphs: 3-5 sentences typical. Occasional one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.
- Quotes: sparingly. Only when the original phrasing is genuinely better than paraphrasing.
- Lists: rarely in the body. Prose flows better.

## 13. MadLab Prototypes

- These demonstrate product thinking through working software.
- Overview pages should answer: What problem does this solve? How does it work? Why this approach?
- Don't oversell a prototype as a production system. It's a prototype - that's the point.
- The prototype should speak for itself. The overview page gets someone excited to try it, not exhaustively document it.
- Focus on what it does for the user first, then briefly explain how. Never lead with the stack.
- Frame technical choices as product decisions. "I chose LangGraph because conversations get messy and multi-step" - not "LangGraph provides stateful multi-turn routing via conditional edges."

## 15. Per-Page Glossary (Replacing Per-Section Explainer Icons)

### Why This Changed

The original approach placed [?] help icons next to section titles, opening slide panels with structured context. This was deprecated because:

1. **Repetition.** "My Take" content repeated what the page prose already said. That's what AI does — it repackages the same information into a different container.
2. **Template uniformity.** The same [?] icon on every section title with the same structure looked machine-generated.
3. **Volume.** 10+ scattered icons is a lot of UI chrome for what amounts to a glossary.

### The Rule: Per-Page Glossary at the Top

Each teardown and prototype page gets its own short glossary section placed **at the top of the page** — after the hero/breadcrumb, before the first content section. The glossary contains only the terms relevant to that specific page. No site-wide mega-glossary. No per-section [?] icons. No inline help panels.

The rationale: if a visitor doesn't know what JTBD or KKB means, they need that context before they start reading, not after they've already been lost for five sections. Put the help where it's useful — at the entrance.

### Glossary Section Rules
- Only include terms a smart reader in a different field wouldn't know. Product people know what a KPI is — don't define it. But they might not know what a KKB framework is.
- 15-30 words per definition. Plain English. Frame technical choices as product decisions, not engineering specs.
- Each page's glossary should only contain terms that actually appear on that page. The GEICO teardown doesn't need LangGraph defined; the chatbot prototype doesn't need DAU/MAU.
- No fixed number of terms. Some pages might need 3, others might need 7. Don't pad or cut to hit a target.
- No "My Take" commentary in the glossary — that belongs in the page prose.
- No methodology explanations ("Why It's Here", "What to Look For") — the work justifies itself.
- The glossary must not repeat what the page prose already says. If a term is already explained inline in the content, don't also define it in the glossary.
- Follows all universal content rules (voice, spelling, anti-AI, accessibility).

### What Does NOT Belong in a Per-Page Glossary
- Terms the audience already knows (KPI, ROI, NPS for product people).
- Methodology narration or reading instructions.
- Anything that duplicates the page prose.
- "My Take" or personal commentary — that's what the teardown itself is for.

### Command Center (Teardown Builder) Rules
When the Teardown Builder generates a new teardown:
- Do NOT generate any explainer icon markup (`explainer-icon` classes, `data-explainer-*` attributes).
- Do NOT generate slide panel content or structured help blocks.
- DO generate a glossary section at the top of the page with only the terms relevant to that teardown.
- Inline definitions in the prose are also fine when a term first appears (e.g., "I used a Jobs-to-Be-Done framework — basically mapping what the user is actually trying to accomplish, not what the app offers"). But if a term is defined inline, don't also put it in the glossary.

### Legacy Cleanup
- All existing `explainer-icon` elements and their `data-explainer-*` attributes should be removed from HTML files.
- `explainer.js` and associated CSS can be deprecated once all icons are removed.
- `scripts/audit-explainers.py` should be updated to verify that no explainer icons remain (inversion: the audit now checks for absence, not correctness).
- The site-wide `glossary.html` has been removed in favor of per-page glossary sections.
