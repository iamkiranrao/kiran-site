GEICO Mobile App

A lookup tool in a resolution world - and the 9-year gap that needs closing

Product at a Glance
Mobile self-service app for GEICO policyholders. The app is where users interact with insurance day-to-day: filing claims, downloading ID cards, managing payments, getting roadside assistance, and accessing their policy. GEICO is a Berkshire Hathaway subsidiary and the second-largest auto insurer in the US, built on a direct-to-consumer, low-cost-provider model. The app does two jobs: keep people from leaving, and keep them off the phone to save money.

Section 01
Discovery
Persona
Alex T.
34, switched from State Farm 3 years ago, commutes daily
Switched to GEICO because the quote came in $40 per month cheaper. Doesn't think about insurance unless something goes wrong. Last time he opened the app was to download his ID card for a rental car at the airport. This time, he notices a charge on his bank statement he doesn't recognize.

The Trustpilot and App Store reviews split into two groups. The first is angry about claims handling. The second is frustrated that simple questions turn into 45-minute ordeals. The second group was louder and more specific to the AI assistant gap. Alex is that person.

I initially considered DriveEasy tracking accuracy as the teardown angle, but the AI assistant problem is more universal and a bigger deal, and a more interesting product problem. Every GEICO customer interacts with the assistant. Not every customer uses DriveEasy.

Jobs to Be Done
When something's off with my policy, I want to get it sorted without it becoming a project. GEICO built an assistant that can look things up, but users don't come to support to look things up. They come because something is wrong and they need it fixed. The assistant solves the easy jobs nobody needed help with and fails at the hard jobs everyone does.
Section 02
Keep / Kill / Build

GEICO's virtual assistant - originally launched as Kate in 2017 and now branded as the GEICO AI Virtual Assistant - has improved since launch. It's available 24/7 on both app and web, and it now tells you when you're talking to AI. That matters. But the core problem hasn't changed in nine years.

Keep
Basic lookups work
Balance checks, payment dates, policy details - the assistant handles these. The problem isn't that these features exist. It's that they're all it can do.
AI transparency labeling
GEICO tells users when they're talking to AI vs. a live agent. Most competitors don't do this. That's good - people should know what they're dealing with.
Kill
Keyword-dependent comprehension
GEICO's own website tells users to keep questions "short and direct" because complex queries break the assistant. Think about that. They published 'don't ask hard questions' in 2026. When your usage guidance is essentially "don't ask it anything hard," the tool has a core problem. Lemonade's AI resolves 27% of claims autonomously. GEICO's can't understand a multi-clause sentence.
No context handoff
This is the killer. You spend 10 minutes with the assistant, give up, call the phone number, and start from zero. The assistant and the phone system don't talk to each other. All that time in the chat? Wasted. Zero progress.
Build
Conversational AI
Users should be able to say "I got charged twice and I don't know why" and get a real answer that references their specific account data. This isn't speculative. Lemonade does this for claims, which are far more complex than billing questions.
Smart escalation with context
When the AI can't resolve, it hands off to a human with the full transcript, policy details, and issue summary. The agent sees everything. The user repeats nothing. That alone changes the whole experience.
Phased AI resolution
I'd start with billing resolution - highest volume, lowest complexity. Then coverage questions. Then simple claims. A PM at GEICO would push back on "rebuild everything at once." Phases make this actually doable.

A PM at GEICO would push back here: "Lemonade is digital-native. They built their stack around AI from day one. We're running on legacy systems across 50 states with decades of regulatory complexity." That's exactly the point. The question is whether GEICO uses that as an excuse or actually treats it as a problem to solve.

Section 03
The Redesign

Two wireframes. The first shows what happens when the assistant can actually resolve things. The second shows what happens when it can't - but hands off intelligently instead of dead-ending.

Wireframe 1 - Conversational AI Resolution
CONVERSATIONAL AI
This is the real change - the assistant knows your account and can do something about it. Instead of keyword matching, it understands that "I got charged twice" means "show me both charges, explain the difference, and let me dispute one." The two buttons at the bottom are the key detail: the user can resolve in-chat or escalate. Either way, they're making progress.
Wireframe 2 - Smart Escalation Handoff
SMART ESCALATION
When the AI can't resolve, it doesn't say "please call us." It says "I'm connecting you - here's what I've already told them." The summary card is the entire point: policy number, issue description, what was already tried, and a recommended next step. The agent picks up where the bot left off. Estimated wait drops because the agent already knows the issue before answering.
Section 04
The Business Case
Executive Summary

This is a cost and satisfaction play. Chatbot interactions cost roughly $0.50 each. Phone calls cost about $6.00. As of Q1 2025, GEICO's NPS sat at 20 - below the insurance industry average of 23 and well behind USAA at 54. The ROI math isn't complicated.

AI Resolution Rate
50-60%
Issues resolved without human agent (18-month target)
GEICO's current rate is estimated under 20% (lookup only). Lemonade handles 27% of claims autonomously, 30%+ of all interactions. Billing and coverage questions are simpler than claims.
Handle Time
Under 10 min
Average time from issue to resolution (AI-resolved)
Current path: 10 min assistant fail + 30 min hold + resolution = 45+ min. Smart handoff alone cuts the repeat-everything step.
NPS Score
30+
Net Promoter Score (12-month target)
From 20 to 30+ closes the gap to industry average. Lemonade's NPS is 75+ for pet insurance customers with AI resolution.
Cost Per Interaction
$2-3 blended
Weighted average across AI and phone
Shifting volume from $6 phone calls to $0.50 AI interactions. Even at 50% AI resolution, the blended cost drops significantly.
Escalation Context
90%+
Escalations that include full AI summary
Direct measurement of the handoff pipeline. Today this is 0%. The target is that every escalation carries the full conversation history.

The honest question is whether GEICO's infrastructure can support this without a ground-up rebuild. Kate launched in 2017. Upgrading from keyword matching to conversational AI might mean replacing the entire stack - not a 6-month project but a 2-year platform migration. Industry benchmarks suggest insurance chatbot implementations see positive ROI in 8-14 months, with initial benefits within 60-90 days. But those benchmarks come from companies building new systems, not retrofitting legacy ones.

I can't see GEICO's internals. A PM there would know whether this is feasible in phases or requires a platform bet. What I can say is: the cost of not doing it is measurable. Every month the assistant can't resolve a billing question, GEICO pays $6 per call instead of $0.50 per chat. Multiply that by their call volume and you've got your answer.

Assumptions & Limitations

I don't work at GEICO and I've never seen their internal systems, call volume data, or technology roadmap. This analysis is built from their public-facing AI assistant (tested directly), their published usage guidance ("keep questions short and direct"), their NPS score (20, per QuestionPro Q1 2025), competitor benchmarks (Lemonade Q2 2025 earnings, Progressive's Azure AI partnership), and industry cost data ($0.50 vs $6.00, from multiple 2025 industry reports). The persona is a composite based on Trustpilot and App Store review patterns. The KPI numbers are educated ranges, not magic numbers.
