Dialogflow ES Google's intent-based chatbot platform (the "Essentials" edition). You define what users might say, map it to responses, and Dialogflow handles the fuzzy matching in between.
Intent A mapping between what someone says and what the bot should do. "Book a table" and "I'd like a reservation" trigger the same intent because they mean the same thing.
Entity A specific piece of data the bot extracts from what you say. In "table for 4 on Friday at 7pm", the entities are: party size (4), date (Friday), and time (7pm).
Context Dialogflow's version of short-term memory. It lets the bot remember what you said in the previous turn so follow-up questions make sense. Expires after a set number of turns.
Training Phrase Example sentences you give Dialogflow so it can learn to recognize variations. The more you provide, the better it gets at matching.

Jurassic Bites

A restaurant reservation chatbot built on Google Dialogflow designed to show what intent-based NLP does well (entity extraction, structured Q&A) and where it hits a wall (multi-turn memory, dynamic responses).

Details

Jurassic Bites is a restaurant reservation chatbot built on Google Dialogflow ES. The goal wasn't just to build a working bot it was to pick a use case that would stress-test the platform and expose both its strengths and its limitations in a single prototype.

Restaurant reservations are a great fit for this because they play directly to what Dialogflow does best: extracting structured data from natural language. When someone says "table for 4 on Friday at 7pm", Dialogflow pulls out the party size, date, and time automatically. That's entity extraction, and it works remarkably well out of the box.

But reservations also expose what Dialogflow can't do. If a user changes their mind mid-flow "actually make it Saturday instead" you need context management to carry state between turns. If they ask something unexpected, you need a separate intent for every possible question. And there's no way to generate a novel response everything the bot says has to be pre-written. It's powerful for structured interactions, but rigid when things go off-script.

System Architecture

The bot runs entirely within Dialogflow ES no backend server, no database, no webhook fulfillment. Everything lives in Google's infrastructure: intent matching, entity extraction, context tracking, and response selection. The web demo is embedded via Dialogflow's built-in iframe integration.

Why no backend? This prototype is deliberately self-contained to show what Dialogflow can do on its own. In production, you'd add webhook fulfillment to check real availability, store bookings in a database, and send confirmation emails. But for demonstrating the conversational AI patterns intent matching, entity extraction, context chaining the platform handles it all.

Try It Yourself

The bot runs through Dialogflow's embedded web demo no login required. Here are some things to try:

The happy path: "I'd like to book a table for 4 on Friday at 7pm" watch it extract the date, time, and party size in one shot, then ask you to confirm.

Piece by piece: Start with "I want to make a reservation" and let the bot ask you for details one at a time. This shows the context management in action.

Info questions: Try "what's on the menu", "do you have parking", "are you open on Sunday", or "do you have vegan options" each hits a different intent with detailed responses.

Break it: Ask something outside the defined intents "what's the Wi-Fi password?" or "can I bring my dog?" and watch the fallback kick in. This is the limitation: if there's no intent for it, the bot can't help. There's no reasoning, no improvisation.
