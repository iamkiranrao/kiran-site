01 · Discovery

Customer insight before opinion.

The strategic chain starts here. Six personas — not invented, but distilled from years of hiring conversations, peer interviews, and uncomfortable feedback. Each is a real archetype I've encountered repeatedly, mapped to what they're actually trying to accomplish when they land on a portfolio site.

The six personas (each defined by mindset, not title)
Persona 01 · Evaluator

Merritt Hunter

Recruiter or hiring manager actively scanning for a role they need to fill, on a clock.

  • When I land on a candidate's portfolio with a JD in another tab
  • I want to rapidly identify whether this person can do the specific job I'm hiring for
  • So I can decide in under 3 minutes whether to bring them into a real conversation or pass
Persona 02 · Seeker

Phil Rivera

Mid-career operator considering their next move, browsing without urgency.

  • When I'm exploring what good product work actually looks like outside my current bubble
  • I want to see how someone who's been around thinks through real product problems
  • So I can calibrate my own thinking and decide if this person's worth following / reaching out to
Persona 03 · Practitioner

Drew Park

Senior PM or design lead who cares about craft and thinks deeply about product.

  • When I encounter a portfolio that signals someone who actually thinks about product
  • I want to dig into how they think — read their teardowns, see how they approached real problems
  • So I can learn something, find a peer worth knowing, or both
Persona 04 · Learner

Paige Martin

Earlier-career PM trying to build their own craft, hungry for examples.

  • When I'm trying to level up my product thinking and looking for real examples
  • I want to see how a more experienced PM frames problems and structures their work
  • So I can borrow the patterns and apply them to my own work
Persona 05 · Technologist

Ray Chen

Engineer, founder, or AI-curious operator evaluating whether someone "gets" modern building.

  • When I encounter a portfolio that's clearly built differently from a templated one
  • I want to understand the technical decisions and whether this person can build with AI fluently
  • So I can assess if they'd thrive in an AI-native team or org
Persona 06 · Inner Circle

Keshav Shah

Friend, former colleague, or trusted connection — already knows Kiran.

  • When I want to share Kiran with someone in my network
  • I want to have a single link that captures who he is in a way I'd be proud to forward
  • So I can make a high-quality introduction without writing a custom pitch

02 · Positioning

The position taken, defended, and lived.

A positioning statement is only useful if it's specific enough to make some people go away — and the right people lean in. This section captures what kiranrao.ai is, what it isn't, and how it stands against the alternatives a thoughtful visitor would compare it to.

Positioning statement
For senior product leaders, founders, and pattern-breakers who hire on judgment rather than pedigree, kiranrao.ai is a permanent home that demonstrates Kiran's product thinking, AI-native craft, and 15+ years of building — through the artifact itself rather than through credentials. Unlike templated portfolio sites or static resumes, this site reveals how its author actually thinks, by being something he could only have built if it were true.

The two axes that matter for the audience this site serves: generic ↔ personal (does this feel like a template or like a person?) and static ↔ living (is this a snapshot or something that responds to me?). Most alternatives cluster in the bottom-left quadrant. kiranrao.ai is deliberately in the top-right, which is also the hardest position to defend because it requires more work to maintain.

Mock competitor archetypes
Archetype 01

The Notion-page portfolio

One Notion doc with bullet-pointed accomplishments and a few embeds. Easy to make, easy to update, almost universally interchangeable. Says "I'm pragmatic" but also "I haven't thought about what makes me different."

vs kiranrao.ai They prove competence at writing structured documents. The site proves competence at building products.
Archetype 02

The heavily-templated designer site

Webflow or Framer template, beautifully styled, heavy emphasis on visuals. Reads as design-credible but the form/content gap is wide — it's the same template thousands of designers use, populated with project-specific media.

vs kiranrao.ai They prove visual taste. The site proves the taste went into the architecture, not just the surface.
Archetype 03

The classic résumé PDF

Two-page PDF, clean typography, bullet-pointed achievements. The format compresses a 15-year career into 600 words and asks the reader to imagine the rest. Privileges title/company over thinking/craft.

vs kiranrao.ai They give the reader 600 words to decide. The site gives them an experience to feel.

So the strategy is layered: the work creates surface area, the network amplifies, distinction earns recognition, organic word-of-mouth compounds.

GTM flywheel · the four-channel layered model

The work itself

Teardowns are the only scalable channel for a mindset-defined audience. A great teardown self-selects for the right reader, addresses the banking stigma by showing universal product judgment, creates natural reasons for outreach, and gives the network something specific to share.

The network

Kiran's career was built on reputation — colleagues pulling him into their next team. That pattern doesn't change. What changes is now there's something specific to forward. "Look at what Kiran built" is a more compelling intro than "Kiran's a great PM."

Distinction

A site at this level of craft, architecture, and personalization is objectively unusual. Awwwards, CSS Design Awards, Product Hunt, Webby Awards — these channels surface the work to people who notice craft and ask "who built this?"

Organic word-of-mouth

The small percentage of visitors who have an emotional reaction — "this is someone I want to talk to" — share unprompted. Can't manufacture this. Can only build something worth sharing.

Channel mix
Tier 1 · Always-on

Teardowns + LinkedIn distribution

One teardown every 2-3 weeks. Each gets a condensed LinkedIn post that stands on its own as product thinking, plus 1-2 warm outreaches to people connected to the product torn down.

Tactical: 2K-4K words on-site · 600-word LinkedIn version · personalized follow-ups
Tier 2 · Activation

Network reactivation

Reconnect with former colleagues, peers, and people Kiran has worked alongside. Not as a job pitch — as a "here's what I've been building, thought you'd find it interesting."

Tactical: warm reach-outs in batches of 5-10 · personal note + specific link
Tier 3 · Surface

Awards + craft community presence

Submit to Awwwards, CSS Design Awards, Webby. Get featured in design-craft newsletters (Sidebar, Designer News). These reach the people who recognize and respect craft.

Tactical: 3-4 awards submissions · 1-2 newsletter pitches per quarter
Tier 4 · Compound

Community presence

Show up in product communities (Mind the Product, Lenny's, Reforge). Comment substantively, not promotionally. Over time, become a known voice — not for self-promo, but for sharp thinking.

Tactical: weekly engagement in 2-3 communities · zero promotional posting
Channel mix pulled from GO-TO-MARKET.md's four-tier framework. Tactics on each channel are deliberately specific so this reads like a real plan, not a vague aspiration. You may want to revise the cadence numbers based on what you actually want to commit to.

06 · Measurement

What I measure, what I deliberately don't.

Most product sites measure conversion funnels — page views, time on site, click-through, contact form submission rate. This site measures none of those, and that choice is itself a strategic position. Measuring what doesn't matter would optimize for the wrong audience.

The KPI framework

What I measure

  • Did the right person reach out?
  • What was the depth of the conversation that followed?
  • Did people share the site / a teardown unprompted?
  • Did anyone in my network forward a specific link to a specific contact?
  • How did a specific teardown perform inside the network it was made for?
  • Quality of inbound from the connect form

What I deliberately don't measure

  • Page views
  • Click-through rate
  • Time on site
  • Bounce rate
  • Conversion funnel
  • SEO ranking
  • Connect form submission rate
Anti-funnel framework
Optimizing for the wrong metric is worse than not optimizing. A site optimized for CTR would surface the most-shareable content first, optimize headlines for clicks, and chase scale. A site optimized for relational connection does the opposite — it rewards depth, makes you commit to the picker before showing the work, and lets the right reader self-select.
Anti-funnel illustration · awaiting MJ asset · visual essay piece
Qualitative criteria — what "success" looks like operationally
  1. The right person reached out. Not someone fitting a template — someone whose worldview matched the Pattern-Breaker persona, who came with specific reactions to specific work.
  2. The conversation went deep fast. No "tell me about yourself" warmup loops. The site already did that work. The first 5 minutes are about substance.
  3. Someone in my network forwarded a specific link to a specific person they know. Targeted, intentional sharing — not generic broadcast.
  4. A teardown reached someone connected to the product. Either they shared it, or someone they knew shared it, or it earned a response from the team being torn down.
  5. The site outlives the job search. A year from now, it still works. Five years from now, it still works. The home compounds.
